Petrographer Privelikhina V.V. (1984) provides the following optical constants for fibrous rhodusite: *Ng* = 1.660; *Np* = 1.658, elongation positive. In thin section, large aggregates (0.5–1.2 mm) of amphibole form a fine network, the interstices of which are occupied by smaller (0.01–0.02, rarely up to 0.1 mm) aggregates. In addition to amphibole, rare grains of garnet (0.01–0.03 mm), ore minerals (0.04–0.06 mm), and leucoxenized titaniferous (0.03–0.05 mm) minerals are present. Rare grains of quartz, plagioclase, and chalcedony are noted.

Electron microscopic images (Fig. No. 81) of rhodusites were obtained at a magnification of 10,000 times. As can be seen from the figure, rhodusite particles have an elongated shape. The dimensions and morphology of the fiber-crystallites are non-uniform. Rhodusite fibers have the form of wide and thin plates. Wide-form fibers are apparently oriented "face-on" under the microscope, and thinner ones "edge-on." Sometimes thick fibers split into separate thin aggregates, forming bundles. The data presented show that the chemical composition of the rhodusites is nearly identical. Samples from boreholes 3-46 and 3-15 exhibit elevated magnesium and aluminum contents and low divalent iron content.

Chemical stability was studied for both the fibrous and lamellar varieties of rhodusite. Acid resistance (37% HCl) for fibrous rhodusite is 4.4, and for lamellar rhodusite — 5.6. Resistance to 10% KOH is 5.7 and 6.0 respectively. Density — 2.89 g/cm³.

X-ray structural analysis unambiguously demonstrated a high rhodusite content in both varieties. Diffractometric studies of samples (boreholes No. 3-46, 3-15) of rhodusites showed the following reflection values: 8.36; 4.44; 3.10; 2.70; 2.16; 1.65 (Fig. No. 83) and 8.24; 4.44; 3.08; 2.69; 2.15; 1.64 (Fig. No. 82). The diffraction effects of the samples correspond to tabulated values, including data cited by Beiseyev O.B. for rhodusites of Kumola and Umbulak (Beiseyev, 1980; table, p. 75).

Mineral fractions of rodusite, after ultrasonic cleaning (22 kHz), both in the fibrous and tabular varieties, acquire barely distinguishable forms in colloidal state with fiber thickness of 0.06–0.29 microns. The thermoeffect of rodusite is 960–1000°.

As a result of the geological prospecting work carried out, a powerful bed of rocks of the Kayraktinsky suite with rodusite mineralization was for the first time established within the area of sheet M-42-VII (boreholes No. 3-8, 3-15, 3-46). In addition to boreholes No. 3-8, 3-15, 3-46, rodusite mineralization was established in reference borehole No. 1-14-B in the interval 1077–1077.5 m. The rodusite mineralization is confined to grey calcareous siltstones with a bluish tint. Rodusite in them is observed in the form of earthy masses irregularly disseminated in the groundmass of the siltstones. The rocks hosting the rodusite mineralization are dense, with massive texture.

X-ray structural analysis of the mineralized sample showed the following reflection values (Fig. No. 84): 8.30; 4.44; 3.10; 2.69; 2.52. The quantity of rodusite, according to X-ray structural analysis data, is approximately 2–3%. The established signs of rodusite mineralization in borehole No. 1-14-B indicate that rodusite-bearing rocks are observed up to the central part of the Taldyk syncline. The rodusite content here is insignificant and disseminated. Apparently, rodusite formation here took place under calm tectonic conditions and was not affected by intensive processes of diagenesis and epigenesis. From the data presented it is evident that the rodusite occurrences of the Taldyk syncline possess certain specific characteristics unique to them. The ore-hosting sequence of the Kayraktinsky suite is composed of grey, light-grey, dark-grey marls, limestones, siltstones, and rarely sandstones. In the section of the ore-hosting sequence, marls with breccia-like texture predominate, to which the rodusite mineralization is confined. This type of sediment extends eastward to the western limb of the Shurumsay anticline.

The formation of rodusite occurrences in the rocks of the Kayraktinsky suite was favoured by the lithological composition of the rocks, formed from lacustrine-type sediments, in which all rodusite-forming components are dissolved. The latter were brought in from the area of the Ishim arc, where rocks with elevated contents are widespread
