The Krivokuduksky intrusive complex is characterized by elevated values of magnetic susceptibility (for granodiorites 1240·10⁻⁶ SI units, diorites 1840·10⁻⁶ SI units), which correlates well with positive anomalies over the contours of their distribution. Over the granitic varieties of the intrusions (whose magnetic susceptibility is 30·10⁻⁶ SI units), a decrease in magnetic field values is observed.

The density of the rocks varies from 2.58 g/cm³ for granites to 2.72 g/cm³ for diorites.

Since the principal composition of the intrusions is granites and granodiorites (σ = 2.66 g/cm³), intensive negative gravitational anomalies are observed in the physical fields over the described intrusions, coinciding in plan with positive magnetic anomalies.

The positive Δg anomaly observed in the northwest of sheet N–42–VII is conditioned by the underlying sediments of the Bratolyubovskaya Formation.

2. Zerendinsky Complex

Quartz monzodiorites and giant-porphyritic granitoids of the Zerendinsky complex are widely distributed in the northern part of the area, within the Akmola basin, where they compose the enormous Zerendinsky massif. The southern end of the massif falls within the survey territory, represented at the day surface, due to poor exposure, by a discontinuous, arc-shaped strip of isolated outcrops extending from the village of Bystrianovka to the Atymek River (N–42–XXXIII). Further to the east, after a large gap occupied by outcrops of Lower Paleozoic sedimentary sequences, granites of the [illegible] complex and covered by a veneer of Cenozoic sediments, rocks of the Zerendinsky complex are exposed on the left bank of the Kokur River north of the villages of Verolubovka and Vasilyevka (N–42–XXXIV).

The study of the Zerendinsky massif over the past 20–30 years has been undertaken by many geologists (see the chapter "Degree of Geological Study").

-287-

The result of this work was the identification, among the formations of the Zerende complex of rocks attributed to three intrusive phases, of phases of additional intrusions, vein formations of the first stage, and a complex of dike formations of the second stage (Serik, 1963). This subdivision scheme is also adopted by us; however, since the study area covers only a small portion of the massif, not all of the above-listed phases are represented within its boundaries. The following have been mapped on the studied territory:

1. Formations of the second intrusive phase, represented by quartz monzodiorites and granodiorites – δ₂O₃z

2. Third intrusive phase – granodiorites and granites – γ₃O₃z,δO₃z;

3. Veins of the first stage – fine-grained granites and aplites.

4. Dikes of the second stage – granite-porphyries, granophyres, diorite porphyrites, spessartites, granodiorite-porphyries, microdiorites, svenite-porphyries.

Outcrops of rocks of the first phase and the phase of additional intrusions within the study area are unknown.

The exposure of the massif is poor; the greater part of it is covered by Cenozoic sediments, and the area of exposed portions does not exceed 65 km². Most commonly, rocks of the Zerende complex outcrop at the surface in the form of alluvial rubble spreads of gravel, sometimes boulder, and less frequently form bedrock outcrops confined to low positive landforms (subdued ridges, grivas). Dike formations frequently form narrow elongated grivas, exceeding in height the ridges composed of rocks of the main phases.

Within the described territory, the granitoids of the Zerende complex intrude undivided lower-to-middle Ordovician sediments and greenstone-altered volcanics of the Garzhinsky suite of the upper Proterozoic. The contact of the granites of the third phase with the lower-to-middle Ordovician sequence is observed, in particular, on the left bank of the Zhabai River to the east of the village of Maksimovka, where hornfelsed rocks are widely represented in the exocontact zone of the massif.

The described formations are overlain by lower Carboniferous sediments and a cover of Cenozoic deposits. In addition, the granitoids and monzodiorites of the Zerende complex are intruded by leucocratic granites of the Balkashino intrusive complex of the middle Devonian.

According to modern concepts, based on the characteristics of the gravitational field, the Zerende massif has a mushroom-like form and the thickness of the granitoid masses within its limits varies from 5 to 10 km. ("Geological Map ......", 1981, p. 192).

**Second phase of intrusion (δ²O₃)**

Quartz monzodiorites and granodiorites of the second intrusion phase within the study area form outcrops on the left bank of the Zhabai River, to the south of the village of Petrovka. Here they form gently sloping hills and ridges with numerous bedrock outcrops (L-42-XXXII). In addition, during mapping at a scale of 1:50,000, analogous rocks were identified beneath a cover of Quaternary deposits (mapping boreholes) to the north and northeast of the ruins of Zhanakol. The described rocks were assigned to the second intrusion phase on the basis of comparison with similar formations from other sections of the Zerende intrusive, where their relationships with the granitoids of the third phase were established (Serikh, 1963). Within the study area, exposed contacts — both between rocks of the second and third phases and between quartz monzodiorites and the enclosing stratified formations — are not known.

From the east, the quartz monzodiorites and granodiorites of the Zerende complex, exposed in the vicinity of Petrovka village, are cut by leucocratic granites of the Balkashino complex. The contact zone itself is very poorly exposed, making it difficult to determine the nature of the contact. Judging by isolated eluvial boulder accumulations, a certain hybridization of the rocks is present, expressed in structural changes, although it is difficult to determine unambiguously to which of the complexes these hybridized granitoids belong based on available data.

As stated above, the principal rocks of the second intrusive phase of the Zerende complex are quartz monzodiorites and granodiorites.

**Third phase of intrusion (γ₃O₃z, γδ₃O₃z)**

The rocks of the third intrusive phase make up the main part of the Zerende massif, both as a whole and within the studied territory. Their outcrop exposure and degree of denudation are poor, the same as for the granitoids of the second phase. The relationships of the rocks with the second phase of intrusion over the area of study are not clear; therefore, the granitoids described are assigned to the third phase on the basis of comparison with adjacent territories (Serykh, 1963).

The main rocks forming the third intrusive phase of the Zerende complex are granodiorites and granites.

**G r a n o d i o r i t e s** are very common rocks within the southern part of the Zerende massif. As noted by V.I. Serykh, toward the center of the massif they are replaced by more acidic varieties. A distinctive feature of the described rocks is the large number of xenoliths with dimensions ranging from 1 to 30 cm (Serykh, 1963).

In appearance, these are pinkish-grey, sometimes buff-tinted rocks, ranging from equigranular to porphyritic due to large segregations of predominantly potassium feldspar. The rocks are generally similar to those of the second intrusive phase. As an example, we provide a microscopic description of the porphyritic variety (sample 7826).

The structure of the rock is porphyritic. The quartz-feldspar groundmass constitutes 25% of the rock volume. The structure of the groundmass is allotriomorphic granular, with rare areas — granophyric.

A characteristic feature distinguishing the described granitoids — in particular from coeval formations of the Krykkuldu complex developed in adjacent geosynclinal areas — is the sharply elevated content of potassium feldspar, forming large (up to 10 cm) tabular segregations. V.M. Serykh, who studied these segregations in detail, was the first to classify them as porphyroblasts, substantiating this with a series of criteria (Serykh, 1963). The widespread development of potassium metasomatism processes within the Serendin massif is of fundamental importance, since, apparently, it is precisely these processes that account for the considerable scatter in absolute age values obtained for the described rocks predominantly by the potassium-argon method.

As can be seen from Table No. 51 and Diagram No. 77, the rocks of the Verendinsky complex are generally characterized by insignificant variations in alkali content. In the majority of analyses, the sum Na₂O + K₂O ranges within 7.5–8.0 wt. % and is practically independent of the SiO₂ content in the rock. In general, the described rocks tend somewhat toward the subalkaline series, which is especially manifested in the more melanocratic varieties (a similar picture was also observed in the rocks of the Kriankuduksky complex).

The ratio Na₂O/K₂O rarely exceeds 1.0; the most common values lie in the interval 0.7–0.9. Thus, although the described rocks as a whole belong to the potassium-sodium series, their potassic specialization is expressed quite distinctly (see Diagram No. 79).

The value Na₂O/K₂O is of interest in that it illustrates the similarity of the chemistry of rocks of the Verendinsky complex with the coeval formations of the Kriankuduksky complex (the Karatasskiy and Bulandı–Akkol'skiy massifs). This similarity is apparently related to the common features of the evolution of the listed intrusions, namely — to those periods of tectono-magmatic activation which they underwent in the post-Ordovician time, and as a result of which Silurian–Devonian, Silurian, and Middle Devonian leucocratic granites were emplaced, exerting a certain metasomatic effect on the composition of the host rocks.

By the aluminosity coefficient *al′* = Al₂O₃ / (Fe₂O₃ + FeO + MgO), the described formations belong to the category of highly aluminous and very highly aluminous rocks, with a clearly expressed tendency toward increasing aluminosity with increasing SiO₂ content in the rock (Diagram No. 78).

The CaO content, on the contrary, regularly decreases with increasing SiO₂, which finds its mineralogical expression in changes, above all, in the compositions of plagioclases.

Variations in the contents of FeO, Fe₂O₃, MgO are related to changes in the content in the rock and the composition of mafic minerals, as well as to their secondary alteration; however, we lack sufficient data to identify any regularities in this matter.

**Dike formations of the first stage**

Dike formations of the first stage are of limited distribution within the studied area. Their outcrops are most often associated with the fields of distribution of granitoids of the third intrusion phase, and specifically with their more acidic varieties (Serykh, 1963). In rarer cases they cut through country rocks (described on the left bank of the Konur River to the east of the village of Vasilyevka by N. Pan (Pan, 1966)), among Lower Proterozoic formations.

In terms of morphology, these are dikes whose thickness ranges from tens of centimeters to several meters. The strike of the dikes within the study area is predominantly northeast, less commonly northwest.

The formations described are assigned to dikes of the first stage on the basis of their relationships both with the older formations (third intrusion phase) and with the younger formations of the Zerende complex (Stage II dikes). Examples of these relationships, confirming the greater antiquity of the Stage I dikes relative to the Stage II dikes, have been cited repeatedly in the works of previous researchers (Serykh, 1963; Babichev, 1963).

The dike formations are composed exclusively of acidic rocks, highly diverse in composition and structural characteristics.

The diversity of the species composition of the dikes is explained by V.I. Serykh as reflecting a degree of diachroneity in the emplacement of the Stage I dikes, with their formation occurring in at least three stages (Serykh, 1963). The following are distinguished: fine-grained porphyritic granites, fine-grained dike granites, and aplites. The first type is characterized by a porphyritic texture conditioned by the presence of phenocrysts of plagioclase and quartz, and an aplitic (locally granulitic) texture of the groundmass.

For the second variety, porphyritic segregations are uncharacteristic; the texture is allotriomorphic granular. The third variety possesses an aplitic texture.

All rocks are fine-grained, less commonly medium- to fine-grained. The principal rock-forming minerals are plagioclase (albite, oligoclase–albite), alkali feldspars, and quartz. Accessory minerals: biotite. Accessory minerals: ore mineral, apatite.

All rocks are characterized by light, pinkish coloration.

**Dikes of the Second Stage**

Stage II dikes are more widely distributed within the study area than the vein formations of the first stage. They are confined to the rocks of both principal phases of the Zerendinsky complex and also occur among the host Proterozoic and Lower Paleozoic rocks. They have diverse strikes, among which northeast orientations predominate. In the relief, the described formations are expressed predominantly as narrow, elongated ridges with well-defined crests. The mineral composition of the dikes is varied — from acidic to intermediate. The numerous cross-cutting relationships between Stage II dikes observed within the Zerendinsky massif allowed V.I. Serykh to establish the following sequence of dike emplacement from oldest to youngest: 1) granite-porphyries, 2) granophyres, 3) dioritic porphyrites, 4) microspessartites, 5) spessartites, 6) microdiorites and granodiorite-porphyries, mutual relationships between which have not been established, 7) natric syenite-porphyries (Serykh, 1963).

**G r a n i t e - p o r p h y r i e s** are pinkish-grey, greyish-light-yellow and light-pink rocks with a microcrystalline groundmass, among which two varieties are distinguished: 1) with phenocryst content of 20–45% and 2) with phenocryst content of less than 5%. Among the phenocrysts, feldspar and quartz predominate; biotite is present in the first type. Dike thickness ranges from 1.5 m to 5.6 m.

**G r a n o p h y r e s** differ from granite-porphyries by the granophyric (sometimes spherulitic) texture of the groundmass and, in general, greater thickness (6–12 m). Phenocrysts in the granophyres are represented by large tabular crystals of K-feldspar, acidic plagioclase, and, rarely, hornblende and biotite. Granophyres form both independent bodies and the central portions of composite dikes. [illegible]

Phenocrysts (35–40%) are represented by feldspar and hornblende. Dike thickness ranges from 10–20 cm to 5–6 m, and frequently varies along the strike of the body.
