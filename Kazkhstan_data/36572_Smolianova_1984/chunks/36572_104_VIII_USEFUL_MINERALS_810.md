Within the investigated territory, the deposits of the sulfiferous horizon also hold certain prospects with respect to polymetallic mineralization. The carbonate and carbonate-terrigenous formations of the sulfiferous horizon are traced as a narrow strip for more than 30 km from southeast to northwest across the area of map sheets M-42-64-B and -65-V,G. These deposits are cut by numerous variously oriented tectonic dislocations into individual blocks, forming, principally, graben-synclines and blocks with monoclinal dip of strata.

The deposits of the sulfiferous horizon are represented by grey, dark-grey crystalline limestones, dolomitized limestones, and dolomites. In the lower sections of the sulfiferous horizon, calcareous siltstones and sandstones are observed.

Associated with the formations of the sulfiferous horizon are numerous secondary halos of lead, zinc, and copper, the large number of which enabled M.S. Grankin (1970) to identify in the southwestern part of map sheet M-42-65-B the polymetallic ore occurrence No. 29. It is confined to dolomites, limestones, and ferruginous-manganiferous rocks of the sulfiferous horizon. In structural terms, it is located in the eastern limb of a graben-syncline. Within tectonic zones, the limestones and dolomites are intensively ferruginized, manganized, and silicified. At the ore occurrence, secondary halos of lead — 0.001–0.03%, zinc — 0.01–0.1%, barium — 0.3%, manganese — 0.01–1.0%, copper — 0.01%, silver — 0.00001%, and strontium — 0.1% have been recorded. The halos are concentrated in areas of distribution of dolomites, manganized, and ferruginized rocks.

The element content in dolomites is as follows: lead 0.05–0.3%, zinc up to 0.005%, and copper 0.001–0.002%, while in the altered rocks, respectively, lead 0.001%, zinc 0.02–0.6%, and copper 0.003–0.006%. As is evident from the cited contents, in the altered rocks an accumulation of zinc and copper occurs, while lead, owing to the low solubility of its carbonate compounds, concentrates in the limestones and dolomites.

**Noble Metals**

The main part of the silver ore occurrences is located within the Kiiminskaya, Aukelbek, and Symtes synclines. The silver occurrence is evidently of sedimentary genesis. **Signs of copper occurrences**

In addition to the above-described ore occurrences of copper, polymetals, aluminium, silver and gold, which are listed in the catalogue of mineral resources, within the limits of the investigated area, signs of occurrences (mineralization points) of copper, lead and zinc have been identified. They were established at the surface and in boreholes, based on chip and core sampling of rocks.

Signs of copper occurrences are confined to various metallogenic levels and are localized in grey-coloured sandstones, siltstones and limestones enriched with carbonized plant detritus. At the day surface, this range of copper occurrences can be traced for the first few metres, rarely tens of metres, with a visible thickness of 0.5 to 3.0 m. Sometimes copper mineralization is noted in the form of individual spots. The mineralization is represented by crusts, films, smears and veinlets of malachite, rarely azurite. The copper content in oxidized ores, according to spectral analysis data, sometimes reaches 1.0%.

In boreholes, interlayers of mineralized rocks with a thickness of 0.1–1.0 m have been intersected. Copper mineralization is represented by sparse disseminations of chalcopyrite. The copper content in primary "ores," according to spectral analysis data, varies from thousandths to 0.4%.

Signs of lead and zinc occurrences were identified by prospecting boreholes. Polymetallic mineralization is confined to the middle Kairaktin and upper Kairaktin sub-formations and is localized in grey limestones, rarely siltstones and sandstones. Sometimes lead mineralization is observed in association with copper. The thickness of the mineralized rocks intersected by boreholes varies from 0.1 to 0.2 m, rarely reaching 1.0 m. **Non-metallic minerals**

**Rhodusite**

**R o d u s i t e o r e o c c u r r e n c e s No. 1, 2, 3** are located in the northwestern part of the Teniz Depression (sheet area M-42-UP) 4 km to the southwest and 8–12 km to the southeast from elevation mark 252.0 m.

Rhodusite mineralization in Upper Paleozoic deposits was first identified in Central Kazakhstan by F.V. Chukhrov in 1946 at the Kyzylborbas site of the Kumolinsky syncline. The first information on rhodusites of the Teniz Depression was provided by G.B. Parshin (Beyseyev, Parshin, 1974). The rhodusite mineralization point identified by G.B. Parshin is of mineralogical interest. Work carried out in 1983 by the Geological-Geophysical Expedition of SKPO (Gay, 1983) in the area of sheet M-42-UP in the Taldyk trough established rhodusite mineralization in deposits of the Kайraktinsky formation. Boreholes penetrated the lower part of the Kайraktinsky formation, represented by grey sandstones, siltstones, pelitomorphic limestones and marls. The dip of the strata is steep at angles of 20–25°.

The ore-hosting rocks are limestones and marls, rarely interbeds of calcareous siltstones. The rhodusite mineralization is confined mainly to light-grey brecciated and fractured marls, limestones, and rarely to siltstones. The thickness of the ore-hosting rocks, according to exploration borehole data, is not uniform. In borehole 3-8 they were intersected in the interval 70.0–160.0 m, and in borehole 3-15 at a depth of 80–120.0 m. The thickness of rhodusite-bearing rocks varies from 40 to 90.0 m.

Two types of rhodusite mineralization have been identified: vein-type and disseminated. Veinlets with rhodusite have a thickness of 0.3–2 cm, their length varies from 1 to 3 cm. In borehole 3-15, at a depth of 78.5 m, rhodusite mineralization is confined to tension fractures concordant with the bedding of the rocks. The thickness of the rhodusite interlayer is 5–6 mm. Veinlets with rhodusite mineralization are oriented at an angle of 60–70° relative to the core axis.

Based on textural-morphological characteristics, three varieties of rhodusite are distinguished: fibrous, lamellar, and loose. Fibrous dense rhodusite forms bristle-like aggregates. The fibers are cemented by montmorillonite, chlorite, chalcedony, and quartz. In places, rhodusite occurs together with calcite veinlets. Lamellar rhodusite consists of small platy aggregates that fill fractures concordant with bedding in siltstones. Loose rhodusite occurs in the form of crusts, coatings, and earthy masses in brecciated limestones. Macroscopically, all varieties of rhodusite have a blue color; under the microscope the coloration of this mineral is brown, colorless, and pale blue. The mineral exhibits clear pleochroism. It is fine-fibrous and radiated. This is an amphibole of the glaucophane group. Texture is fibroblastic. In places, the fabric of the rock is randomly oriented.
