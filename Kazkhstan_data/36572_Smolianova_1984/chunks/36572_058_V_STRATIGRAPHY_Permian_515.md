The composition of the clastic fraction is polymictic, but grains of feldspars and quartz predominate. Feldspars are represented mainly by plagioclases. Rock fragments are extremely diverse: acidic and intermediate porphyries and their tuffs, limestones, chloritic, quartz-chloritic, quartz-sericitic schists, argillites, microquartzites. As secondary clasts and admixtures in the rocks there are present: ore minerals – titanomagnetite, magnetite, biotite and chlorite fragments, fragments of carbonized plant tissue, fragments of ostracod shells.

Cement is carbonate, carbonate-chloritic, albite-quartz-

iron-bearing, ferruginous-chloritic. By pore-filling type – basal, regenerational, film-type.

The microstructure of the rocks is disordered or microlaminated, frequently clotted.

**Carbonate rocks** are represented by chemogenic limestones. The rock texture is microgranular, microcrystalline, with grain sizes less than 0.01 mm. Microclotted texture is frequently encountered. In all carbonate rocks, an admixture of terrigenous material is observed, of microsiltstone and clayey composition. It constitutes from 10 to 25%. Its quantity decreases upward through the section; the purest carbonate rocks are the nodular limestones of the Upper Kairakty subformation.

The composition of detrital grains of the terrigenous admixture: quartz, feldspars, insignificant quantities of rock fragments, ore grains; almost always present are fragments of carbonized plant tissue, 1–3%. Occasionally the limestones contain inclusions and lenses filled with coarsely crystalline calcite, barite-celestine. In the limestones of the Achily and Aukelbek synclines, practically in all thin sections, especially from the upper clays, valves and nuclei of ostracods are encountered. Ostracods are very abundant here; they are readily identified even during field observations. In all other structures they are significantly fewer.

By textural characteristics the following varieties are distinguished: stromatolitic, subparallel-bedded, massive, brecciated, breccia-shelly, oolitic, pseudo-oolitic, angular, and lace-like varieties of limestones.

**Stromatolitic limestones** are distributed within the Middle Kairakty subformation. In other parts of the section they were not encountered. The most powerful and laterally persistent beds of stromatolitic limestones were observed at the base of the Middle Kairakty subformation in the Achily syncline, on its western limb, in the core of the Mendybay and Shurumсay domes, and on the northern and southern limbs of the Aukelbek syncline.

In these structures they form nearly continuous, persistent beds with thicknesses up to 0.8 m with colonial structure. The structure **(Fig. 44)** of the colonies can be observed in the cliff faces of the Achily River, as well as in the reference stratigraphic section along the Kamen-Kairakty River, slightly upstream from the village of Belovodskoye.

In alluvium, these beds on the crests of ridges yield shells of oval form.

Solitary stromatolites of fairly large dimensions (Fig. 45) were encountered in the lower portions of the Srednekayrakt Formation, in its lower third, and sometimes also in its roof. In the latter case, small nodules of black chert were observed in the cores of the stromatolites (Fig. 46). In the Aukelbek syncline and especially on the domes of the Burumbaisk uplift, crystals of barite-celestine were observed in the cores of stromatolitic limestones (Fig. 47). Such stromatolitic limestones are clearly distinguished on the well-log curves of selective logging.

In colonial stromatolites the core is poorly developed; for solitary ones, especially those with chert and barite-celestine, a well-developed core and a laminated "streamlined" envelope are characteristic. The envelope is characterized by lamination caused by the alternation of layers with algal structures, generally of darker color, and layers that are lighter and thinner, enriched with terrigenous admixture. In weakly weathered specimens it is evident that the algal layers have a flaky-lumpy or bundle-canalicula structure (Fig. 48). During weathering these layers are destroyed more rapidly and are partially replaced by iron hydroxides and secondary calcite.

A characteristic feature of the envelope of stromatolitic limestones is the thinning of laminae on the lateral parts of the stromatolitic structure. The maximum thickness of the envelope is observed in the upper central portion, which is apparently related to the growth characteristics of the structure.

The core of a stromatolite is typically composed of algal limestones, as noted above, with nodules of chert, crystals of barite-celestine. Sometimes large prismatic, rhombic porphyroblastic segregations of calcite, less commonly of barite and barite-celestine, are observed in the cores of stromatolites (Fig. 47).

In boreholes, stromatolitic limestones are identified with reasonable confidence (Figs. 48, 49). Only in those cases where a borehole intersects the lateral parts of stromatolitic structures can they be confused with breccia-like shell limestones or with wavy-laminated ones.

According to thermal analysis data, they consist of calcite with admixtures of illite, quartz, and aragonite.

The rock texture is microgranular, evenly granular, or poorly defined clotted. An admixture of argillaceous and fine aleuritic material is observed. The composition of detrital grains of terrigenous admixture is quartz-feldspar, occasionally lithoid. In addition to terrigenous admixture, fragments of carbonized plant tissue, shells, and ostracod nuclei are almost always present in the rock. Sometimes the limestones contain large (3–5 mm) nests filled with crystalline calcite.

**Nodular limestones** are especially widespread in the upper part of the sequence in the Aukelbek, Symbaev, and Achilin synclines; they represent rocks of black, dark-gray color, dense. Against the background of a homogeneous carbonate mass, formations of rounded or irregular shape are observed in them, having the appearance of nodules. They differ from the main rock mass in their texture, but do not have sharp boundaries. In thin sections, the nodular limestones represent a fine-grained carbonate rock with an insignificant amount of aleuritic admixture (3–5%) and authigenic quartz. In some areas, organogenic-algal, clotted textures are observed. The limestones contain many ostracod shells.

In individual beds of nodular limestones, chert concretions of dark-gray and black color are observed, ranging in size from 3–5 to 10 cm in length with a thickness of 2–3 cm. The surface of the concretions is uneven, the boundaries are sharp. They are oriented parallel to the rock bedding. Chert concretions occur at the boundary of lenses in the upper Kairaktin substage.

In the Taldyk syncline, in borehole 145, argillaceous dolomites were encountered. This is a very dense, evenly granular rock consisting of equal amounts of argillaceous and carbonate material. Dolomite forms rhombic crystals in it, evenly distributed throughout the rock mass. Possibly it is of authigenic origin, i.e., this is a dolomitized argillaceous limestone. In the mass of such rock, minute aggregates of chlorite with pyrite are not uncommon. Inclusions of twinned aragonite are encountered. Twins frequently form intergrowths in the form of "hourglass" shapes.

According to measurements of the physical properties of the Kairaktin suite deposits, the described rocks possess low values of magnetic susceptibility on the order of 5–45·10⁻⁶ SI units, and in individual cases 190, 280·10⁻⁶ SI units. The density of the rocks ranges from 2.61 g/cm³ to 2.66 g/cm³. The age of the Kairaktin suite deposits, apart from their stratigraphic position, is determined by numerous finds of phyllopod and ostracod fauna, plant remains, and spore-pollen assemblages.

A large number of ostracods have been extracted from the rocks of the Kairaktin suite; their identification was carried out by S.B. Mamutova. The results of the identifications are compiled in Tables 29, 29a, arranged according to structural-facies zones. The species composition of the ostracods is the same in all zones, rich and diverse. Characteristic of the Kairaktin ostracod assemblage is an insignificant number (17–30%) of Late Carboniferous species and 4–10% of Upper Permian species. The number of Upper Permian species increases in the Upper Kairaktin sub-suite, while in the Lower Kairaktin they are practically absent. The distribution of ostracods across the area is uneven. In some places ostracods are so abundant that no effort is required to find them (Adilinsky synclinal basin, Aukelbek synclinal basin), while in individual structures they are almost absent (Galdinsky synclinal basin, Vladimirovsky and Tretyakovsky synclinal basins).

The following Lower Permian species are most frequently encountered in the assemblage: *Darwinula gabaevi* Belous., *D. malachovensis* Belous., *D. stelmachovia* Belous., *D. stelmachovella* Belous., *D. ordinata* Belous., *D. fidelis* Kasch., *D. fragiliformis* Belous., *D. nasaliformis* Belous., *D. kazakhstanica* Belous.
