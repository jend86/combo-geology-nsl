The study of dolomite rocks of the Simorinsky horizon has
shown that within the work area there are differences between
both the first and second groups. Among the sedimentation
(primary ) dolomites, we distinguish four varieties: pelitomorphic
uniform-grained, pelitomorphic lumpy, pelitomorphic
brecciated, and oolithic-pseudolitic. All of them are installed
in the core of well 150 and, partially, in the reference-

parametric well 06.

Pelitomorphic rough-
and-gray dolomites are gray-colored rocks,

they are massive and wavy-layered. They react sluggishly with
dilute, which distinguishes them (like other rocks of the prevailing pre-
lominitic composition) from similar-looking limestones. They are composed
of fine, pelitomorphic dolomite, equal to coarse-grained or non-coarse-
grained. In the form of thin lenses, they contain an admixture of clay-
siltstone material.

Pelitoid rocks

are dense, massive light gray rocks. The fragments
range in size from fractions of a millimeter (rounded, clump-
like) to several millimeters (angular, irregular shapes). They
are composed of very thin, almost black dolomite. The binding
mass is slightly better crystallized, and it is more transparent
in the slot. They contain numerous micro-nests and cracks
made of either monocrystalline calcite or crystalline quartz.

Pelitomorphic lumpy and clumpy

dolomites are characterized by an irregularly
grained structure. Lumps of rounded and oval- rounded shapes,
measuring 0.08-0.35 mm, are isolated, sticking together in
solid fields. They are composed of pelitomorin dolomite. The
lumps are cemented with small and medium-sized rhombometric
dolomite crystals (apparently diagenetic). Quartz develops
in sections along the pores. In some cases , crystalline
anhydrite precipitates are present in the rock. A peculiar variety of
the described rocks are clumped dolomites. The nature of
their microstructure makes it possible to assume their biogenic
algal origin.

We include a variety of clear-grained dolomites and
dolomitized limestone into the group of metasomatic dolomitic
rocks.

Clear-grained dolomites are
gray rocks of various shades. Their crystalline structure
and characteristic "sparkling" luster are clearly visible
on the fresh chip, which is better manifested in large-scale
crystalline differences. The rock textures are mottled,
crystalline, massive, and often porous, with pores encrusted
with carbonate crystals, usually calcite.

The microstructure of the described rocks is
granular, often unevenly grained. They are composed of small
(0.01-0.05mm) dolomite crystals of regular polygonal and rhombic
shapes, which can increase to 0.3-0.4mm in sections. Less
often, there are differences completely composed of crystals
measuring 0.2-0.4mm, usually zonal ("sugar-like" dolomites). A
characteristic feature of rocks is silicification. Silica (quartz
and chalcedony) forms granular and radiant aggregates in the
pores. There is an increase in the size of dolomite grains near the
silicified areas. Terrigenous admixture is generally not typical for
rocks; at best, it consists of single grains of an ore mineral and
small fragments of quartz.

Dolomitized limestones are
limestones of the limestone type - biomorphic- detritus,
pelitomorphic, crystalline - containing up to 50% of
the growths of romic dolomite crystals. "Poikillite"
structures are developed in crystal differences, when
small dolomite crystals are "cemented" with poikillite
calcite cement.

Sulfate rocks, gypsum and anhydrites, are widely distributed
among the rocks of the Simorinsky horizon. They are distributed
in the upper part of the horizon, mainly in the area of the
southern side of the Kipshak trough. Sulfates are not exposed
on the surface, as they are easily susceptible to weathering. The
core of rock containing anhydrite and dolomite, left in the air even
for a short time, becomes covered with a white powdery crust
and quickly collapses, most likely the so-called reverse Haidinger
reaction occurs here, occurring in the presence of water:

Two main varieties can be distinguished among
anhydrite-containing rocks : anhydrites that are the product
of primary deposition (chemogenic, syngenetic) and

Syngenetic anhydrites

form fairly stable interlayers among carbonate rocks.
These are macroscopic rocks of fine-grained structure,
massive or lenticular-wavy. The color is gray, light gray,
white with blue and pink shades. Microscopic examination
shows that these rocks are composed mainly (from
95% to b0") of small prismatic crystals of anhydrite. The
rock structure is uniformly-irregularly grained, less often

calcite and dolomite carbonate rocks are widely represented.
The carbonate in them is usually fine-grained, forming
loose microlenses and layers. Occasionally the rocks are
silicified. The syngenetic origin of anhydrite is indicated by
the formation form of its occurrence, association with pelitomorphic carbonates,
and micrograin.

Diagenetic anhydrites

(refilled) are usually found in the form of large (up
to cm) isometric shaped nests in dolomitic rocks, they
are usually better crystallized than syngenetic differences
(medium- coarse-grained). In addition to anhydrite

large crystals of carbonates are developed in these nests.

Kassinsky Horizon ( C, ks )

The deposits of the Kassinsky horizon are involved in the structure
of the Kipshak trough and the southern side of the Teniz depression.

The alignment of the rocks of the horizon is different. The
lower part is usually not exposed; at the place of its outcrops on the
surface, there is a well-defined hollow in relief, bounded on one side
by a ridge composed of dolomites of the Simorinsky horizon, on
the other by limestones of the upper part of the horizon. The base
of the upper part can be clearly traced along the entire southern
side of the trough in the form of a low ridge with several strips

of limestone outcrops.

The decipherability of sediments is good (Fig. 15). On aerial
photographic materials, the ridges are traced in the form of white stripes on gray

As noted above, the deposits of the Kassinsky horizon
are composed of a complex of terrigenous and carbonate rocks.
Terrigenous rocks are mainly represented by sandstones.
They are usually light gray, almost white in color,
massive, "draining" appearance (in the core of wells). From
fine-grained (0.08-0.5 mm) to coarse-grained (more than one mm).
Differences in mixed granulometric composition are common (fine-

-243-

medium-grained, coarse-grained, etc.). The sorting of
grains varies, and according to the limited data available to
us, its degree increases westward from the southern side of
the Kipshak trough. The roundness is also diverse, usually
no higher than average, Often the degree of rolling is difficult
to establish due to the abundance of corrosion-regenerative forms. According to the
composition of clastic grains, two large varieties can be distinguished:
feldspar-quartz sandstones with approximately equal content
of quartz fragments and feldspar, and substantially quartz
sandstones in which the quartz content can significantly
exceed 50% of clastic grains. Lithoid clastic grains form
a more or less significant admixture (up to 15%) quartz and
feldspar fragments. Other impurities include rounded biogenic
debris, ore grains, chlorite, and flakes of hydrobiotite (?).
The nature of cementation and the composition of cement are
variable even within a single sample. The following types prevail:
indentation cementation, regeneration, pore and semi-basal.
The composition of the cement is clay-carbonate, clay-siliceous,
clay- mica, pure carbonate. The widespread development of
bicarbonate calcite-dolomite cement is characteristic, in which calcite
fills the pores (sometimes up to poikiyalite cementation), and
dolomite forms small rhombic growths in these pores or
in pores made of clay.

Clay-anhydrite cement is found as a rare type.

Siltstones are not widely represented in the described sediments,
and we will not dwell on them in the description.

represented by limestones and dolomites. Limestones
are characterized by a wide variety of structural types. We
distinguish: pelitomorphic uniformly grained limestones with calcite
nests, pelitomorphic lumpy limestones; biomorphic detritus and
biodetritus limestones; algal (?) limestones; oolitic limestones
