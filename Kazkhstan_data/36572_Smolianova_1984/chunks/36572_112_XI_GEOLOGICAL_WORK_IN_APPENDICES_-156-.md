**CHAPTER XI. GEOPHYSICAL INVESTIGATIONS IN BOREHOLES**

Drilling and, consequently, geophysical investigations in boreholes were conducted on the studied territory in areas of development of deposits of the Kishiansk, Kirei, Tereakkan, Tenis, Nairaktinsk, Kitiminsk, and Poltykul suites of Carboniferous and Permian age. The primary significance in the geological section is held by terrigenous sedimentary rocks with a predominance of sandstones, siltstones, siltstone-sandstones, and limestones. Shales and volcanics are of subordinate significance.

Rocks of various lithological composition are characterized by different physical and radioactive properties (specific electrical resistivity, density, natural radioactivity, effective atomic number, and natural spontaneous polarization).

Different lithological varieties possess definitive geophysical characteristics. Changes in geophysical values on diagrams are caused solely by the lithological affiliation of rocks and are almost independent of their age. Suite boundaries, as a rule, are not distinguished. An indirect indicator of their location is the sharp change in lithological varieties of rocks during the transition from deposits of one suite to another.

At the given site, the following geological problems were solved using geophysical methods:

— lithological subdivision of geological sections of boreholes;

— identification in the section of intervals with elevated content of radioactive elements;

— detection of ore intervals in the geological section of boreholes;

— monitoring of the technical condition of boreholes.

**1. Methodology of Geophysical Work**

**1.1. Electrical Methods**

Geophysical measurements were performed by logging stations AZKS-1500 and SKV-69 with visual recording. Registration of resistivity (RS) diagrams was performed by a bottom-type gradient probe A1.2M0.1M. The resistivity scale was selected depending on the electrical resistivity of the rock formations and borehole conditions. Due to the large work area, rock resistivities vary within wide limits; accordingly, the recording scale also varied from 10 to 100 ohm·m and 1 ohm·m.

SP measurements were performed with electrode M, gradient – SP – with probe M0.1M, as a rule, at a scale of 25 /cm. The recording speed of RS and SP diagrams did not exceed 1000 m/hr.

Recording of SP diagrams was performed with a standard unit with a zinc brush electrode, at a scale of 12.5–25 /cm, at a speed of no more than 300 m/hr.

To assess the quality and accuracy of measurements, repeat measurements were conducted in each borehole over an interval of no less than 10% of the surveyed interval. Discrepancies between primary and repeat measurements did not exceed 10%.

Borehole diameter measurements were performed with caliper KM-2. Before and after completion of the CAL diagram registration, calibration of the caliper was carried out on rings of known diameter.

In addition, to assess the accuracy of the caliper operation, measurements were performed within the interval of casing pipes. The error in diameter measurements did not exceed 10 mm.

**1.2. Radioactive Well Logging**

Measurements of natural and scattered gamma radiation were performed using RSKM instrumentation.

Gamma logging was conducted in accordance with the requirements of the "Provisional Methodological Guidelines for Mass Prospecting of Uranium Deposits." The recording scale for natural radioactivity was 1.5–3 μR/hr/cm.

On the GK diagrams, values of the natural background, equipment sensitivity from the working reference standard (before and after operations), and operational stability of the equipment were recorded. The recording speed did not exceed 360 m/hr. Control measurements were performed at a volume of 10%.

GTK-S diagrams were recorded using a borehole sonde with a clamping device, a probe spacing of 0.09 m, and RSKM instrumentation. An isotope [illegible]–75 was used as the source of ionizing radiation, with a power of 3–10 [illegible]; the recording speed of the GTK-S curve was up to 360 m/hr, at a fixed time constant of 1.5–3 sec. The intensity recording scale was selected depending on borehole conditions and the presence of useful components in the borehole cross-section.

**2. Interpretation Methodology**

In processing geophysical data for the purpose of lithological subdivision of borehole cross-sections, delineation of intervals containing electrically conductive sulfide inclusions, and refinement of stratigraphic boundaries of rock formations, a combined interpretation method was applied, based on the physical properties of rocks.

Contacts between lithological rock varieties were determined from the complex of geophysical methods. Sandstones and limestones exhibit high specific electrical resistivity values (30–2500 Ω·m) compared to siltstones (20–750 Ω·m), and low natural radioactivity values (1–9 μR/hr). The natural radioactivity radiation intensity of siltstones amounts to 12–20 μR/hr.

A characteristic feature of carbonate sediments is the intense absorption of scattered gamma radiation, which is evidently associated with elevated barium content.

Rock intervals enriched in sulfides are marked by positive anomalies on the SP curve. On the GGK-S diagrams, disseminated sulfides, as a rule, are not distinguished. This is due to the lower resolving capability of the method compared to SP, and the content of the useful component. Intervals with elevated sulfide content on the resistivity (KS) curve are distinguished by decreased values.

Taking into account the above-described signs of geophysical parameters and anomaly polarities, ore intervals with disseminated and especially high-grade sulfide mineralization are confidently identified.

## 3. Results of Work

The results of geophysical work are presented on the drawings.
The use of well logging for constructing geological cross-sections along boreholes is evident from the drawings themselves. Most often, logging refined the positions of boundaries of individual beds and packages. The accepted geological column was constructed taking into account the interpretation of logging diagrams. Let us examine in more detail the identification of sulfide mineralization in boreholes.

All investigated boreholes can be divided into two groups. The first group of boreholes — those in which intervals of disseminated sulfide mineralization are identified in the geological section of sedimentary rocks; the second group — non-ore boreholes.

As a rule, sulfide mineralization is confined to sandstones and siltstones, less commonly to limestones, and is distributed throughout the entire borehole section or its lower part. Sulfide mineralization with low content is distinguished only from SP diagrams.

In boreholes Nos. 136, 137, 138, 133, 139, 155, 162, 163, 165, 164, 161, 170, 387, 388, 390, 393, 395, 396, 397, 398, 399 there is, as a rule, insignificant sulfide dissemination throughout the entire borehole section.
