**CENOZOIC GROUP (Fig. 66)**
**PALEOGENE SYSTEM**
**Upper Oligocene (P₂³)**

Continental deposits of the Upper Oligocene are represented by sandy-argillaceous and sandy-conglomeratic sequences of lacustrine and alluvial genesis. At the present time they are eroded over the greater part of the studied territory, and where preserved, are in most cases buried beneath younger formations.

The Upper Oligocene sequence everywhere rests with an unconformity on the weathering crust. In cases where the weathering crust is structural, the boundary is sharp; if the weathering crust is argillaceous and clays also occur at the base of the Oligocene, the lower boundary of the Upper Oligocene deposits is established with difficulty, based on a noticeable enrichment of the Oligocene clays with sandy material. Only in the Kara-Turgay basin (west of the described area) do the Upper Oligocene deposits rest on rocks of the Middle Oligocene Chiliktinskaya suite; throughout the remainder of the territory — on older formations, including rocks of the folded basement. The Miocene deposits and younger Cenozoic formations rest unconformably on the Upper Oligocene rocks.

The Upper Oligocene rock sequence, in previously published maps at scale 1:200,000 by various authors, was correlated either with the Chagraiskaya suite of the northern Priaralye, or with the Navruzumskaya suite, presently considered the stratigraphic equivalent of the Chagraiskaya suite. Within the Turgay synclinorium, deposits of analogous composition are assigned to the Chelkannuranskaya, Naidagulskaya suites and, possibly, to the lower portions of the Terekskoye suite. Over a significant part of the Tenizkaya depression these deposits are widely distributed. They are developed in isolated areas, in individual depressions of the ancient relief, many of which have been partially inherited by the modern erosional network. They form erosional outliers on watershed uplands or are exposed in the flanks of watersheds composed of Lower Pleistocene loams.

Dense opoka-like rocks and quartzite-like sandstones, upon weathering, serve as armoring surfaces forming the relief of table-like erosional remnants. Clays are exposed on the slopes of hills. Outcrops of Upper Oligocene sequences are usually surrounded by deluvial trains, draped like a mantle over the erosional remnants. Rounded fragments of siliceous and opoka-like rocks are frequently redeposited and encountered within Pliocene and Quaternary deposits, already in secondary occurrence in the form of blocks, boulders, and pebbles. Individual outcrops of sandstones and conglomerates are frequently encountered in the form of blocks and "perched caps" on the watershed and near-river small-hill terrain.

The sequence is well interpreted by irregular white and grey patches with scalloped margins, characteristic lace-like pattern (fig. 68).

The most complete sections of the Upper Oligocene on sheet M-42-XV are described to the north of Lake Aksu, along a mapping borehole near elevation 373, where their thickness is 19 m; on the watershed between lakes Tengiz and Mailykoľ, where their thickness exceeds 15 m.

On sheet M-42-XIV, on the left bank of the Tersakkан River, upstream from the settlement of the state farm named after A. Matrosov, O.V. Minervín described a section of Upper Oligocene deposits from boreholes. Their thickness is 35 m, with the sandy lower part being 15–18 m, and the upper clayey part 17–18 m. North of the Tersakkан River, in mapping boreholes No. 378, 379, the thickness of the Upper Oligocene deposits decreases to 12–13 m due to reduction of the lower sandy part of the section (fig. 67).

On the area of sheet M-42-IX there are numerous surface outcrops of Upper Oligocene deposits, which have been studied in great detail and mapped during geological surveys at scale 1:50,000, the results of which have been transferred to the map at scale 1:200,000 proposed in the report (fig. 4). Thicknesses of Upper Oligocene deposits here range from 10–15 to 35–40 m.

On the territory of sheet M-42-VII, Upper Oligocene deposits have been preserved in the form of very small erosional remnants on the near-river low-sandy plain, or are penetrated by some mapping boreholes. Their thickness does not exceed 5–6 m.

The lower part of the Upper Oligocene section on the territory of sheet M-42-VII was studied in detail by V.V. Taranin along the Nursulu stream. Thickness does not exceed 10 m. The area of development of Upper Oligocene deposits shown on the proposed map (Fig. 6 ) is considerably reduced in comparison with the published map at scale 1:200,000 (author N.M. Prudius), since on the latter, weathering crusts developed on Lower Carboniferous sediments were attributed to Upper Oligocene deposits. A regular increase in the thickness of Upper Oligocene deposits is observed on the area of sheet M-42-VII to the west of the studied territory, where it reaches 38–41 m and where the sections of Upper Oligocene deposits are most complete and characterized by finds of spores and pollen.

Significant surface exposures of Upper Oligocene deposits on the area of sheet M-42-II are observed in the Miren-Aigyr tract, on the left bank of the Inim River, to the south of the settlement of Iniima. Here there are numerous clay pits, from which white kaolin-like clays of the Upper Oligocene are used by the local population for construction purposes. Also here, to the south of the settlement of Bitei, on published maps at scale 1:200,000, a comparatively large outcrop of Upper Oligocene deposits is delineated. On the proposed maps (Fig. 7 ), the delineation of the Upper Oligocene deposits here has been substantially revised based on the results of mapping boreholes of the Kustanai Geological-Geophysical Expedition, which were studied by the authors (see Fig. 49 ).

The described section characterizes a sequence of lacustrine-alluvial genesis.

In the northern part of the studied territory (sheets No. 42–XXXIII–XXXIV), Upper Oligocene deposits form small erosional outliers with rubble of well-ferruginized, ferruginous-siliceous concretions on the plateau-like surfaces of these outliers. Semi-cemented white siltstones and quartz sandstones are sometimes observed in the slopes of the outliers.

**Clays**, according to thermal analysis data, are hydromica-kaolinitic, kaolinitic with admixture of quartz. Their color is white, violet-white, yellowish, greenish-grey, greyish-white. The coloration of the rocks is often mottled, which is caused by uneven distribution of iron hydrooxides. The clays are plastic, fatty, sometimes with admixture of sand, inclusions of flint and quartz pebbles up to 5–6 cm in size. The quantity of pebbles increases both upward and downward through the section. Concretions of iron hydrooxides up to 0.8–1 cm in size are encountered, often with an admixture of siliceous material.

**Sands** in composition are quartzose, from fine-grained to coarse-grained, with gravel and fine pebbles predominantly of siliceous composition. The roundness of the clastic material is variable, but more commonly good. Admixture of clayey material is significant.

**Weakly cemented sandstones** apparently formed as a result of secondary cementation processes of sands by clayey, clayey-ferruginous and gypsum cements. In connection with secondary ferruginization processes, the sequence is locally colored in cherry, brown, and lilac tones. Ferruginization creates pocket-shaped penetrations into the depth of the rock.
