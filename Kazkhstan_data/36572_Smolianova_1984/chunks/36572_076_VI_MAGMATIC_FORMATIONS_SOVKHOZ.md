**D e l u v i a l – p r o l u v i a l** deposits on the studied territory are very widely distributed. They form *shleify* [slope wash fans], developed on the valley walls, in floor depressions, and in inter-hill lowlands. This stratum overlies, principally, Paleozoic rocks, on watershed loams, and on older clays of alluvial origin. On aerial photographs they are distinguished by their characteristic "streaked" pattern, conditioned by the presence of a large number of small channels of ephemeral streams (Fig. 75).

The lithological composition of these formations is directly dependent on the substrate rocks over which they develop. *Shleify* occupying inter-hill depressions or developed at the foot of slopes are, as a rule, composed of loams and sandy loams with a significant admixture of randomly distributed gravel–boulder material and lenses of weakly rounded and poorly sorted sand, gravel, pebbles, and rubble.

For *shleify* developed over clays and loams on valley walls and floor depressions, a loamy composition is characteristic. Loams and sandy loams in composition and textural features are almost indistinguishable from the parent rocks. Only the more friable appearance of the rocks, the presence of rare pebbles, lenses of clayey composition, and lenses of reworked clayey sand allows these deposits to be distinguished. The boundary with the watershed loams is drawn conditionally on the basis of the presence of traces of slope erosion by shallow floor depressions and traces of sheet wash.

Thickness of *shleify* deposits from 1 to 4–5 m.

The age is adopted on the basis of geological–geomorphological analysis. These deposits are developed frequently over watershed loams of Lower Quaternary age, and on the valley walls pass into the sediments of the second floodplain terrace, having complex interrelationships with it. *Shleify* everywhere form inclined surfaces, dissected by the modern gully network. Their stratigraphic position, in this manner, allows them to be dated within the interval Middle Pleistocene–Holocene.

**Contemporary Deposits ( Q IV )**

Contemporary deposits are divided by genesis into lacustrine and alluvial.

Among the contemporary alluvial deposits two main types can be distinguished: fluvial and gully alluvium. Gully alluvium is always characterised by a predominantly loamy composition, and in many respects resembles in appearance the deluvial deposits of slopes.

**- 257 -**

River alluvium has its own characteristics, related to the hydrogeological regime of the rivers of the described territory. Many rivers of the district dry up in summer. The conditions of accumulation and composition of alluvial deposits on the floors of their valleys often differ very little from those of gully deposits. Rivers frequently lack a morphologically defined channel. This determines the conditional nature of the accepted subdivision.

The composition and structure of alluvium, even on one and the same river, does not remain uniform along different stretches of its valley. In the area of the latitudinal stretch of the Ishim River, both in the modern channel and in the buried channel alluvium, fine-grained sands with loamy interlayers predominate, frequently replaced by dark-colored clays of oxbow facies. The upper horizons of the alluvium of both the low and high floodplains are entirely composed of loams, which in a continuous cover overlie the broad valley floor. The loams are frequently enriched with gypsum and are even salinized.

Modern alluvial deposits of the right tributaries of the Ishim River are distinguished by an even finer-grained composition than the alluvium of the Ishim River. In them, especially on small rivers, loams and sandy loams predominate. The rock textures are thinly laminated. The color of the rocks, as a rule, is yellowish-brown in the high floodplain, while in the low floodplain more dirty grayish-brown tones predominate. Interlayers enriched with humus are common. Small interlayers and lenses of sands occur only in the lower part of the sequence. On the rivers Zhaman-i Zhaksy-Kairakty, Archaly, Konur, Zilandinka, stretches of the channel deeply incised into bedrock are observed. On such stretches, modern alluvium is practically absent, reduced to small patches of low-capacity sandy and gravel-pebble deposits on the bedrock floor of the river. Similar stretches with practically absent modern alluvium are characteristic of the drying-out channels of saevs, which are tributaries of the lower course of the Tersakkan River.

The channel of the Tersakkan River in its middle and upper reaches is characterized by relatively coarse-grained alluvium. Its upper floodplain horizons are composed of laminated brown loams with thin, humus-stained interlayers and numerous discontinuous interlayers and lenses of sand and gravel. The lower channel horizons are composed of variably grained sands and gravel. Near large stagnant pools, the alluvium acquires a more clayey composition. Silty sands and clays make up a large part of its sequence, and also cover the channel floor.

Modern lacustrine deposits are present in all parts of the described territory. Traces of a recently higher lake level are observed. They are expressed in the form of one, and in some places (on Lake Teniz) a series of step-like low terraces and ancient beach ridges. Young, well-preserved abrasion scarps are frequently found located far from the present water's edge and delimiting the outer margin of sometimes quite extensive swamped or salt-flat-covered coastal lowlands that were, evidently, until recently the lake bottom. Modern lacustrine deposits are widespread within such lowlands and on the floors of present-day lakes. In the majority of cases they are represented by silty, often dark-colored, organics-enriched clayey sediments. Such lacustrine deposits are especially characteristic of water bodies having a flat shoreline composed predominantly of clayey and loamy formations (in particular, watershed loams). Even under such conditions, sandy deposits are widespread in the coastal zone of larger water bodies; these result from the elutriation of sandy particles contained in the wave-eroded loamy bluffs. Sandy coastal deposits are even more commonly formed on lakes in the northern part of the studied area, in localities where granitoid massifs are found nearby. On such lakes (e.g., Yaki–Yangistau) almost the entire coastal strip is composed of weakly rounded, sometimes gravelly sands that form substantial (up to 2 m) beach ridges.

Modern lacustrine deposits of the southern part of the area are distinguished by elevated salinity. Frequently, especially on such lakes as Kaltchan, Kirei, Teniz, they coat the lake bottoms — exposed during the dry season — with a white efflorescence or even a salt crust.

True self-precipitating (salt-pan) lakes are absent within the described territory. A number of lakes in the southern part of the territory greatly reduce their area during the dry season or dry out completely. The floor of such water bodies at that time represents either an overgrown shallow swamp, or a swampy solonchak meadow, or is entirely bare of vegetation (or on individual sections) and covered by dark silty sediment cracked by desiccation.

Adequate data for determining the thickness of modern lacustrine deposits are lacking. In large lake basins it may reach a considerable magnitude.
