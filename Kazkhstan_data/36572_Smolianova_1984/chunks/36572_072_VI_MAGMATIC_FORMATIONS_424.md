Deposits of the Upper Pliocene outcrop at the surface in the valley of the Irgiz River at its confluence with the Aman-Kairakty River and in the Ishim depression to the north of the settlement of Bratolyubovka. On the area of sheet N-42-XXXIII they outcrop at the surface in the valley of the Kochubai stream and on the watershed of the Labai–Ailandina rivers. Over the remaining territory they are encountered in numerous boreholes, trenches, and adits beneath the Lower Quaternary "watershed" loams, beneath the deposits of the second floodplain terrace, and beneath present-day lacustrine deposits.

Upper Pliocene deposits overlie the deposits of the Teniz Suite, and in this case a gradual transition from the clays of the Teniz Suite to the Upper Pliocene clays is frequently observed (Fig. 69).

The color of the rocks in this case is often brown with interlayers and lenses of dirty gray-brown color, and the rock sequence consists exclusively of clays. Precisely such a section is observed in borehole D-43 to the northeast of the settlement of Borisovka on the sheet M-42-II, where beneath the deposits of the watershed loams of the Lower Pleistocene, at a depth of 15 m, dark-gray, nearly black, dense viscous clays occur, rich in plant detritus, bone remains, and bivalve shells. The thickness of these deposits is 11 m. They overlie greenish-gray weathering crust clays (Figs. 72, 73, 74). The bone remains belong to fossil beavers and muskrats, according to the conclusion of G.F. Lychev, in the Middle–Late Pliocene.

In borehole D-100 to the southwest of the settlement of Kairakty on sheet M-42-II, beneath the "watershed" loams with a thickness of 15 meters, the following section occurs (top to bottom):

1. Clays, dark-brown, sandy — 10 m.
2. Clays, dark-gray with a greenish tint, thinly laminated with interlayers enriched in sandy siliceous material — 7 m.
3. Clays, light-gray, sandy, carbonatized — 2 m.
4. Clays, gray with a greenish tint, with admixture of sandy material — 4 m.
5. Clays, gray, dark-gray with sandy material at the base of the layer — 3 m.
6. Clays, light-gray, packed with quartz gravel, flints, plant fragments — 3 m.

Total thickness of the described section — 29 m.

Apparently, the lower part of this section was observed by the authors in the steep banks of the right bank of the Zhaman-Kairakty River, in outcrop D-12 (Fig. 74). Here, in high cliffs beneath the alluvial-proluvial loams of the Middle–Upper Pleistocene, greenish-gray clays occur, enriched with leaf flora, and beneath them — gravel composed of siliceous pebbles. From the clays, from sample D-12, a spore-pollen assemblage has been identified (Table 39).

By lithological composition, among the Upper Pliocene deposits, sands, gravels, and clays are distinguished.

**Sands** are predominantly quartzose, gray-brown, greenish-gray, variably grained, well-washed and clayey, heavily waterlogged, with gravel and pebbles.

**Gravels** are cemented with clayey cement, consist of well-rounded, often flattened pebbles. Their composition is polymictic.

**Clays** are black, earth-gray, less commonly brown in color, dense, heavy, plastic, humified, with numerous plant remains, pyrite concretions, marly nodules; frequently contain interbeds enriched with sandy material.

The genesis of the deposits is lacustrine, alluvial, and alluvial-lacustrine. The presence of gray, black, brown, often silty clays, humified with interbeds and lenses of sands, indicates formation of sediments under floodplain, oxbow, and backwater lake conditions.

The age of the sequence is determined not only by its stratigraphic position: it overlies deposits of the Tenizia Suite and is overlain by Lower Pleistocene "watershed" loams, but also by spore-pollen assemblages (Table ). According to R.A. Tereshchenko, the spectra from samples D-12, D-16 correspond to spectra previously obtained from clays underlying the "watershed" loams and described by O.M. Rozanov and V.P. Kapsomun as the Airtava sequence, which is dated to the Lower Pleistocene. The spectra of borehole D-43 are characterized by negligible content of arboreal pollen, spores, and a predominance of herbaceous plant pollen.

Among arboreal species, pine (*Pinus sylvestris*) pollen is most abundant; small quantities of spruce, birch, and alder pollen were noted. Pollen of broad-leaved species was encountered singly: hornbeam, walnut, hazel, oak, elm.

In the group of non-arboreal plants, the dominant position was occupied by grasses, goosefoot family, wormwood, Asteraceae; pollen of aquatic plants (*Nuphar luteum* Sm., *Polygonum amphibium* L., *Myriophyllum*) is consistently recorded, as well as pollen of riparian-aquatic and plants growing under strongly waterlogged conditions. This indicates accumulation of characteristic deposits under conditions of oxbow lakes, overgrown lakes, and heavily waterlogged meadows.

Spore and pollen spectra are correlated with spectra from the Bityene [illegible] layers. The age of the deposits hosting this spectrum is Upper Pliocene.
