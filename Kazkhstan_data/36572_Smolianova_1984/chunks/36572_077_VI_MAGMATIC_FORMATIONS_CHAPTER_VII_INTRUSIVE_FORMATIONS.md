**CHAPTER VII. INTRUSIVE FORMATIONS**

Intrusive formations play a significant role in the geological structure of the framing and basement of the Teniiz Depression. They are composed predominantly of rocks of intermediate and acidic composition: diorites, granodiorites, granites, and their subalkaline analogues. Intrusions of basic and alkaline composition are distributed in subordinate quantities.

The greater part of the intrusive bodies occurring within the studied territory has a multiphase structure and is frequently formed by rocks of different intrusive complexes. Using primarily the results of preceding geological survey and thematic works, within the studied area we distinguish intrusive formations of different ages belonging to the following intrusive complexes:

I. Intrusions of Late Ordovician age (O₃)
- a) Krynkuduk complex (O₃k)
- b) Zerendinsky complex (O₃z)
- c) Stepnyaksky complex (O₃S)

II. Intrusions of Silurian age (S)
- a) Aralaulsky complex (Sar)

III. Intrusions of Silurian–Early Devonian age (S–D₁)
- a) Ishimsky complex (S–D₁i)
- b) Borovsky complex (S–D₁b)

IV. Intrusions of Middle Devonian age (D₂)
- a) Balkashinskiy complex (D₂b)

Within each individual massif, among the rocks of one or another complex, either bodies of several intrusion phases are distinguished, or they are regarded as undivided formations of the given complex. The degree of subdivision depends primarily on the degree of study of the intrusive body.

**I. Krynkuduk Complex (O₃k)**

two main intrusion phases, phases of additional intrusions, vein rocks of the first stage and dikes of the second stage ("Geological Map of the Kazakh SSR, scale 1:500,000," 1981).

This scheme is also adopted by us as the basis for the subdivision of intrusive formations correlated with the Krynkuduk complex within the studied territory. The following are distinguished:

**Rocks of the first intrusion phase:** diorites, quartz diorites, granodiorites, tonalites, adamellites. Subalkaline analogues of rocks of the diorite–granite series are fairly widely distributed. Granites (predominantly plagioclase and biotite–hornblende varieties) are developed in subordinate quantities (δO₃k; γδO₃k; γiO₃k).

**Rocks of the second intrusion phase:** granodiorites, biotite and biotite–hornblende granites (γ₂O₃k; γδ₂O₃k).

**Rocks of the additional intrusion phase** are represented by leucocratic aplite-like granites and granite porphyries (γ'O₃k).

**Vein rocks of the first stage:** fine-grained granites, aplites, granite porphyries, pegmatites.

**Dikes of the second stage** – diorite porphyrites, diabases, spessartites, granite porphyries, granodiorite porphyries.

In addition, we distinguish intrusive formations correlated with the Krynkuduk complex but not subdivided into phases.

These include small bodies mapped in the central part of the Marinovsky uplift and a number of massifs concealed at depth, revealed by boreholes or established from geophysical data beneath the cover of Upper Paleozoic deposits within the Teniz depression. The rock assemblage composing the undivided intrusions of the Krynkuduk complex is analogous to that of the subdivided formations: granites (γO₃k), granodiorites (γδO₃k), diorites (δO₃k); granite porphyries (γπO₃k), granodiorite porphyries (γδπO₃k).
