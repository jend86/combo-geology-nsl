Here, rodingite-asbestos forms thin veinlets (0.5–2 cm) in alkaline peridotites and congresites and represents longitudinally fibrous individuals. The length of the veinlets ranges from several centimeters to 1.5–2.0 m, with fiber length of 0.5–2.0 cm.

The genesis of rodingite occurrences in the Taldyk syncline is probably sedimentary. This is evidenced by their strict stratification, the confinement of rodingite mineralization to specific litho-facies rock types, and the absence of signs of metamorphism and hydrothermal alteration in the rocks hosting the rodingite. Thus, taking into account the geological-structural, litho-facies, and mineralogical-petrographic characteristics of rodingite occurrences, the Taldyk syncline should be considered prospective for the discovery of new rodingite occurrences. Exploration work should first be concentrated on the northern framing of the Taldyk syncline. The ore-hosting deposits of the Kairakty Formation are here, probably, fractured, brecciated and, consequently, prospective. The western limb of the structure (beyond borehole No. Z-46) is evidently less prospective, as the rocks here dip gently, are less disturbed, and carry weak rodingite mineralization (borehole No. 1-14-B). In the southeastern part of the Taldyk syncline, the rocks of the Kairakty Formation favorable for rodingite localization lie at great depth and are, apparently, weakly mineralized (borehole No. T-14-B). Here also a barrier (uplift) is observed, beyond which rodingite mineralization, in the southeastern direction, probably does not extend. The rodingites identified in boreholes No. Z-8, Z-15, Z-46 are, in terms of their structural-textural, chemical, and X-ray structural characteristics, very similar to the rodingites of Kumola and Umbulak of the Dzhaskezgen depression.

**Secondary Dispersion Halos of Useful Components**

On the forecast maps of mineral deposits, secondary dispersion halos of copper, lead, zinc, and silver have been plotted, as well as anomalous silver contents in individual lithogeochemical samples. The halos of lead and zinc were examined above in the description of polymetallic mineralization and are not described here. Among the identified copper halos, the majority are confined to the deposits of the Tersakkansk and Kiiminsk suites, as well as the lower Kairaktinsk subsuite.

In the deposits of the Dairin suite, individual small halos with copper concentrations of 0.007% have been identified, localized in grey arkosic sandstones.

In the Tersakkansk suite, as in other stratigraphic subdivisions, copper halos are closely associated with manifestations of copper mineralization and are controlled, primarily, by layers of grey-coloured sandstones and siltstones, less commonly by red beds. In plan, the copper halos have varying orientations but, as a rule, are subordinate to the strike of the rocks. Structurally, they are in most cases located at the transition from palaeouplifts to troughs and in zones of flexural bends. The maximum number of copper halos is observed in the vicinity of the Vladimirovskoye, Poltavskoye, Tretyakovskoye, and Kenenskoye copper occurrences. The form of the halos is elongated, irregular, and isometric. Their length ranges from 400 to 8000 m at a width of 200–1000 m. The dimensions of the halos, as a rule, exceed the dimensions of the mineralized rocks. The intensity of the copper halos varies within 0.007; 0.008; 0.01 and 0.015%. Maximum copper contents in the halos have been established at the Vladimirovskoye, Tretyakovskoye, and Poltavskoye occurrences and amount to 0.02%.

The deposits of the Kairaktinsk suite are less copper-bearing, which is reflected in the geochemical field as well. The greatest number of copper halos is confined to the contact zone of the grey-coloured deposits of the middle Kairaktinsk suite with the red beds of the lower Kairaktinsk subsuite. Copper halos are also noted in the zone of junction of the Tersakkansk and Kairaktinsk suite deposits. The orientation of the halos is generally consistent with the strike of the host rocks. Along strike, the halos extend over distances of 400–3000 m at a width of 200–500 m, rarely 1000 m. The copper content in them is 0.008; 0.01 and 0.02%.

A large number of copper halos are concentrated within the sedimentary troughs of Dukelbek, Kiymin, Mailykol, and Ashilin. The halo zones have a linearly elongated form and are confined to limestones of the Kiymin and Shoptykul formations. The sizes of the halos vary from 400 to 8000 m; the copper content in the halos is 0.008–0.01%, rarely reaching 0.02%. Within the halo zones, limestones with stains, crusts, and rarely disseminations of malachite and azurite are noted. A significant portion of the copper halos, localized in favorable geological settings at sites with visible copper mineralization, was evaluated using mining and drilling operations. The results were negative.

Copper halos associated with deposits of the Kiymin and Shoptykul formations are confined to pelitomorphic limestones with poor copper mineralization. The copper-bearing potential of these rocks, as shown above, is low.

Silver forms limited secondary halos. They are distributed in the southeastern part of sheet M-42-VII, where outcrops of dark-gray bituminous limestones of the Kiymin formation are mapped. The area of the halo zones is 500×200 m and 800×300 m. The silver content in them is 0.0003%. In the same deposits of the upper Kiymin subformation, three anomalous points with silver content of 0.000003; 0.000004 and 0.000005%, confined to pelitomorphic limestones, have been established. The presence of silver halos indicates contamination of the Kiymin formation limestones with silver. This is confirmed by drilling of borehole No. 90, which exposed silver mineralization at shallow depths in limestones in amounts of 15 and 51 g/t. These data indicate the possibility of discovering industrial accumulations of silver in the deposits of the Kiymin formation.
