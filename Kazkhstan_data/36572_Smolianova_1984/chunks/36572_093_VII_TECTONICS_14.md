**CHAPTER VII. TECTONICS**

In tectonic terms, the study area belongs primarily to the zone of Caledonian stabilization and the superimposed structures of the epi-Caledonian cover.

Geological-historical and structural analysis of available geological materials, as well as geophysical data, allow identification — within the western part of the depression and its margins, with varying degrees of detail and reliability — of zones of Karelian, Baikal, and Caledonian stabilization.

The first two zones, as well as the zone of epi-Baikal activation, have very limited distribution within the studied territory, in the margins of the Teniz Depression, the study of which was not part of our objective.

Within the zone of Caledonian stabilization, four genetic complexes of structures are distinguished:

1. Complex of the main stage of geosynclinal development.

2. Complex of the orogenic stage of development.

3. Complex of superimposed structures of the quasi-platform (sub-platform) cover.

4. Complex of platform formations.

The last is not considered in this section and is excluded from the tectonic map (Drawing 43).

The subject of detailed study was the epi-Caledonian sub-platform cover complex; its description will be given in greater detail.

The report presents a structural scheme of the Caledonian basement, compiled from geophysical data (Drawing 104), on which, with greater or lesser probability, fault dislocations and massifs of intrusive rocks are identified — most often without age characterization — and the character of one or another complex composing the tectonic block of the basement is shown only tentatively.

By *complex*, the authors understand an association of formations of sedimentary, volcanogenic-sedimentary, or volcanic rocks, formed under specific types of geological structures, a defined tectonic regime, and specific physico-geographical conditions, and consequently characterized by a distinctive assemblage of deposits and features of fold forms. The concept of *complex* is broader than the concepts of *stage* or *stage-unit*.

By **structural stage** the authors understand a group of geological formations separated from overlying and underlying formations by surfaces of regional unconformities and characterized by a definite type of folded structure and degree of metamorphism.

By **structural tier** is understood a part of the structural stage, representing a single series of geological formations that formed during a narrower interval of time, during one stage of the tectono-magmatic cycle, sometimes during the period between orogenic phases.

The tectonic scheme displays the formational composition of each complex, stage, and tier. In this context, by **formation** the authors understand "natural assemblages, associations of rock masses, individual members of which (rocks, rock members, suites) are paragenetically linked to one another both in lateral directions and in vertical stratigraphic succession." The identification of formations is carried out on the basis of lithological, tectonic, climatic, geochemical criteria and degree of metamorphism.

Thus, the **continental red-colored terrigenous formation** is characterized by an association of rocks: coarse- to very coarse-grained and medium-grained sandstones, interformational and intraformational conglomerates and gravellites, siltstones of brown, reddish-brown, reddish, and brownish-gray colors, with rare interbeds of gray-colored, lacustrine, alluvial-lacustrine, and diluvial-lacustrine origin. The formation arose under conditions of dissected relief on an uneven basement floor, with predominance of physical weathering over chemical, under oxidizing, rarely alternating oxidizing-reducing conditions, during a developing regression of the water basin, under arid climatic conditions.

The **continental predominantly gray-colored carbonate-argillaceous formation (lacustrine)** differs by a finer terrigenous component, widespread development of limestones, greater orderliness in the distribution of chemical elements. The formation developed under a more tranquil tectonic setting, under conditions of peneplained relief of denudation and accumulation zones, with significant chemical weathering, under conditions of a predominant reducing environment and a milder climate.

The **continental variegated carbonate-terrigenous-argillaceous formation**, in contrast to the one just described, arose under oxidizing conditions during further aridization of the climate.

The terrigenous marine formation of the transgression period is characterized by gray-colored terrigenous rocks with specific conditions of their transgressive occurrence.

The carbonate formation of the maximum transgression period is characterized by the widespread development of limestones with marine fauna.

The terrigenous marine formation of the regression period is characterized by both gray-colored and red-colored rock compositions with marine fauna, regressive occurrence of rocks, the presence of locally continental and locally lagoonal deposits, a gradual transition from marine to continental deposits, and the disappearance from the section of individual beds, layers, and rock lenses.

The red-colored molasse formation is characterized by coarse-clastic red-colored rocks of proluvial and deluvial-proluvial origin with rare interbeds of gray-colored rocks with flora and acidic effusives.

The red-colored liparite–andesite–terrigenous formation differs from the preceding one by the presence of volcanogenic rocks: amygdaloidal diabases, andesites, diabase and andesite porphyrites.

The siliceous–terrigenous formation is characterized by the presence in the rock composition of cherts and siliceous schists, which originated in the sea under conditions of intense hydrothermal exhalations.

The terrigenous flysch-like formation is characterized by a terrigenous rock composition with wide development of all varieties from conglomerates to argillites and rhythmicity of various orders. Its formation occurred, apparently, at the final stage of geosynclinal development in narrow intrageosynclinal troughs and uplifts under shallow-water conditions, in contrast to typical flysch formations.

The formation of alkaline gabbroids, alkaline and nepheline syenites arose under conditions of consolidation of the folded region and is represented by small hypabyssal intrusions. On the studied territory it is most closely associated with the formation of epileidsite porphyries and trachytes. Both formations are associated with a large, apparently, mantle fault.

The andesite–basalt formation arose at an early stage of geosynclinal development and is characterized by the widespread development of diabases and the gabbroids and gabbro-diabases accompanying them.

The alaskite formation developed during the waning tectonic movements in the late stage of the tectono-magmatic cycle. It is characterized by rounded, oval forms of intrusions. They are composed of leucocratic and alaskite granites.

The granodiorite formation developed in the final stage of the geosynclinal stage of development. It is characterized by intrusion forms concordant with the principal fold structures of this stage. The composition of the intrusions is quite varied — from granites to diorites. Their emplacement is most commonly polyphase.

The normal-series granitoid formation developed during the orogenic stage of development. It is characterized, like the alaskite formation, by isometric, rounded forms of intrusive massifs and by biotite and biotite-hornblende granites with negligible dark-mineral content.

The subalkaline granitoid formation is associated with the granodiorite formation and forms subsidiary intrusions during the development of granodiorite massifs, occasionally detached from the latter in space.

In the studied area, as already noted above, the distinctly Caledonian structures of the framework and basement of the depression, and the structures of the superimposed epi-Caledonian quasi-platform cover, are identified. Among the framework structures, the first-order major structures are the Zharkainsky anticlinorium, the Kokshetav glybe, the Kalmykkol synclinorium, the Kireysky uplift, and the Stepnyaksky synclinorium.

The studied area encompasses, in the north, a very limited portion of the **K o k s h e t a v g l y b e**, in the structure of which complexly dislocated rocks of the Zerendinsky and Kokshetav series, Ordovician sediments, and granitoids of the Zerendinsky and Balkashinsky intrusive massifs take part. In the geophysical fields, the deposits of the Proterozoic series are associated with positive gravimetric fields; the granitoids of the Zerendinsky and Balkashinsky intrusive massifs are mapped by positive magnetic anomalies and negative — gravimetric ones.

Further south lies the **K a l m y k k o l s y n c l i n o r i u m**, the formation of which was completed in the final stages of the geosynclinal stage of development.

The southwestern boundary of the Teniз depression is drawn here along a series of large faults of northwest strike, extended along the northern spurs of the Karagoin mountains; the most probable boundary of the depression serves the Kishaksky deep fault, which is clearly defined both in gravity-magnetic fields and from the results of seismic survey work. It cannot be excluded that Upper Paleozoic formations were distributed to the south of the Kishak fault, as far as the Kireysky thrust. Here, in the zone of the Kishak flexure, in individual synclinal structures, deposits of the Kirey formation are observed. South of the Kireysky thrust, Upper Paleozoic deposits apparently were not distributed, and the Sarysu–Teniaz uplift, beginning from the Upper Carboniferous, served as a zone of denudation, as evidenced by the composition of the pebbles of the reformational conglomerates of the Tersakkansk formation.

The northeastern part of the studied territory represents the western limb of the submeridional **S t e p n y a k s k o y e s y n c l i n o r i u m**, which in the area of the Novocherkassk uplift, and to the north of it beneath the deposits of the sub-platform cover of the Archalinsk and Dolboldinsk troughs, is conformably joined with the subhorizontal structures of the Kadmyksalsk synclinorium.

Fragments of structures of the orogenic stage of development are noted in the Ilanskaya Luka, on the Kireysky promontory, and on the Novocherkassk horst. Devonian superimposed basins arose through graben reworking of the Caledonian basement and its reactivation. Within the studied territory they are filled with coarse-clastic molasse, which in the direction toward the southeast is replaced by volcanics of basic and acidic composition. Practically all Devonian structures are associated with large fault zones.

We shall not dwell on the internal structure of either the geosynclinal complex or the orogenic one, since the study of the framework of the depression, as already noted above, was not included in the task of the follow-up study.

**The Achilin syncline** is located within map sheet M-42-XV, occupying nearly one quarter of the sheet. The syncline is composed in its core of a variegated carbonate-argillaceous-terrigenous formation of the Kiyamin and Shontykul suites, and on its limbs — of the carbonate-argillaceous formation of the Kavrakt suite. The fold axis is slightly elongated in the northeast direction. The limbs of the fold are gentle, 5–7°, with dip angles of strata flattening toward the central parts of the syncline to 2–3°. On the limbs, a network of small transverse and more extensive longitudinal faults is widely developed, forming radial and annular systems within the syncline. The northern and northwestern limbs of the syncline are complicated by a flexure, with strata dipping within it to the southeast at angles of 18–20°. The western limb of the syncline is complicated in the mouth of the Achilin River by a northeast-trending fault, and in the headwaters of the Doum stream by a northwest-trending fault. In the northeast direction along the Mialin–Itauysk–Kopkazgan flexure, the Achilin syncline passes into the Yadyshen–Kolutun syncline, located beyond the boundaries of the studied area. In the southwest direction along the same flexure, the Achilin syncline passes into the Kenensky (Mialinsk) syncline. The latter is separated from it by the Terakken flexure, with strata dipping within it up to 20–30°.

The key reflecting horizon T₁, which corresponds to the base of the terrigenous marine formation of the transgression period, lies in the center of the syncline at a depth of 4.3 km. The reflecting horizon T₃, corresponding to the base of the Terakken suite, lies in the center of the syncline at a depth of 2.7 km. The thickness of the epicaledonian quasi-platform cover in the syncline varies from 4.5 km to 2 km; at the same time, the thickness of the Terakken suite according to seismic and geological data varies from 800–900 m on the southern limb to 350–400 m on the northern, from 1,060 m on the western to 600 m on the eastern. On the map, a weak increase in the intensity of the magnetic field is observed over the sediments of the Achilin syncline.

In the gravimetric field, the southern part of the syncline coincides with a gradient zone of a negative field — the lateral effect of a granodiorite massif located to the south.

**Kiiminsk group of synclines**, previously identified as the single Kiiminsk syncline, is situated along the northwestern limb of the Teniz depression.
