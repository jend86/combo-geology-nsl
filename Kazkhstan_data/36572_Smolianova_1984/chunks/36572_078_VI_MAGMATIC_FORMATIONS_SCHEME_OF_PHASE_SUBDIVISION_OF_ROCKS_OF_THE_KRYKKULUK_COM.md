**Geological Structure of the Intrusions of the Krikkuduk Complex**

Within the work area, diorites and granitoids of the Krikkuduk complex participate in the structure of a whole series of massifs. They are entirely composed of the Sarsymbai, Borliakol, Naumov, Efremov, Ergol, and a number of small, unnamed massifs in the area of the Ishim Arc (Markainarachi anticlinorium), Kirei uplift, Stepnyak synclinorium; Marinov uplift; the western part of the Bulandy-Akkol and the eastern margin of the Derzhavin intrusions. Bodies composed of rocks of the Krikkuduk complex are known within the northern part of the polyphase Karatas pluton.

Furthermore, as noted above, the presence of rocks analogous to those of the Krikkuduk complex is established from geophysical data and drilling results beneath the cover of Upper Paleozoic deposits of the Teniz depression.

Let us briefly review the characteristics of the geological structure of the intrusive bodies of the Krikkuduk complex.

**K a r a t a s   m a s s i f** is located within the Kirei uplift. Only its northern part, with an area of approximately 50.5 km², falls within the studied territory.

The granitoids of the Krikkuduk complex make up the western part of the Karatas massif, intrude the argillaceous-terrigenous deposits of the Ulutau series, and are in turn intruded by alaskite-type granites of the Araldul complex.

The described rocks are overlain by deposits of the Dairin suite and Cenozoic sediments. Contacts with other geological formations of the area are of tectonic character. Data on the depth extent of the massif are practically absent; given the sinuous character of the contacts, a gentle dip of the intrusion contacts may be inferred. The erosional incision into the pre-

of the massif are, apparently, small, as evidenced by numerous roof pendants. According to data from M.S. Grankin, the thickness of the intrusive rocks in the indicated block amounts to approximately 2.1 km (Grankin, 1970).

In the structure of the described part of the Karatas massif, quartz diorites and granodiorites of the first phase of emplacement participate, as well as biotite and biotite-amphibole granites of the second phase, with the bodies of diorites and granodiorites of the first phase gravitating toward the periphery of the pluton, as if framing its central part, which is composed of granites of the second phase. Due to poor exposure of the rocks, the mutual relationships between the granitoids of the first and second phases have nowhere been observed. Subdivision into phases is carried out by analogy with the areas adjacent to the described one from the south and southeast. Late-stage formations, represented by fine-grained granites and aplites, are located predominantly among the granites of the second phase, forming small bodies confined, apparently, to primary tectonic fractures.

The exposure and decipherability of rocks of the Krinkuduk complex within the described territory are poor. The fields of their development are frequently expressed in relief as broad valley-like depressions.

**B u l a n d i – A k k o l s k y   m a s s i f** is located within the Stepnyak synclinorium. The western part of it falls within the work area, forming two separated groups of outcrops: a northern one with an area of exposed sections of approximately 146 km² (M-42-XXXV, east of the village of Voznessenka) and a southern one, with an area of exposed sections of no more than 21 km² (M-42-V, east of the village of Yefeyevka).

The form of the outcrops in plan is irregular. The contact of the intrusion with the host rocks is sinuous. Numerous roof pendants, composed of hornfelsed rocks, as it were, break up the body of the pluton into a series of smaller massifs.

Within the northern group of outcrops, these smaller massifs are connected to one another and to the main intrusion, located to the west, already beyond the limits of the work area, by connecting bridges. In the southern group of outcrops, a small Yefeyevsky pluton is separated from the Bulandi–Akkolsky massif by a roof pendant.

The granitoids of the Bulandi–Akkolsky massif intrude, within the work area territory, the deposits of the Stepnyak series and the Nikolsko–

of the Burdukan series, overlain only by Cenozoic deposits.

Numerous roof pendants and xenoliths of the host rocks, characteristic of the described intrusive formations, testify to the insignificant erosional incision within the massif, while the irregular boundaries and fairly thick exocontact alteration zones (up to 1 km) indicate the complex relief of its buried roof and the relatively gentle dip angles of the contact in the near-surface portions of the massif. At depth, according to geophysical data, the contacts become steep.

The exposure of rocks is fairly good. The areas of distribution of the granitoids of the Krankuduk complex are characterized by hilly relief with bedrock outcrops on the crests of ridges and hills, and with alluvial pebble deposits on the lower ground.

On the described area, the Bulandy–Akkol massif, like the majority of the smaller satellite bodies, is composed of rocks of the first phase of emplacement: diorites, quartz diorites, granodiorites, and biotite-hornblende granites. All authors who have studied the pluton note the gradational transitions between rock varieties and the increase in their mafic index in the direction from the center of the massif toward the periphery.

Within the northern group of outcrops, small vein-like bodies composed of aplite-like granites and granite-porphyries of the additional intrusion phase are fairly widely distributed. The bodies reach 1.4 km in size. They occur among rocks of the first phase, with which they have sufficiently sharp contacts (Yakimenko, 1963).

On adjacent areas to the west, analogous granites intrude the granitoids of the second phase of the Krankuduk complex of the Bulandy–Akkol massif (Kiryakov, 1970; Zhukov et al., 1972).

Dike rocks of the first stage and dikes of the second stage are most widely distributed in the southern group of outcrops. They are associated both directly with the intrusive formations and with the host rocks.

Dikes of the first stage form steeply dipping bodies with thicknesses ranging from 3–5 to 12–15 m.

They are represented by aplites, fine-grained granites, granite-porphyries, and pegmatites. They intrude the granites of the additional intrusion phase and are in turn intruded by dikes of the second stage.

Dikes of the second stage are similarly steeply dipping and vertical bodies with thicknesses from 1–2 to 12–15 m. They are represented by granite porphyries, granodiorite porphyries, diorite porphyries, diabases, and spessartites (Kiryakov, 1970; Dukov et al., 1972).

The second large intrusion in the area of the Stepnyak synclinorium, located within the working territory, is the **N a u m o v s k y p l u t o n** (M-42-XXXV, area of vil. Naumovka–Filippovka). It has a plan shape close to oval. Dimensions are 17×7 km, the elongation of the long axis is northwest. Judging by the rectilinear boundaries, one can assume a steep dip of its contacts, which is also confirmed by geophysical data. The Naumovsky intrusion cuts through Middle Ordovician deposits and is overlain in its northwestern part by Upper Paleozoic sediments of the Tenizkaya depression. It is composed of granites and granodiorites of the first phase of emplacement. Exposure of the massif is good in the northwestern half and poor in the southeastern and eastern parts.

A few words must be said about the **K o l u t o n s k o y m a s s i f**. It is located on the area of sheet M-42-V, southwest of vil. Mal. Alexandrovka. Exposure of the massif is extremely weak; the greater part of its outcrop area is concealed beneath overburden. The contacts with the host strata are unclear and it is difficult to judge the form of the massif. According to data of I.F. Kiryakov, who described this pluton, it is composed of biotite and biotite–hornblende granites of the second phase. Assignment of these granites to the second phase is made on the basis of comparison with the central part of the Bulandy–Aktolsky pluton, where analogous granites cut the granitoids of the first phase and are in turn cut by granites of the phase of additional intrusions (Kiryakov, 1970).

In the group of massifs of the Ishim Arc area (Marinagatachinsky anticlinorium), rocks of the Krasnokuduksky complex compose the **B o r l i n k o l s k y m a s s i f**, the extreme eastern termination of the **D e r z h a v i n s k y m a s s i f**, and a series of small unnamed massifs, which are apparently apophyses separated from the principal plutons by screens of the host rocks. All these massifs are located on the area of sheet M-42-VII.

The **B o r l i n k o l s k y m a s s i f** is situated to the southwest of Lake Borlinköl. The area of the exposed part of the intrusion is approximately 18 km². The degree of exposure is moderate; better exposed [text continues]

the southwestern part of the pluton. In relief, the granitoids form low ridges and swells with bedrock exposures at the summits. Drainage is poor. From the northwest, the Borlikol intrusion cuts through the deposits of the Kubasadir Formation of assumed Upper Ordovician age, and from the southeast — the deposits of the Akkairakt Formation. The southwestern contact of the massif is overlain by Lower Carboniferous deposits, and the northeastern contact — by Cenozoic formations.

In the exocontact zone, reaching 1.6 km in width, the enclosing rocks are strongly hornfelsed. The contacts of the massif are sinuous, which, together with the varying width of the exocontact zone along strike, testifies to the complex morphology of the roof of the massif, to variability of the dip angle of the contact, and to a shallow erosional cross-section. From analysis of geophysical fields, it can be assumed that the northwestern contact of the intrusion is the more gently dipping one.

The Borlikol intrusion is composed of quartz diorites and granodiorites of the first phase of emplacement.

**D E R Z H A V I N S K Y   M A S S I F** falls within the territory of operations with its eastern termination. It forms a belt of outcrops only 1.6 km wide and approximately 6 km long along the western boundary of the area (M-42-VII). The pluton is composed of biotite and biotite-hornblende granites of the second phase of emplacement. It should be noted that within the area the mutual relationships of rocks of the first and second phases are unknown, so that their separation is quite provisional and is made on the basis of comparison with analogous formations of the Krimkuduk complex of other districts of Central Kazakhstan.

The Borlikol massif is practically barren both with respect to bodies composed of rocks of the additional intrusion phases, and with respect to vein formations. The same can be said of that part of the Derzhavinsk massif which lies within the territory of operations. Plagiogranites of the additional intrusion phases, vein rocks of the first stage, and dykes of the second stage were studied and mapped in the central parts of the Derzhavinsk pluton beyond the limits of the studied area (Taranin, 1967).

**S A R S I M B A Y S K Y   M A S S I F** is located in the southern part of the territory of sheet M-42-VII, in the headwaters of the Tasty-Taldy stream. Since all its contacts with the enclosing and overlying stratified formations are concealed beneath a cover of Cenozoic deposits, it is difficult to judge the form of the massif.

The Sarybai pluton is composed of granites and granodiorites, tentatively correlated with the second phase of the Krykkundy complex (by analogy with the Derzhavin massif). Also known here are numerous dikes of dioritic porphyrites and spessartites belonging, apparently, to the second-stage dikes.

Tentatively correlated with the formation of the Krykkundy complex are rocks of acidic and intermediate composition outcropping at the surface within the northern limb of the Marinovsk uplift (M-42-III; M-42-IV). Here, to the north and northeast of station Adyr, more than 30 small (maximum area 3.5 km²), stock-like and dike-like bodies have been mapped. Some were mapped based on exploratory drilling data. The intrusions cut through the deposits of the Kalmanköl Series of the Middle–Upper Ordovician and are overlain mainly by Cenozoic formations. Exposure of the rocks is moderate; their bedrock outcrops are fairly often confined to the crests of low ridges. The stock-like bodies are composed of granodiorites and granite-porphyries; among the dikes, in addition to granite-porphyries and granodiorite-porphyries, dioritic porphyrites and spessartites are also present (Kvesenko et al., 1978).

Assimilation processes are widely developed in the rocks, and as previously noted by researchers, "the scatter of compositions corresponds to the normal course of assimilation of acidic-composition intrusions and fully corresponds to the characteristic features of the Krykkundy intrusive complex" (Kvesenko et al., 1978).
