CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

1. The geological assignment is to be considered fulfilled.

2. The geological maps are recommended to be recognized as conforming to scale 1:200,000.

3. The authors' recommendations regarding the direction of further work in the western part of the Teniz Depression should be taken into account when determining the direction of exploration work on specific areas.

4. To concur with the decision of the Scientific and Technical Council (NTS) of the PGO "Tsentrkazgeologiya" that the question of the advisability of identifying new suites and renaming known ones be considered additionally and resolved specifically before the commencement of work on compiling summary maps for the entire Teniz Depression.

5. Attention is drawn to the fact that the NTS of the PGO "Tsentrkazgeologiya" accepted two reports on work at scale 1:200,000 in the eastern and western parts of the Teniz Depression (Petrylak, 1983; Smolyaninova, 1984) with inconsistent stratigraphy along the work boundaries. At the same time, both works were carried out within one expedition and during one time period.

6. To propose that the authors supplement the report with a catalogue of boreholes drilled by the West-Teniz Party and with conclusions of specialists on fauna and flora.

In conclusion, it should be stated that the authors have carried out an enormous body of work in analyzing the existing and acquired GDP-200 materials.

The report of the West-Teniz PSP on the whole deserves the highest evaluation; however, taking into account the underestimation of the results of scale 1:50,000 work on questions of stratigraphy, the inconsistency of stratigraphy between the western and eastern parts of the Teniz Depression, as well as the deficiencies of the authors in calculating forecast resources for copper, the report is recommended to the NRS for acceptance with the rating of **good**.

**HEARD:** Report by the Head of the West-Teniz PSP, Smolyaninova E.M., on the results of the group re-study at scale 1:200,000 in the western part of the Teniz depression.

In accordance with the geological mandate, the West-Teniz prospecting-survey party in 1978–1984 carried out group geological re-study at scale 1:200,000 over the territory covering the northern and western parts of the Teniz depression. The work formed part of the integrated programme for assessment of the copper-bearing potential of Upper Palaeozoic se-[*illegible — text continues on next page*]

**21**

deposits of the region. In this regard, the focus of geological follow-up study was determined, concentrated, above all, on the study of Carboniferous–Permian deposits.

The entire territory of geological follow-up study is provided with standard-grade maps of gravitational and magnetic fields at scales of 1:50,000 – 1:200,000. In addition, a significant volume of profile gravimetric and magneto-exploration observations was carried out by the authoring team during field operations for the resolution of specific geological problems.

Having heard the report of Smolyaninova V.M., the NTS NOTES:

1. The authors have conducted significant work on the study of Carboniferous–Permian deposits of the Teniз Depression. Formation boundaries have been refined, lithological characteristics of the formations have been identified and described in detail, and a rich paleontological material has been collected, allowing well-founded conclusions to be drawn regarding the age of the identified stratigraphic subdivisions.

2. The stratigraphic subdivisions are described at a high level, unprecedented for reports of this type. In terms of the abundance, detail, and diversity of the presented material, the report can be regarded both as a generalization and as a reference work. The lithology of the deposits is described with useful detail and purposefulness.

3. Analyzing the causes of formation of tectonic structures of the Teniz Depression, the authors arrive at a well-founded conclusion regarding the dominant role of block movements of the basement, expressed in block folds of the sub-platform cover. This relationship and its validity are demonstrated on a series of maps and diagrams.

Internal uplifts of various types are identified — inherited and late-stage.

4. The main attention of the authors during the follow-up study of the region is devoted to the assessment of the oil-bearing potential of the sub-platform cover of the Teniz Depression. Oil-bearing sandstones are genetically associated by the authors with deltaic, alluvial, and alluvial–lacustrine facies, which are confined to seven stratigraphic levels corresponding to the identified productive horizons.

An assessment of the comparative prospectivity of these horizons is provided; in particular, the low prospectivity of horizons confined to the Shoptykul, Kimian, and Lower Cretaceous stratigraphic levels is noted, and higher prospectivity of horizons confined to [continues]

to the upper Devonian (Deirian), lower Carboniferous (Spassky), upper Carboniferous (Tersakkanian), and middle Carboniferous (Kireysky) stratigraphic levels.

This assessment can be agreed with.

5. The main exploration criteria and prospective-prognostic assessment have been thoroughly analyzed.

6. The geological maps are distinguished by high detail and reliability. In terms of detail, they significantly surpass previously published maps at a scale of 1:200,000.

7. The reliability of many conclusions has increased as a result of using geophysical data. In the overwhelming majority of cases, the geological interpretation of geophysical anomalies has been carried out correctly and raises no doubts.

8. The results of the interpretation of geophysical fields, transferred to cartographic materials, have been carefully correlated with the available geological data.

9. The error in gravity survey and magnetic survey observations is significantly lower than that stipulated by the project, which attests to the high quality of field observations.

Alongside the positive aspects of the reporting materials, NIS notes the following shortcomings:

1. The inadvisability of replacing the Vladimirovsky suite with the Tersakkanian. Apparently, it is necessary to raise the question of replacing the stratotype.

2. Insufficient substantiation of the identification of new suites: the Krasnoselskaya and the Tenizskaya.

3. Insufficient substantiation [illegible] of the exclusion from the stratigraphic scheme of the Teniz depression of the Archedinskaya suite and its treatment as the lower Kairraktinian sub-suite.

4. The unjustified replacement of old names of fault dislocations in the basement of the depression with new ones.

5. The lack of consistency (both in nomenclature and in substance) of the key marker horizons for the various parts of the Teniz depression (southern, northern, and western), as well as of horizons traced areally and identified from wells.

# REVIEW

of the report of the West-Teniz prospecting-survey party of the Central Geological-Prospecting Expedition of the Central Kazakhstan Industrial Association on the work of 1978–1984.
"Geological re-study at scale 1:200,000 over the area of sheets B-42-XXXII (126–B,G; 137–B,G; 138); M-42-XXXIV–(128–G; 139; 140); B-42-XXXV–(129–B,G; 141); M-42-I–(3–B,G;4; 15; 16); M-42-IV(7–A,B; 8–A,B); M-42-8; M-42-V(9); M-42-VI–(26,38); M-42-VII; M-42-IX–(29,41); M-42-XIV(31,52,64–A,B); M-42-XV". Authors: Smolyaninova L.M., Naumenko V.B., Glukhov A.M., Deriga L.P., Filatova G.V. et al.

---

According to the geological assignment, the West-Teniz prospecting-survey party in 1978–1984 carried out group geological re-study at scale 1:200,000 over a territory encompassing the northern and western parts of the Teniz depression. This work formed part of a comprehensive programme for evaluating the copper-bearing potential of Upper Palaeozoic sequences of this region. In connection with this, the focus of the geological re-study was determined, concentrated above all on the study of Carboniferous-Permian deposits and their copper-bearing potential. Accordingly, both in the field work and in the report, the main attention was devoted to the characterisation of the corresponding geological complexes. This determined both the content of the report text and the significance of its sections.

We now proceed to a review of the report.

The chapters "Introduction" and "Physico-Geographical Outline" are well written and raise no comments. The section on geological-geophysical knowledge makes a very good impression. It contains a clear critical analysis of existing conceptions regarding the stratigraphy of the Carboniferous-Permian, enabling the reader to immediately appreciate the essence of the problems under consideration.

One of the principal chapters of the report is "Stratigraphy". The description of Proterozoic formations of the Lower and Middle Palaeozoic up to the Famennian is given, in the main, from third-party materials — their detailed study was not part of the authors' task. Nevertheless, in the course of the work a number of new data was obtained that refines the structure of the ancient sequences, and a critical assessment of existing conceptions was carried out. We consider that the doubt expressed by the authors regarding the attribution to the Late Proterozoic of the Bratolyubovskaya, Germinskaya, Nikolaevsk-Burdunsk series and their younger age is fully justified. correlations of the Ulutau series with the Bratolyubovskaya and Urtyn-Dzhalskoye [series].

Let us proceed directly to the Lower Carboniferous. The description of the Lower Carboniferous deposits, their stratigraphic subdivision, and paleontological characteristics are presented fully and with a high degree of informativeness. There are a number of remarks, however.

**Rusakovsky Horizon.** The description of the borehole section from top to bottom cannot be considered successful; the same applies to the table of brachiopod distribution in the deposits of the Yagovkinsky and Dalnensky horizons and the Kypshakoye Formation identified by the authors. For some reason, the Dalnensky Horizon is assigned to the Middle Substage of the Visean Stage. The authors, concluding the description of the Dalnensky Horizon of the Tenizskaya Depression, emphasize the significant lithological difference between the sections of this structure and the stratotype along the Niya River, and propose that, during large-scale mapping, the allocation of this stratigraphic subdivision as a "horizon" be abandoned in favor of replacing it with a formation. It is possible that a local formation should be identified, but this does not preclude simultaneous assignment to the "Dalnensky Horizon," which is a biochronostratigraphic subdivision.

Above the Dalnensky Horizon, the authors identify the Kypshakoye Formation, replacing the Beleutinsky Horizon here. The need to identify an independent formation is caused, in the authors' opinion, by differences in lithological composition and a higher upper boundary of the unit. Both of these arguments are, in our view, presented in insufficiently convincing form. Variation in the composition of deposits within the volume of a formation is inevitable, and in any case, evidence should be constructed on the basis of comparison of sections along Beleutay and Kypshakou. More complex is the question of the paleontological substantiation of the age range. First, no comparison and analysis of organic remains from the Beleutinsky Horizon and the Kypshakoye Formation has been carried out. Second, as far as I am aware, in the compilation it would be necessary to operate with different groups of paleontological remains, which in itself will already lead to imprecision. On the other hand, regrettably, the authors themselves, based on paleontological remains, draw conclusions about age; in any case, conclusions of specialist paleontologists are not provided. In connection with this, a whole series of questions arises when reading the text.

In general, the deposits of the Kypsak Formation have been assigned by the authors to the Serpukhovian – lower Bashkirian stages. At the same time, in justifying the age of the deposits, it is stated that the complex of brachiopods and pelecypods from the upper part of the formation, identified by G.G. Aksenova and M.Ya. Tokareva, "allows dating the age of the enclosing rocks as middle–upper Carboniferous."

Completely incomprehensible to us is the authors' conclusion drawn from the ostracod determinations: "The appearance of late Carboniferous species in the ostracod complex allows assigning this part of the section (apparently the upper B.K.) to the lower part of the Bashkirian stage." What, then, are the final conclusions of the paleontologists themselves? Unfortunately, the conclusion of L.A. Goganova regarding plant remains is also absent. Of great interest is the appearance of middle Carboniferous argillaceous-lepidophyte [forms] already in the lower levels of the section and the preservation, in turn, of early Carboniferous lycopsids up to the very top of the formation. It should be noted that the floristic complex of the Kypsak Formation (and, apparently, of the Beleutian horizon) is close to the fossil flora of the Kalmenmels and Burultas formations of Northern Prialkhashye, with the exception of the high stratigraphic position of lepidophyte remains. This gives us the opportunity to correlate the formations of these structurally and formationally very different zones and to refine the age of the deposits of Northern Prialkhashye.

The Kirensk Formation, which follows the Kypsak Formation, is described in detail; questions related to the determination of its lower and upper boundaries are examined; and detailed descriptions of sections are provided. With regard to the characterization of the sections, the following remarks apply: the description proceeds from bottom to top by beds, but within an individual bed — from top to bottom; the continuity of the deposits is lost; moreover, on what grounds, and is there any need, to distinguish hundreds of beds of several meters thickness that are very similar to one another? It is far more effective to distinguish larger groups of deposits of similar facies type; the details are also well read from the accompanying diagrams–columns. In the course of their work, the authors collected rich paleontological material, primarily fossil flora. Unfortunately, as in the case of the Kypsak Formation, the conclusion of L.A. Goganova has not been provided. Here again we encounter an unexplained combination of early Carboniferous lepidophytes with articulate lepidostrobili that are already not only middle Carboniferous but also late Carboniferous,

or even Permian — parakolomiites.

The red-colored and variegated sediments overlying the Kireisky Formation have long been established in the literature under the name of the Vladimirovskoye suite, or the Vladimirovskoye layer of the Arzelinskoye type. The authors came to the conclusion about the necessity of renaming this stratigraphic subdivision to the Teresekanskaya suite. They cited the reasons that prompted them to take this step — these are the incompleteness of the section in the stratotype, the discrepancy in the volumes of the Vladimirovskoye suite in the south and north of the Teniz depression, and a number of other less significant circumstances. We believe that the authors are essentially correct in their remarks, and that on the basis of studying an enormous amount of material, they have sufficiently definitively established the extent of this stratigraphic unit across the entire area of the Teniz depression. At the same time, we consider the introduction of a new name (more precisely, the restoration of a very old one) to be unjustified. The name "Vladimirovskoye" suite has become firmly established in the literature and in the understanding of geologists, and there is no point in changing it on the grounds that the old stratotype lacks section exposures.

Having concentrated their efforts on substantiating the necessity of distinguishing the Teresekanskaya suite, the authors have, in essence, glossed over the question of the Arzelinskoye suite, which some researchers include within the Vladimirovskoye suite and others separate as an independent suite. The age of the Teresekanskaya suite according to the authors is late Carboniferous in the full range. It should be noted that floristic remains in many cases belong to Permian forms. The presence of parakolomiites already in the lower part of the suite and the abundance of such species as

alongside

attests to a Permian age, at least for the greater part of the suite's sediments. The same conclusion is reached from spore-pollen analysis data. The parakolomiite-kolomiite association of the Teniz depression is similar to that in the Koidarskaya suite C₅–P₁ of the Northern Priaralye. The Koirentinskoye suite is described with a thoroughness and completeness characteristic of the report. Based on abundant paleontological and palynological data, the age of the suite is determined as early Permian. It seems to us that the age of the suite should be restricted to the Artinskian and Kungurian stages, including the Sakmarian still within the Vladimirovskoye suite (if the Arzelinskoye suite is considered part of its composition). The Koirentinskoye suite in age approximately corres-

*Page 29*

corresponds to the Kyzylkinian–Chubareigarian suite of the volcanic belt of Northern Prialkhashye. It appears excessive to distinguish a new Tenizian suite.

The authors have carried out extensive work on the study of the Kiliminian and Shoptykol suites of the Upper Permian. The boundary criteria of the suites have been refined and defined, their composition has been described in detail, and rich paleontological material has been collected.

Deposits of the Cenozoic group are described in detail on the basis of materials from predecessors and the authors, and provide a sufficiently clear picture of their composition and character of distribution.

Turning to the general characterization of the stratigraphic section of the report, and above all its Carboniferous–Permian portion — the principal object of geological re-examination — I must note the very high level of description, perhaps unprecedented for reports of this type. In terms of volume, detail, and diversity of the material presented, the report can be regarded both as a monograph and as a reference work. Even the somewhat "extended" sequential descriptions of sections provide important details and characterize the suites. I see nothing objectionable, and even the contrary — I consider valuable the tables of occurrence frequencies of individual paleontological forms, which are used only in specialized paleontological monographs. The lithology of the deposits is described with useful thoroughness and purposefulness. We consider it a positive feature that the authors do not yield to demands for restricting the volume of description, which invariably leads to a fragmentary and unsubstantiated, formal presentation of materials with loss of collected and generalized information.
