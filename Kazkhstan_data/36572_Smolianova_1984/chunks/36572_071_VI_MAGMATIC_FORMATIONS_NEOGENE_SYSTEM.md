**NEOGENE SYSTEM**

**Miocene–Pliocene (undivided)**
**TENIZ FORMATION ( N₁₋₂tn )**

On the undulating eroded surface of the Upper Oligocene clays, as well as on older formations, lies a sequence of distinctive continental deposits characterized by elevated gypsum content and persistent argillaceous composition. Outcrops of this sequence at the day surface are observed within the studied territory on the left bank of the Tersaikhan River on the area of sheet M-42-XIV; also to the east of Lake Msindykol, deposits of the Teniz Formation are exposed in the deepest erosional incisions of rivers and streams. Significant surface outcrops of Teniz Formation deposits are located in the southern part of sheets M-42-VII, VIII, in the headwaters of the Taldy-Bairat and Usharasu streams, on the area of sheet M-42-II, south of the settlements of Eltyai, Nikolaevka, and also southwest of the town of Atbasar in the valleys of lakes Uzankol, Kamyshnoe, and Berlykol. Throughout the remaining territory, deposits of the Teniz Formation are overlain by Quaternary deposits and are penetrated by numerous boreholes.

Based on borehole sections, the formation is subdivided into either two or three sequences, or not subdivided at all. It should be noted that in boreholes the subdivision into sequences is very clear, whereas in surface outcrops the mapping of each individual sequence is not feasible. In boreholes, the Teniz Formation is most commonly subdivided into two sequences: a lower greenish-grey to variegated, greenish-coloured sequence, identified by many researchers as the Aral Formation or the lower portion of the Turgai, and an upper red-coloured to red-variegated sequence, which individual researchers assign either to the Pavlodar Formation or to the upper Turgai. The characteristic variation of the Teniz Formation sections even over short distances is shown in the figure.

Thus, the full thickness of the Teniз Suite deposits within the studied area is 35–40 m. The structure is most often two-membered: the lower part of the sequence is green-colored, and the upper part is substantially red-colored. At the base, either coarse-grained sands or reworked siliceous-ferruginous nodule layers are frequently observed, with very rare admixtures of pebbles of polymineral composition, and entirely rare gradual transitions to upper Oligocene clays.

The genesis of the sequence is lacustrine, alluvial-lacustrine, and less commonly diluvial. The monotonous appearance of greenish-gray and brown clays is associated with the specific characteristics of the processes of weathering, redeposition, and diagenesis of sediments on the surface of a low-lying, waterlogged land under conditions of a warm semi-arid climate. Even the subaerial deposits of that time acquired the external appearance of lacustrine deposits. In the north of the Teniз depression, the green and greenish-gray fatty plastic clays differ from those in the south in that they are enriched with sandy material and gravel, and frequently, by geomorphological position, occur in the form of lenses abutting against slopes. Their formation is possibly diluvial or diluvial-proluvial.

The lithological composition of the suite: clays, sands, gravel, pebble beds, marls.

lithic with admixture of hydromica, gypsum, limonite, hydrohematite; hydromicaceous-kaolinitic with admixture of quartz, limonite, beidellite–montmorillonitic.

**Sands** are polymict, often argillaceous, from fine-grained to gravelitic. Their color is greenish-gray, yellowish-gray. Composition is predominantly quartzose.

**Gravels** form thin interbeds (0.25–0.5 m), predominantly in the lower part of the sequence. The gravel is well-worn, rounded, sub-rounded, 0.5 cm to 8–10 cm in cross-section. Gravel composition: quartz, flint. Occasionally the gravel is weakly cemented by argillaceous sand, fine- and coarse-grained.

The age of the described deposits is determined by their stratigraphic position: they overlie with erosional unconformity the Upper Oligocene deposits (Fig. 69) and are overlain by brown, brownish-brown sandy clays, the age of which is determined as Upper Pliocene. Cross-sections of the suite within the studied area are confidently correlated with cross-sections of these deposits along the Nura and Kulan-Utpes rivers, where the Teniz suite was first identified.

Within the studied territory, a spore-pollen complex was identified from the suite deposits in boreholes 173 and 190 (Figs. 70, 71, Table 38), in which, according to the conclusion of R.A. Tereshchenko, pollen of herbaceous-shrub plants constitutes 75–93%, with chenopodiaceae dominating at 16–59%, wormwood 16–40%. Pollen of woody species and spores are noted in single occurrences or first percentages. Pollen of woody species belongs to birch, oak, elm, and hornbeam. Spores belong to green and liverwort mosses, and sphagnum.

The low saturation of samples with pollen and its composition also indicate semi-arid climatic conditions at the time of formation of the Teniz suite clays.

The composition of the spore-pollen complex indicates, according to the conclusion of R.A. Tereshchenko, a Middle–~~Upper~~ Pliocene age of the host rocks of this complex.

Skeletal remains of invertebrates within the studied territory, 15 km from the mouth of the Tersakkana River, in deposits attributed to the Teniz suite, were found by A.S. Kumpan in 1957. Repeated collection was not possible, as there are no outcrops of the Teniz suite here. It was precisely 15 km from the mouth of the Tersakkana that numerous skeletal remains were later collected from deposits of the Naiman suite.

Bone remains from the green gypsiferous clays of the Teniz Formation were collected with the participation of the authors by D.V. Dmitrovsky in 1974 along the Kulan-Utpes River. The section of the Teniz Formation in the studied area correlates with the section along the Kulan-Utpes River. Thus, the clays of borehole 190 in the interval 27–31 m, as well as the clays of borehole 173 in the interval 14–24 m, correspond to the clays from which bone remains were collected on the Kulan-Utpes River. Among the remains, A.T. Abrakhmanova identified bones of the fore and hind limbs of a giraffe and a rhinoceros of the genus *Chilotherium*. From the same collections, N.A. Aubekerova identified bones and teeth belonging to *Hipparion* cf. *elegans*. According to the conclusions of both researchers, the deposits containing these bone remains were formed during the Lower Pliocene.

Based on all of the above, the age of the Teniz Formation is considered to be Miocene–Pliocene, most likely Miocene–Lower Pliocene.

**Middle Pliocene (N₂²?)**

Deposits attributed to the Middle Pliocene form flat, leveled terrace-like surfaces not associated with the modern drainage network, lying hypsometrically above the alluvial terraces composed of Quaternary sediments. They crop out at the surface in the southern half of the area of sheets M–42–XIV, XV. Outcrops of analogous deposits were observed by the authors at the confluence of the Kon and Kulan-Utpes rivers.

Within the studied territory, sections of Middle Pliocene deposits were observed in the cliffs of the right bank of the Kirei River, at observation point 695. These are light-colored rocks in coarse-lenticular interbedding. The thickness of lenses and interlayers reaches 0.3 m with lengths up to 10 m. They alternate with white, weakly cemented sandstones, gravels, and conglomerates. The total thickness of the sequence does not exceed 7–8 m.

It overlies the green gypsiferous clays of the Teniz Formation, which is clearly visible in the cliffs of the Kirei River. Closer to Lake Kirei, this same sequence lies on an eroded surface of Oligocene deposits. The overlying of gravels on the clays of the Teniz Formation was also observed in boreholes 353, 354 in the area of sheet M–42–XIV. The thickness of the sequence here does not exceed 3–3.5 m.

The **sandstones** are semi-cemented with argillaceous cement. Their composition is polymictic, but with a predominance in the clastic mate-

-229-

material of semi-rounded quartz grains. The admixture of gravel and pebbles, and in some places crushed fragments of flint and quartz, is considerable. The strength of the cement is locally reduced and the sandstones grade into fine- to medium-grained quartzose and polymictic coarse-grained sands.

**CONGLOMERATES** consist of well-rounded and semi-rounded pebbles and boulders ranging from 1.5–2 to 7–8 cm in cross-section. The composition of the pebbles and boulders is polymictic: quartz, siliceous rocks, Upper Oligocene quartz-like sandstones, limestones of the Lower Carboniferous, black flints, jaspers, and acidic effusives. The pebbles are sometimes cemented by a clayey material, but more often by sandy material. The cement contains an admixture of carbonate and clay.

The genesis of the sequence is apparently alluvial, as evidenced not only by the narrow belt-like zone of distribution of the rocks, but also by their coarse-grained composition: sands, gravel, and pebble deposits. Lens-shaped textures with short, powerful lenses indicate powerful multi-channel streams.

The age of the sequence is established by stratigraphic position: it overlies the deposits of the Teniз Suite; into it are intercalated, within the studied area, the deposits of the second and first floodplain terraces. To the east of the studied area, at the confluence of the Kon and Kulan-Utmes rivers, similar pebbly formations are overlain by Upper Pliocene and Lower Quaternary watershed loams. No organic remains have been found in the sequence within the studied area. The age of the sequence is provisionally accepted as **Middle Pliocene**.
