A detailed description of the methodology for delineating tectonic dislocations from geophysical data is given in the chapter "Physical properties of rocks of the study area and methodology of interpretation of geophysical materials."

It remains to be emphasized that on gravity maps, dislocations were traced primarily along gradient zones and displacement of anomaly axes. On magnetic maps, dislocations were traced along chains of negative anomalies, changes in field character, gradients, and changes in the direction of anomaly axes. On seismic cross-sections, dislocations are identified by loss of correlation zones and changes in depths to reflecting boundaries.

The above analysis of the structure of the western part of the Teniz Depression shows that it is characterized by the following principal features:

1. The discontinuous character of the folds, among which anticlinal box-shaped folds, domes, broad gentle troughs, brachiform anticlines and troughs, and gentle dome-shaped uplifts predominate;

2. The absence of horizontal orientational consistency in the movement of masses, where the axial planes of the folds have varying orientations and their vergence is frequently observed;

3. The absence of linearity, expressed in the significant variability of strike of the strata from one fold to another;

4. The wide development of flexures;

5. The syndepositional nature of the structures.

All these features characterize the fold structure of the western part of the Teniz depression in morphological terms as **discontinuous folding** according to the classification of V.V. Belousov, and in kinematic terms as **deep-seated (basement-controlled)**, the formation of which occurred under the influence of vertical displacements in the basement. The displacements were uneven, which is emphasized by the variations in thicknesses of various layers, members, and suites.

It should be emphasized that in the study of the basement of the western part of the Teniz depression, the leading role belongs to **geophysical investigations**. All uplifted and subsided blocks find their expression in the gravity field in the form of gravitational maxima and minima. No less effective in studying the structure of both the basement and the epi-platform sub-platform cover are seismic methods, in particular the **CDP (Common Depth Point) method**. By means of this method, the thicknesses of the sub-platform cover are determined practically without error, and its lower boundary is fixed by the key reflection horizon **T₁** down to the Kokchetav uplift and **T₂** to the north of it.

Joint analysis of gravitational, magnetic, and wave fields has made it possible to establish, within the studied territory, zones of **major faults** which led to the **block structure of the basement**. In individual blocks, intrusions of **granitoids** have been identified from geophysical data.

Somewhat different from the structures of the Teniz depression proper are the structures of the **south-western flank of the depression**, composed of carbonate and terrigenous-carbonate marine formations. According to the classification of Belousov V.V., the folding of this flank is **intermediate** between the discontinuous folding of the Teniz depression proper and the linear folding of its basement. In morphological terms it corresponds to **deep-seated, "box-type" folding**. It is precisely this circumstance that served as the basis for distinguishing **two structural stages** within the sub-platform cover.

Analyzing the structure of the central part of the depression and its south-western flank, **O.A. Mazarovich (1959)** put forward the hypothesis that if the folds of the south-western flank are linked to and controlled by series of **north-western faults**, then for the central parts of the depression the formation of folds is determined by the **strikes of structures of the ancient folded basement**. This position cannot be agreed with. Joint examination of tectonic schemes of the basement and

Seismic survey data across the entire studied territory yield concordant, sub-parallel reflecting boundaries for all deposits of the sub-platform cover. But this relationship, apparently, may also be apparent; we allow both the inheritance by upper-stage structures of lower-stage structures, and their inversion. Apparently, more complex variants are possible, when a glide fold of the lower structural stage is partially inherited in the upper stage. The question of structural inheritance in each specific case can be resolved on the basis of the relationship of thicknesses, facies and formations. Deep borehole drilling is necessary for its resolution.

The reliable history of crustal development in the western part of the Teniz depression can be reconstructed from the late Proterozoic.

It was precisely in the late Proterozoic that the studied territory entered the geosynclinal stage of development. Sequential accumulation of siliceous-terrigenous-basaltic, siliceous-terrigenous, flysch, and variegated terrigenous formations took place. The tectonic regime was intense throughout this period. In the Late Silurian – Early Devonian, the region entered an orogenic stage of development. During this period, large intrusive massifs of subalkaline and normal series were formed, along with the Ishim volcanic-plutonic complex of aplite porphyries and alkaline gabbroid rocks. In the Early–Middle Devonian, the first fragmentation of the basement occurred. Large faults served as pathways for lava to reach the surface, where they are sometimes observed together with red-colored molasse, and sometimes without it, accumulating in individual intermontane depressions. It should be noted here that the structure of the depressions did not coincide with the present-day structure of the Upper Paleozoic subplatform cover. And we cannot agree with the opinion of V.A. Babichev *(1965r.)* that the formation of the Teniз depression is attributed to the Middle–Late Devonian. Its formation is associated with later phases of tectogenesis. Possibly, individual large faults of the **orogenic stage** continued their development into the Upper Paleozoic as well, owing to which an appearance of inherited structural relationships between the subplatform cover structures and those of the orogenic stage is created, but this inheritance carries more of an element of coincidence than regularity.

In the Famennian Age, an extensive marine transgression began, which by the beginning of the Carboniferous epoch had captured only a small portion of the studied territory, reached the level of the Kokpektin uplift, and spread northward from it through individual shallow-water bays. The formation of the subplatform cover, its lower stage, began. The maximum of the transgression falls on the Lower–Middle Visean; at that time, marine conditions existed throughout the entire territory of the depression. The entire area, with the exception of the Nurumсайского and Atbasar uplifts, subsided intensively. Only the region of the aforementioned uplifts experienced weak ascending movements, where gently sloping low islands and shoals were formed.

Beginning from the end of the Visean Age, an increase in the role of vertical positive movements occurred, shallowing of the sea, expansion of erosion areas; the most intensive ascending movements began in the Bashkirian Age, but the structural plan did not change at that time — as before, the most intensively subsided area remained the region to the south of the Kokpektin uplift, to the north of which there existed a marine shoal with a system of islands at the level of the Nurumсайско-Atbasar uplifts. It is precisely at this time that the depression itself was formed through intensive but uneven subsidence of the area bounded to the southwest by the Derzhavinsk–Taldyk–Kireysk and Tasty–Tersakkан–Kipshak fault zones, and to the northeast by the Imanovo–Atbasар fault zone. At the site of the future depression, a large lacustrine basin formed in which sediments of continental origin accumulated against a background of multidirectional and uneven movements of basement blocks. At the same time, fragmentation of individual folds and blocks that had formed during the Lower Carboniferous period continued, and upper-stage folds developed on their basis. In this process, alongside the inherited character of folds, where upper-stage structures continued to develop on the basis of lower-stage structures (Achilinskaya, Yuzhno-Maylakul'skaya syncline, Shurumbaiskoe uplift, Atbasarskoe uplift), structures that underwent inversion also emerged (Kiiminskaya group of synclines, Tret'yakovskaya syncline).

By the beginning of the Permian period, relief leveling occurred both in areas of denudation and in areas of accumulation; the climate became more humid, owing to which grayish sediments began to be deposited, mainly of lacustrine origin. Lakes and swamps were inhabited by ostracods and algae. Possibly, some of the lakes developed in isolation. The water bodies were shallow and frequently dried up.

At the end of the Early Permian, the climate changed again, becoming arid, and salinization of the basin occurred.

At the end of the Permian period, the Upper Paleozoic sediments were dislocated. The present-day structural appearance of the depression was formed.

Mesozoic deposits are absent in the studied area; therefore, one can only assume that during this period surface leveling and formation of weathering crusts occurred. These processes continued, apparently, until the Late Oligocene. Subsequently, deposition of terrigenous strata of the platform cover began. Upper Oligocene deposits were apparently very widespread in the area but were subsequently eroded and are preserved to the present day only as isolated remnants. A new subsidence
