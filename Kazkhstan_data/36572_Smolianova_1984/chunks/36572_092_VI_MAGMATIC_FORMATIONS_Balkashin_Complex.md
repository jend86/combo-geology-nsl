**Balkashin Complex**

In the Balkashin complex of ultrapotassic granites, identified by V.I. Serykh (1963), a series of intrusive bodies located within the Kochetav uplift and composed of alexite and leucocratic granites was originally combined.

Directly within the study area, two of these massifs are located — the Balkashin and Bolshiye Tikti.

**The B a l k a s h i n m a s s i f** is the type locality for the complex, whereas the B. Tikti massif at the present time is by a number of authors attributed to a more ancient complex (the Zolotonosha–Borovskoy) ("Geological Map 1961" ["Magmatic complexes..."], 1982).

The criteria for distinguishing the alexite granites of the B. Tikti massif as part of the Balkashin complex are not clear to us, and in the subsequent description we follow the original opinion (Serykh, 1963), which, in particular, found its reflection in the more recent geological survey work at a scale of 1:50,000 (Pan, 1966).

The study area covers only the margins of the Balkashin and B. Tikti massifs. The Balkashin massif is located within the boundaries of map sheet M-42-XXXII (vil. Petrovka – vil. Novoromanovka), and the B. Tikti massif – to the east, on the left bank of the Sarykrean River (N-42-XXXIX). Two poorly exposed bands of scattered outcrops of alexite granites of the Balkashin complex: the first of northwest trend in the center of sheet M-42-126-G, and the second – sublatitudinal, extending

along the boundary of the Teniz depression (east of the village of Bogoslovka to the village of Doroginka) V.I. Serykh combined 2–3 isolated small massifs, a large part of which is concealed beneath a cover of Cenozoic formations. Subsequently, according to mapping drilling data (Pan, 1966), it was established that the first belt connects with the B. Tyktinsky massif, forming its lower arc-shaped termination, while the second represents an isolated massif separated from the B. Tyktinskiy pluton by a belt of hornfelsed rocks of the Lower–Middle Ordovician and the Garshinsky Suite of the Upper Proterozoic (which, however, does not exclude the possibility of the two intrusive bodies joining at some depth).

The exposure of rocks of the Balkashinskiy complex is generally good; they frequently form strongly dissected mountainous relief with relative elevations reaching 100 meters and more (Balkashinskiy massif) and numerous rocky outcrops. For formations of the supplementary intrusion phase, an increase in relief forms is characteristic. The prospectability of the rocks is greatly reduced due to dense forest cover.

In the rocks of the main phase, a "dome-shaped" jointing is clearly expressed, which is parallel to the roof of the massif (Serykh, 1963).

The granites of the Balkashinskiy complex in the described area intrude the metamorphic rocks of the Verendinskaya Series of the Lower Proterozoic, the greenstone volcanics of the Garshinskaya Series of the Upper Proterozoic, and the volcanogenic–terrigenous formations of the Lower–Middle Ordovician. In the endocontact zone, hornfelsing of the host rocks is intensively developed; moreover, to the northwest of the village of Doroginka (sheet N–42–XXXIII), the width of the zone reaches, according to the data of N. Pan, 2–2.5 km, which apparently indicates a gentle dip of the massif roof in this area.

Observations of the nature of contacts of the alaskite granites of the Balkashinskiy intrusion with the host rocks established their predominantly steep attitude (∠60–80°) with dips toward the host rocks, which, in combination with the isometric shape of this body in plan, allowed V.I. Serykh to draw the conclusion about a pipe-like three-dimensional form of the pluton (Serykh, 1963).

Active intrusive contacts of the alaskites of the Balkashin complex with the quartz monzodiorites and granitoids of the Zerendin complex have been repeatedly described by previous researchers. The younger position of the rocks of the Balkashin complex relative to the Zerendin rocks was unambiguously established (Serykh, 1963).

These interrelationships were observed by us in traverses T.7853–7853/2 and T.8001/4–8002. The first section was described in the chapter dedicated to the Zerendin complex, and in the second case it is possible to observe endocontact alterations of the granites of the Balkashin complex, which are expressed in a reduction of grain size (down to fine-grained), and in the formation of light, even-grained "saccharoidal" aplite-like granites.

Exocontact alterations were expressed in the potassic feldspathization (kalishpatization) of the quartz monzodiorites of the second phase of the Zerendin complex.

The contact line itself has unfortunately not been exposed.

Among the rocks of the Balkashin complex we distinguish: 1) rocks of the main intrusive phase — alaskitic and leucocratic granites, coarse-grained, large-grained and medium-grained — ( αγD₂b )

2) rocks of the additional intrusion phase — alaskitic and leucocratic granites, fine-grained and porphyritic — γ'D₂b

3) vein formations of the first stage — aplites, aplite-like granites, fine-grained granites.

4) dykes of stage II — dioritic porphyrites, diabasic porphyrites, quartz-dioritic porphyrites, granite-porphyries, trachytes (bostonites).

This scheme corresponds to that adopted by N. Panov and A.N. Kopytova in the compilation of maps at scale 1:50,000 (1966) and differs from the scheme of V.M. Serykh in that the rocks of the main phase are not subdivided into the first (coarse- and large-grained alaskites) and second (medium-grained alaskites) phases, since there are insufficient convincing data for the identification of these phases within the territory studied.

The dominant petrographic type of rocks of the Balkashin complex are coarse-grained, medium-to-coarse-grained alaskitic and leucocratic granites, with the first and second varieties characteristic of the Balkashin massif, and the third variety — of the B.Tikty massif.

Macroscopically these are light, pink rocks, practically free of mafic minerals. They are composed of **potassium feldspar** (40–70%), represented by microcline and microcline-perthite. Grain size 3×10 mm; **quartz** (20–25%), forming several generations; plagioclase (7–25%) — albite, albite-oligoclase; and **biotite**, the content of which rarely exceeds 1%. Accessory minerals — magnetite, apatite, zircon, sphene, monazite. The texture of the rocks is hypidiomorphic-granular.

V.I. Serykh noted that in the apical portions of the Balkashin massif the composition of the granites is somewhat different from that of the main phase. Leucocratic varieties are developed here, characterized, in comparison with the main-phase rocks, by increased biotite content (up to 5.8%) and plagioclase (up to 21.8%), and by some porphyritic texture.

**Rocks of the phase of additional intrusions (γ'D₂B)**

Fairly numerous bodies composed of granites of the phase of additional intrusions of the Balkashin complex have been mapped

-344-

in the process of surveying at a scale of 1:50,000 (Pan., 1963). Practically all of them are concentrated within the southern margin of the B. Tyktinsky massif, in the interfluve of the Aty-Mek and Sarkrama rivers.

Intrusive contacts with rocks of the main phase were described by V.I. Serykh (1963) both in areas adjacent from the north, and within the studied area — in the vicinity of the settlement of Sinovyevka (N-42-XXIII). Here, too, a breakthrough of granites of the additional intrusion phase by vein-like aplite granites of the first stage was observed. The total outcrop area of granites of the additional intrusion phase within the district is 14–15 km², although in practice it is larger, since part of the bodies is concealed beneath a cover of Cenozoic formations. In plan view, the bodies have a shape close to isometric; their spatial form is apparently stock-like.

The rocks of the additional intrusion phase are represented by fine-grained and porphyritic granites of alaskitic and leucocratic composition. Their mineral composition is close to that of the rocks of the main phase. Structural differences are noted: fine-grainedness, porphyritic texture, presence of zones with pegmatitic, granophyric, and aplitic microstructures.

Among the outcrops of granites of the additional intrusion phase, zones of silicification, epidotization, and greisenization are frequently encountered.

**Vein formations of the first stage**

Vein formations of the first stage are widely distributed among the rocks of the main phase and the additional intrusion phase of the Balkashin complex. In the majority of cases they have the form of dikes with northeast and northwest strike. The thickness of the dikes ranges from 5–20 cm to the first tens of meters (rarely). Vein formations are as a rule confined to the system of proto-tectonic fractures. Both steep (subvertical) and gently dipping dikes are distinguished, with the latter frequently infilling fractures of dome-shaped jointing.

In well-exposed rocky outcrops (Balkashin intrusion), it is often possible to observe a complex form of vein bodies formed by a combination of vertical and subhorizontal veins, with no active cross-cutting relationships observed between them, which is indicative of these fractures being filled by a single batch of melt.

The position of the described formations as veins of the first stage is determined on the basis that they intrude the rocks of the base of the additional intrusions of the Balkash complex and are in turn intruded by dykes of the second stage. These relationships were established by V.I. Serykh (1963) both in the adjacent areas to the north of the Kochetav block, and partly within the territory of the work area (vicinity of the settlement of Zinovyevka).

The vein formations of the first stage are composed of aplite-like granites, aplites and fine-grained granites. Porphyritic varieties are not uncommon. In large dykes, an increase in the degree of recrystallization (grain size) from the periphery toward the central parts of the body is observed.

**APLITES AND APLITE-LIKE GRANITES** are composed of K-feldspar (50–60%), acid plagioclase (5–15%), quartz (30–40%). Dark-colored minerals are present in the form of single bleached flakes of biotite. Accessory minerals: magnetite, pyrite, apatite. Rock texture is aplitic, aplite-like.

**FINE-GRAINED GRANITES** are characterized by approximately equal contents of quartz, K-feldspar and plagioclase. Biotite content varies from fractions of a percent to 4–7%. Accessory minerals: magnetite, sphene, apatite, zircon. Rock texture is hypidiomorphic-granular, sometimes granulitic.

**Dykes of the Second Stage**

Dyke formations of the second stage occur both among the granites of the Balkash complex and in the host rocks, and they are most widely distributed within the B. Tektinsky massif and in the areas immediately adjacent to it. In the southern part of the Balkash intrusion, located within the work territory, dykes of the second stage were not encountered by us. The greatest number of dykes is concentrated within the area of the northwestern corner of sheet B–42–XXXIV, on the left bank of the Sarkrама River, in the contact zone of the granites of the B. Tekty massif with the rocks of the Verendin series. The dykes have a predominantly northeast and northwest strike and can be traced for distances of up to 750 m.

The dykes are composed of **granite-porphyries**, among which, by composition of phenocrysts, N. Panov identified a great number of varieties

(plagiogranite porphyries, biotite–diopside granite porphyries, biotite granite porphyries, and others); **granitoids**; alkaline rocks — **syenite porphyries**, **bostonites**, **vogesite**s; **diabases** and **diabase porphyrites**, rocks of intermediate composition; **diorite porphyrites**, **markites**, **spessartites** and **alnitites**.

The position of the dikes as dikes of the second stage is established on the basis that within the massifs they cut through the major formation of Stage I (Serykh, 1963), and when located among the country rocks they tend toward the contact zones of the massifs and toward the apices of the massifs, which are composed of hornfelsed country rocks.

We shall not dwell on the petrographic description of all varieties of dike rocks of the second stage, since this question has been covered in detail in the works of previous researchers (Pan, 1966; Serykh, 1963).

In the majority of recent generalizing works on the magmatism of Central Kazakhstan, the age of the Balkhash (Orlinogorskoye) complex is recommended to be considered Middle Devonian. This same age is reflected in the "Correlation Schemes of Magmatic Complexes of Central Kazakhstan" adopted at the 2nd Kazakhstan Petrographic Conference (1974). This point of view is also accepted by us, since we do not have any new data shedding light on this question. However, one should note a certain degree of convention in such an interpretation, since it is contradicted both by the results of some absolute age determinations for the Balkhash massif and the B. Tokty massif (Serykh, 1963; "Magmatic complexes...", 1982), and by the fact that leucogranites similar to the Balkhash ones are noted in the composition of pebbles of conglomerates of the Lower (?) – Middle Devonian (Pan, 1966; Serykh, 1963).

In the gravity-magnetic fields, the Balkhash complex of alaskite granites is mapped by negative anomalies of gravity force and the field ΔT, which is well consistent with their physical properties. The magnetic susceptibility of the rocks of the described complex ranges from 0 to 247·10⁻⁶ CGS units (κ mean = 10·10⁻⁶ CGS units), density from 2.49 g/cm³ to 2.75 g/cm³ (δ mean = 2.56 g/cm³).

**Magmatic Formations of Post-Middle Devonian Age**

The youngest of the magmatic formations of the area known to us is a dike-like body mapped by the authors during a reconnaissance traverse along the Konur River (7868/5).

The described body is exposed on the left bank of the river, 2.8 km to the south from the southwestern edge of the settlement of Ostrogorka (N-42-XXXIV). The outcrop condition of the site is good, which allows observation of the relationships of the dike with the host rocks. The strike of the body is submeridional, the thickness is consistent at 15–20 cm; the body can be traced along strike for the first few meters. The dip, judging by the linearity of the contacts, is subvertical. The dike cuts red-colored sandy–silty deposits whose age is established as Middle–Upper Devonian. The relationship with the host rocks is cross-cutting (the strike of the bedded sequence approximately corresponds to the course of the river — sublatitudinal); on both contacts of the dike, 1–2 cm bleaching zones of the host rocks are noted.

The dike is composed of gray-green, fine-grained rocks of aphanitic texture. Spheroidal jointing is noted.

Microscopic study has shown that the rock has a porphyritic texture, with porphyritic phenocrysts comprising 5–10% of the rock volume and having dimensions from 0.1 to 0.6 mm, at present completely replaced by crystalline calcite. Judging by the form of the pseudomorphs, it is possible, with a large degree of assumption, to propose that the original plagioclase — amphibole composition [of the phenocrysts]. In addition to the phenocrysts, the rock contains numerous small mandibles of irregular form, of zonal structure, filled with calcite and fibrous chlorite.

The groundmass of the rock has a hyalopilitic texture. It is composed of randomly arranged laths of plagioclase immersed in a decomposed, chloritized, glassy mass. The rock is saturated with the finest segregations of leucoxenized ore mineral. Based on the microscopic characteristics of the rock, it can be defined as an andesite-basaltic plagioclase–amphibole (?) porphyrite.

Such a viewpoint is not applicable to the dyke described by us in the channel of the Konur River, since it cuts through the terrigenous deposits of the Middle–Upper Devonian, the conglomerates of which contain pebbles of volcanites of the Lower–Middle Devonian — the latter established by previous researchers and confirmed by our observations.

Thus, in light of the data available to us, it does not appear possible to associate the formation of such a body with any manifestations of volcanic activity within the studied territory. It is undeniable, however, that already after the accumulation of volcanites of the Lower–Middle Devonian, local manifestations of magmatic activity were noted in the area.

The age of the dyke, based on the totality of geological data, is defined as post-Middle Devonian.
