**Age of formations of the Borovo complex**

All intrusions of the Borovo complex in the study area cut through Proterozoic and Lower Paleozoic deposits, the youngest of which is the Kubasadir Formation of the Upper Ordovician (Sandyksu massif). They are overlain by Upper Devonian and Lower Carboniferous rocks.

Relationships with intrusive formations of other ages are usually unclear, as their direct contacts are either absent or obscured.

According to data from A.N. Nurlybayev (1973), in the area of Ishim Arc, granitoids analogous to the rocks of the Sandyksu massif actively affect the alkaline volcanites and plutonites of the Ishim complex. According to data from A.I. Prudius (1968), leucocratic granite dykes of the Borovo complex cut through the alkaline syenites and aplite-leucocratic porphyries of the Kubasadir massif. These facts indicate a younger age, at least for part of the granitoids of the Ishim Arc compared to the rocks of the Ishim complex.

Radiological data on the age of the described massifs are absent. The main criterion allowing refinement of the age of the described granites is a comparison of them with compositionally similar formations of adjacent territories, in particular — the Kireyevo outcrop of the Sarysu–Teniz uplift. Such a comparison, carried out by M.S. Grankin for the western and northern parts of the Karatas massif (Grankin, 1970), showed that by a whole range of characteristics the granites of this intrusion are close to the rocks of the Kashurlin pluton, whose age has been unambiguously determined by P.M. Shulga on the basis of geological data and radiological dating as Silurian–Lower Devonian (Shulga et al., 1969). In the current classification scheme of magmatic complexes of Central Kazakhstan, normal and substantially potassic-feldspar granites whose age of formation is defined as Silurian–Early Devonian are grouped into the Borovo complex. The same complex affiliation and age is accepted by us for the normal and leucocratic granites of the Sandyksu, Karatas, and Obalykol massifs.

Leucocratic granites of the Borovsky complex are non-magnetic (χср. = 22·10⁻⁶ CGS units), biotite granites are weakly magnetic (χср. = 220·10⁻⁶ CGS units). Density ranges from 2.59 g/cm³ to 2.61 g/cm³.

In gravimagnetic fields, the described complex has no distinct expression.

**Complex of dikes of the Karatassky and Obalikol'sky massifs**

Diverse dike formations are widely distributed among the granitoids of the Karatassky and Obalikol'sky massifs. They are composed of intermediate and acidic rocks — granite porphyries, diorite porphyrites, etc., among which a number of petrographic varieties were identified by previous researchers. It was also established that the dike bodies show no notable selective confinement to rocks of any one of the three granitoid complexes forming the Karatassky massif. It was noted only that in areas of distribution of granodiorites and quartz diorites of the first phase of intrusion of the Krinkudunsky complex, dikes of intermediate composition are more widely distributed (Grankin, 1970). Individual bodies are grouped into dike fields, so that overall the dikes are distributed unevenly across the massif.

For the Karatassky massif, northeast strikes of dikes cutting through the granitoids are characteristic, as well as strikes dependent on the folded structure for bodies intruding into the country rocks. Within the Obalikol'sky massif, due to poor exposure, no regularity in the distribution of dikes can be discerned.

By morphological characteristics, dike bodies of linear-elongated, arc-shaped, and irregular form are distinguished. Thickness of bodies is 5–20 m, rarely 1–3 m. Length up to several kilometers. Contacts are usually steep and near-contact alterations are practically absent.

By mineral composition (Grankin, 1970), M.S. Grankin et al. subdivided the dikes of the Karatassky massif into 10 types, and field study of their interrelationships allowed the following sequence of formation to be established (from older to younger):
1) felsite granite porphyries; 2) diorite porphyrites type I; 3) diorite porphyrites type II; 4) fine-porphyritic granite porphyries; 5) quartz-free granite porphyries; 6) granite porphyries; 7) diorite porphyrites type III; 8) spessartites; 9) diorite porphyrites type IV; 10) grano-diorites.
