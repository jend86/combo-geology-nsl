**Limestones** until recently were practically not described in the section of the Tersakkansk Formation, and if described, then only at the transition to the Kayraktin Formation. Study of the formation sections from wells showed that they occur throughout the entire section, but usually form discontinuous along strike interbeds and lenses with thicknesses up to 0.2 m. They are usually associated with siltstones and argillites, most often with those having gray horizontal lamination; they occur at the same level as interbeds of tuffites and tuffs. In the northern part of the studied territory they are more massive, since the section there is more fine-grained compared to the southern half of the studied territory.

Chemically pure limestones are absent in the Tersakkansk Formation — they contain a significant admixture of argillaceous and siltstone material. The color of the limestones varies from pinkish-brown to greenish-gray. Textures are massive, sometimes microstucco-like [microtexture]. Silicification spots are not uncommon in them.

Among the tuffogenic rocks, tuffs and tuffites are distinguished. Tuffs are macroscopically very light pink, greenish-pink, dense rocks of massive texture. Under the microscope they exhibit either vitric (Fig. 88) or lapilli-vitric (Fig. 90) structures. Crystal-vitric structures are frequently noted. Crystal-lithoclastic structures are, apparently, poorly defined in tuffites and tuffs due to admixture of sedimentary, semi-rounded terrigenous material.

The chemical composition of tuffs from borehole P-292 corresponds to the class of strongly silica-oversaturated rocks, close to liparites, but with higher contents of potassium, magnesium, and calcium. An analogous composition is found in tuffs of the southern part of the studied territory (borehole C-23). Both in the north and in the south of the studied territory, tuffs are partially or completely replaced by zeolites. Zeolite replacement in borehole C-23 is complete: the entire mass of the tuff is replaced by an aggregate of large-crystalline (up to 1.2 mm) zeolite of the analcime group. Sometimes, together with zeolite, calcite and hydromica develop in the tuffs. Hydromica forms in cases where the admixture of normally sedimentary argillaceous or argillaceous-aleuritic material constitutes more than 10–15% in the rock.

In addition to tuffs, tuffites are frequently encountered in the section of the Tersakkansk Formation, especially in its lower member. The content of tuffogenic material in them ranges from 25 to 65%, and sometimes up to 80%. Tuffites are macroscopically homogeneous, have a light brown, pinkish-brown coloration; at tuffogenic admixture content exceeding 50%, tuffites are externally indistinguishable from tuffs; gradational transitions between tuffs and tuffites are observed.

Pyroclastic material in the tuffites is represented by glass shards, crystal clasts of plagioclase of the oligoclase–andesine series, less commonly andesine, quartz, biotite flakes, fragments of the groundmass of liparites, dacites, and rhyodacites. The cement is basal, argillaceous-ferruginous with a significant admixture of ash material.

Obviously, during the formation of the tuffite in the core of the cosedimentation anticline, in a zone of more intensive mixing of material, enrichment of the volcanogenic material with normally sedimentary clayey matter could have occurred. The saturation with clayey material led, during diagenesis and epigenesis, to more intensive replacement of the tuffite by hydromica aggregate with calcite admixture.

In the northern part of the studied area, in layer 72 of the Archalinsk reference section (thin section A-72), the admixture of siltstone material constitutes more than 50%; the rock is almost entirely replaced by carbonate, zeolite, and quartz aggregate; ash fragments within it are sparse, but the admixture of crystal-clastic material is significant. In the section of the Tersakkan Formation along the Kenен anticline, during geological mapping at a scale of 1:50,000 and prospecting operations, a marker bed of distinctive rocks was described, which received the proper name "Kenen." It occupies a completely definite position in the section: it is confined to the twenty-second horizon from the base of the formation, to its fine-clastic part. The thickness of the bed varies from 0.5 to 3.5 m. Macroscopically this is a dark-brown, very loose rock, even at depths of 200–300 m, almost never exposed at the surface, but easily identified in boreholes. It consists of fragments of plagioclases of the oligoclase series, oligoclase–andesine, potassium feldspars, the main mass of trachytes, syenite-porphyries, a small amount of quartz, rare fragments of amphibole and pyroxene. Fragments of apatite, zircon, and magnetite are present. All fragments are angular or subangular. They are cemented by a clay–ferruginous material with a large quantity of scales of weathered, decolorized biotite.

In the section along the southern limb of the Iano–Mailikol'skaya syncline, at the same stratigraphic level, a loose arkosic coarse-grained sandstone was encountered, resembling in appearance the rocks of the "Kenen" horizon, but with a lower content of hematite and biotite in the cement.

In reference borehole 05, this same horizon was encountered at depth. It is more dense, therefore it was possible to prepare a thin section from it. The rock is clastic, in which there is no size sorting of grains whatsoever. Fragment sizes range from 0.1 to 1.2 mm. The coarse fragments are represented by dacites, dacite-liparites, and individual grains of well-rounded sericite-chlorite schists. The fine-to-medium-grained fraction consists of plagioclases of the oligoclase–andesine series, potassium feldspars, quartz, and biotite; amphibole, apatite, zircon, and magnetite are also encountered.
