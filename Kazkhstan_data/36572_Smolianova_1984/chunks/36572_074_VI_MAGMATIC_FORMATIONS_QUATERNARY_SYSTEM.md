**QUATERNARY SYSTEM**

**Lower Quaternary Deposits Q₁**

Within the Tersakkansk–Turgai and Ishim–Teniz watershed divides, cover deposits are widely developed, predominantly loamy rocks, identified by some researchers in the western part of the studied territory as belonging to the Kunshillik suite. The lower horizons of the sequence rest on very diverse-age rocks ranging from folded Paleozoic to upper Pliocene deposits. A common feature of the "watershed" sequence is the carbonate content of the rocks comprising it, the presence of carbonate and manganese concretions, and greater or lesser gypsum content. The structure of the section and the stratigraphic extent of the described deposits are not uniform in different parts of the district.

Almost everywhere within the sequence it is possible to distinguish two main parts. The lower part — greater in thickness — is characterized by lesser homogeneity of composition, by the presence of heavy loamy and clayey rocks of red and red-brown hues, interbeds of sands and grey clays. Many researchers assign this part of the sequence to an independent subdivision — the Muzbelsk beds, or the so-called "lacustrine horizon of the cover loam sequence." The upper part, as a rule, is composed of yellow-brown, homogeneous and light loams of loess-like appearance, macroporous, calcareous. This part of the section is called the "loess-like horizon of the cover loam sequence." In general, the upper part of the section has a complex alluvial–proluvial genesis, whereas the lower part was formed in lacustrine basins.

Deposits of the upper sequence form very extensive surface outcrops and have been studied in survey boreholes.

On the area of sheet N–43–XXXV, lower Quaternary deposits have not been encountered; however, they comprise more than 50% of the surface of sheets M–42–VII, VIII, XIV. A characteristic section for this district is exposed by borehole 374, south of Lake Mondykol (top to bottom):

1. Light brown sandy loamy loess-like loams of massive texture with small carbonate inclusions — **21 m**

2. Light brown sandy loamy clays with insignificant admixture of sandy material in individual areas — **6 m**

3. Brown sandy clays. Admixture of coarse-grained sandy material up to 10% — **1 m**

The thickness of the Lower Quaternary deposits in the north of the Teniz depression ranges from 10–20 m to 45–55 m. Such variations in thickness values among different researchers are apparently related not only to changes in the thickness of this sequence, but also to the fact that the underlying upper Pliocene clay sequence is often assigned to the watershed loams as well.

The lithological composition of the sequence, as already noted above, is very uniform. Loams predominate in the sequence. The loams are yellow, fawn, and reddish-brown. Textures are massive; the rocks lack any signs of bedding, with a characteristic columnar jointing reminiscent of loess. The loams are frequently gypsiferous and salinized, as evidenced by numerous small crystals of gypsum, salt efflorescences, and light-grey and white carbonate "zhur-avchiki" [carbonate concretions] on outcrop surfaces. After elutriation of the loams, a fine sandy residue remains in quantities of 10 to 20%. The composition of the sand is quartzose. The material is poorly rounded but well sorted by grain size. In the lower part of the sequence the loams are closer in composition to clays.

By its stratigraphic position, the sequence of "watershed" loams was most likely formed in the Lower Pleistocene, since it overlies Upper Pliocene deposits and into it are either intercalated, or are abutted by, alluvial deposits of the second floodplain terrace, the age of which, based on bone remains, is no older than the Middle Pleistocene.

Therefore, the age of the "watershed" loam sequence is accepted as Early Pleistocene.

Lower–Middle Quaternary Deposits ( Q I–II ? )

Lower–Middle Quaternary alluvial deposits are fairly widely distributed on the left bank of the Ishim River within the area of sheets M–42–II and M–42–П. They form a slightly elevated surface of the third floodplain terrace, gently inclined toward the present-day channel of the Ishim River. The absolute elevation of the surface of this terrace is 280–300 m. The belt of Lower–Middle Quaternary deposits extends along the river to the east, where these deposits have a wider distribution.

**Middle–Upper Quaternary deposits ( Q II–III )**

Among the Middle–Upper Quaternary deposits, alluvial and deluvial–proluvial types are distinguished.

**A l l u v i a l** Middle–Upper Quaternary deposits compose the second floodplain terraces of the Ishim, Labai, Tersaikana, Ashily, Archaly, Konur, Kairanty, Zaman-Kairanty, and other rivers. They occur with a considerable incision predominantly on watershed loams and on more ancient Neogene, Paleogene, and even Paleozoic deposits, and are partially overlain by sediments of the first floodplain terrace. In the valley slopes they have complex relationships with deluvial fans: they are frequently laterally replaced by, and sometimes overlain by, them. The contact with the underlying rocks is fairly sharp and is marked by horizons of sands.

In terms of lithological composition, a two-member structure of the second terrace is noted: the lower part of the sequence is sandy, the upper part is loamy. The sands are polymict, variable-grained, with gravel and pebbles, interbedded with pebble beds. The thickness of the lower horizon reaches 12 m (borehole D–46,

sheet M-42-II). The composition of the gravel and pebbles is predominantly quartzose, siliceous; near outcrops of Paleozoic rocks — semi-rounded, angular fragments of local rocks, sandstones, siltstones. The upper horizons of the second terrace are composed of a homogeneous sequence of loams, sandy loams, and sands of brown-straw color.

In the loams there are interlayers of more argillaceous composition and lenses of coarse-grained sandy material. The thickness of this part of the section is 1–4 m.

The two-member structure of the terrace is characteristic of the greater part of the described territory, but there are places where the structure of the section is single-member, or the thickness of the sandy part is very insignificant.

**Structure of the second floodplain terrace in the vicinity of Atbasar, as follows:**

The main part of the section — fine-grained sands, the upper horizons of which are compacted and locally show rare vertical jointing and even columnar jointing. Beneath these sands, boreholes have exposed a horizon of gray clays (4–5 m below the water level of the River Ishim), occurring in the form of large lenses. These clays are apparently lacustrine deposits.
