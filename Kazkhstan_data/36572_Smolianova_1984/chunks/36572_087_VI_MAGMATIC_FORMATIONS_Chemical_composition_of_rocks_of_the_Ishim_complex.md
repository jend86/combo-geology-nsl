**I. Ishim Volcano-Plutonic Complex**

The alkaline rocks of the Ishim complex crop out at the surface in the extreme northwest of the work area, within the axial part of the Markainagach anticlinorium. Here they form the unique Kubasadir structure (vicinity of Mt. Kuba-Sadir, M-42-VII) and the small-area Minsai massif (vicinity of the Minsai stream, M-42-II).

-306-

**KUBASADYR MASSIF** represents an isometric plan structure measuring 8–10 km × 13–15 km, elongated in the northeast direction. Its eastern half is located within the working area. The host rocks are sediments of the Kubasadyr and Akkairaktin formations of Ordovician age.

All previous researchers considered the Kubasadyr massif as an example of a typical volcano-plutonic association, within which formations of two facies are distinguished — effusive and intrusive.

The defining structure of the Kubasadyr massif is, according to current understanding, a deeply eroded subsidence caldera, filled with effusives of basic alkaline-series composition and their tuffs. At the second stage of massif formation, intrusive bodies of nepheline syenites, äpy– and pseudoleucite syenites of varied mineralogical composition and structural-textural characteristics were emplaced. The formation of the Kubasadyr volcano-plutonic structure was completed by the emplacement of intrusions composed predominantly of subalkaline-series rocks, ranging in composition from alkaline gabbros (essexites, kentallenites) and monzonites to alkaline syenites and granosyenites.

Based on the described sequence of formation of the rocks of the Ishim complex, we adopt the following simplified subdivision scheme:

**1. Effusive facies** — epileucitoite porphyrites, leucititites, trachi-epileucitoite porphyries, etc. — *S–D₁*

**2. Intrusive facies:**

a) Alkaline subcomplex — nepheline and nepheline-bearing rocks — *ε₁S–D₁i*

b) Subalkaline-earth subcomplex:

First phase of intrusion — monzonites, kentallenites, essexites — *ν–δ₂ⁱS–D₁i*

Second phase of intrusion — granosyenites, quartzose syenites — *ξ₂²S–D₁i*

c) Undivided formations — *γ–ξ₂S–D₁i*

**1. Effusive Facies ( S–D₁ )**

Alkaline volcanics of the Ishim complex occupy, within the Kubasadyr massif, an extensive depression of oval form with a length of

-307-

axis of northeast strike. As noted above, in genetic terms this depression is a subsidence caldera formed during the process of volcanic activity.

In the structure of the sequence, a distinct concentric zonation is evident, particularly well expressed on the northeastern flank of the structure. It is manifested in the regular replacement of rocks from older to younger in the direction from the periphery toward the center of the massif. In other localities it is somewhat obscured by intrusive formations of the alkaline and alkaline-earth subcomplexes, which cut through rocks of the effusive facies.

The volcanogenic rocks of the Shim complex accumulated over a prolonged time interval. A.N. Kurлыбаев (1966) identifies three periods of volcanic activity activation, alternating with periods of its waning. Each new period of activation was accompanied by the formation of new feeder channels and led to the formation of calderas of the 2nd and 3rd order.

The evolution of the magmatic chamber led to the formation within the Kubasadir structure of the following associations of volcanic rocks (from early to late): 1) leucite tephrites, leucititites, augite porphyrites; 2) leucite foidolites and their pyroclastics; 3) trachyte-epileuciticite and alkaline trachyte porphyries. Thus, through time the chemistry of the rocks changed toward increasing silica content with a simultaneous decrease in their alkalinity and potassium content. Characteristic of the described formations are steep dip angles (60–75°) and a dominant dip direction toward the center of the structure. Only the youngest members of the series — trachyte-epileuciticite and alkaline trachyte porphyries, whose outcrops are confined to the central and southwestern part of the massif — according to data by V.V. Taranin (1967) dip gently (∠ 5–10°) and form a sequence that overlies the underlying volcanites with angular unconformity.

The described formations are cut by numerous fractures and faults, frequently arcuate in form, close in their strike to the strike of the rock beds.

The thickness of the volcanites of the Shim complex is estimated at 1530–3350 m (Taranin, 1967).

In addition to effusive and explosive rocks, within the Kubasadir massif, formations of the subvolcanic facies have become widespread.

The formation of these bodies is associated with the period completing the eruption of volcanic products of phonolitic composition. These bodies are composed of epileucocratic porphyrites with large regular phenocrysts of epileucocratite. They mark the position of the lateral vents of volcanoes (Nurlybayev, 1966).

The subvolcanic facies is also characteristic of rocks of trachyte-epileucocratic composition. It is represented by small oval and dike-like bodies of trachyte-epileucocratic and trachytic porphyries. A brief description of the mineral composition of the predominant types of alkaline rocks of the Ishim complex is given mainly from the materials of A.N. Nurlybayev (1973). The results of silicate analyses illustrating the chemical composition of the rocks are presented in Table No. 54.

**LEUCITE BASALTS AND LEUCITE TEPHRITES** – dark green, greenish-grey coloured rocks. Textures are porphyritic (basalts) and porphyritic-like (tephrites). The groundmass texture in basalts is microlitic, in tephrites it is holocrystalline ophitic, microlitic, trachytoid and pilotaxitic.

The amount of phenocrysts in basalts is approximately 25–30%, in tephrites about 35–40%. Phenocrysts are represented by pyroxene (diopside or diopside-augite), pseudomorphs after leucite. Additionally, phenocrysts in basalt include plagioclase, and in tephrites – olivine. The porphyritic segregations are subject to secondary alteration. The groundmass is composed of an aggregate of pseudomorphs after leucite, K-feldspar, plagioclase, dark-coloured minerals, epidote, apatite, sphene, and ore minerals.

**PYROXENE, PYROXENE-PLAGIOCLASE (AUGITE) PORPHYRITES** – nearly black rocks with a porphyritic texture.

Pyroxene predominates among phenocrysts; plagioclase is less common. The groundmass consists of plagioclases, secondary K-feldspars, dark-coloured minerals, epidote, calcite, apatite, sphene, and ore minerals. Usually well recrystallized.

**EPILEUCOCRATIC PORPHYRIES** – rocks with a strongly porphyritic texture. Phenocrysts of epileucocratite predominate, forming regular pseudocrystals, ovals, or irregularly shaped grains up to 3 cm in size. They constitute approximately 15% (up to 25%) of the rock volume. They are composed of a mixture of orthoclase and liebenerite; in subordinate amounts biotite, grains of hornblende,

epidote, calcite, albite, fluorite.

The groundmass has a trachytoid and prismatic-granular structure, composed of orthoclase and biotite with albite and carbonate. Among the epileucocratic porphyries, rocks of the marginal facies are distinguished.

**T r a c h y t i c and t r a c h y t e - e p i l e u c o - c r a t i c p o r p h y r i e s** — dark-grey coloured rocks with frequent segregations of K-feldspar (trachytic porphyrites) and epileucocrite (trachyte-epileucocratic porphyrites), less commonly pseudomorphs after mafic minerals. The groundmass consists of orthoclase, albite, biotite, ore minerals, muscovite, etc. It possesses trachytoid, microlithic, and microgranular structure.

All the effusive rocks listed above have pyroclastic equivalents represented by tuffs, lavas, and tuff-lava breccias.

**2. Intrusive Facies**

**of the alkaline subcomplex (E₁S–D₁i)**

---

Outcrops of nepheline syenite bodies, the most widespread rocks of the alkaline subcomplex, are confined, as a rule, to the peripheral parts of the Kubasadyr massif. Their formation is associated with a series of arc-shaped conical faults formed around the volcanic centres of the "Ishim-type" stratovolcano. The form of the bodies is dyke- and stock-like; the dimensions of individual bodies are small, and the dips of contacts are, as a rule, steep (65°–80°).

The emplacement of the alkaline subcomplex intrusions occurred in several phases. A.N. Nurlybayev distinguished 3 phases: a) garnet-amphibole nepheline syenites, b) taxitic, epileucocratic and pseudoleucocratic syenites, c) medium- to coarse-grained sodalite-nepheline syenites. V.K. Belov and N.S. Sykova, on the basis of the study of the interrelationships of rocks of the Ishim complex in the course of large-scale mapping and according to documentation data from a large number of boreholes and mine workings, distinguished five phases within the alkaline subcomplex:
a) first, second, and third — nepheline syenites: biotitic, diopsidic, aegirine-bearing, taxitic, and others; b) fourth — liebenerite syenites; c) fifth — cancrinite-sodalite nepheline syenites.

-310-

The emplacement of nepheline syenite intrusions was accompanied by the formation of a complex of vein formations, which occurred in several stages. A.N. Nurlybaev identifies the following series of dike rocks (from early to late), succeeding one another in the process of emplacement of the intrusive facies: garnet-amphibole nepheline syenites; pseudoleucite porphyries; pegmatites; sodalite-nepheline syenites and syenite-aplites; minettes and vogesites.

The emplacement of minette–vogesite group lamprophyres completed the formation of the alkaline subcomplex of the intrusive facies of the Ilim complex.

The small dimensions of the nepheline syenite bodies and their complex mutual relationships do not allow representation at a scale of 1:200,000 of the full diversity of rocks of the alkaline subcomplex. Therefore, they are shown on the map as undivided.

The similarity of material composition and the features of geological structure indicate a close genetic relationship between the alkaline volcanics of the effusive facies and the nepheline syenites. Both the former and the latter are apparently products of the evolution of a single magmatic melt, accompanied by complex processes of magmatic differentiation and interaction with the enclosing rocks, which explains the diversity of types of alkaline volcanogenic intrusive rocks. The composition of the primary melt, according to A.N. Nurlybaev (1966), approached that of leucite basalt.

As noted above, the principal rocks of the alkaline subcomplex of the Ilim complex are nepheline syenites.

**N E P H E L I N E S Y E N I T E S** — rocks of gray color of various shades, from nearly white to dark gray. Grain size varies from coarse (5 cm) in pegmatoid varieties to fine in rocks of endocontact facies. Porphyritic structures are common. Textures range from massive to spotted and "gneissic", usually conditioned by the uneven distribution of dark-colored minerals. The most widespread type is medium-grained leucocratic nepheline syenites. The predominant microstructures of the rocks are allotriomorphic-granular and hypidiomorphic-granular.

The principal rock-forming minerals are: K-feldspar — up to 80%; nepheline — up to 40%, usually less than 30%, and its alteration products: cancrinite, liebenerite and zeolites; dark-colored minerals — alkaline pyroxenes (aegirine, aegirine-augite), hastingsite, biotite (often as an alteration product of other dark-colored minerals). Sodalite and melanite are commonly present,

rarely – corundum.

More than one third of the outcrops of alkaline intrusions of the Kubasadyr massif are composed of the so-called **libenerite syenites** – medium-grained nepheline syenites, transformed as a result of intensive auto-metasomatism into kalishpar-muscovite rocks of red and pink colors. We provide a group description of several samples of libenerite syenites (samples 7726/1, /2; 9225 and others.)

The rock texture is coarse- to coarse-grained, the fabric is massive or spotted. Mineral composition: potassium feldspar 65–70%, libenerite 20–25%, mafic minerals 5–10% or 10–15% at lower nepheline (libenerite) content. Microstructure is hypidiomorphic-granular, allotriomorphic-granular.

Feldspars form prismatic crystals, irregular grains ranging in size from 0.5 mm to 5.0–6.0 mm. Composed of perthite and microperthite.

Libenerite (epinepheline) occupies xenomorphic interstices between prisms of kalishpar. It represents a homoaxial pseudomorph after nepheline. Composed of a fine-scaly aggregate of muscovite with grains of cancrinite.

Mafic minerals are represented by secondary tabular aggregates of brown biotite and ore mineral.

Accessories: corundum, apatite, sphene, zircon.

**Alkaline-earth subcomplex**

Intrusive bodies composed of rocks of the alkaline gabbro-monzonite – granosyenite series are identified in one way or another by all researchers of the Ilmeny complex. Different authors regard them either as one of the phases of this complex (Nurlybaev, 1966), or as an independent complex close in age to the alkaline one (Lebedev-Zinoviev, 1963).

We, following V.V. Tarashin (Tarashin, 1967), apply the term "subcomplex" to denote the described rock association, in order on the one hand to emphasize its close spatial and temporal connection with the alkaline rocks of the complex, and on the other – to highlight the differences in the material (petrochemical and mineralogical) composition of these rock groups.

Within the Kubasadyr massif, the rocks of the alkaline-earth subcomplex form several relatively small stock-like [continues]

– 312 –

of bodies confined to its peripheral and central parts. According to A.N. Nurlybaev's data, the emplacement of these bodies is weakly controlled by the general concentric-zonal structure of the massif, which also distinguishes them from the formations of the alkaline subcomplex. Contacts of the bodies are sharp; the xenolith content of the host rocks is low. Gradational transitions between rocks of the alkaline-earth and alkaline series are absent.

Within the alkaline-earth subcomplex, we distinguish formations of two intrusive phases.

1. The first intrusive phase (ν–δ²₁S–D₁i) is represented by small stocks of alkaline gabbroids (essexites, kentallenites) and monzonites, located predominantly in the central parts of the Kubasadyr massif.

2. The second intrusive phase (ξ²₂S–D₁i) is composed of syenites and quartz syenites (granosyenites). Two relatively large bodies, composed of these rocks, have been mapped on the northwestern and southern margins of the Kubasadyr structure, respectively.

The interrelationships between the rocks of these phases, as well as between the rocks of both subcomplexes, were established during the preceding geological survey and prospecting work (Belov, Zykova, 1965).

Secondary alterations of the rocks of the intrusive facies during the evolution of postmagmatic metasomatism were expressed in albitization, liebeneritization, corundization, biotitization, zeolitization, and carbonatization.

In the exocontact zone, hornfelsing and skarnification of the Ordovician host rocks are noted, and in exocontact with the rocks of the effusive facies — fenitization.

**KENTALLENITES** – dark green, green, dark grey rocks. Texture is porphyritic, holocrystalline. Composition: pyroxene – 26–27%, olivine – 15–16%, biotite – 12–13%, K-feldspar – 20–21%, plagioclase – 23%; accessories – magnetite, sphene, zircon, apatite, fluorite, pyrite.

Olivine porphyrites (more poorly crystallized varieties) are confined to the marginal parts of the kentallenite bodies.

**ESSEXITES** – dark, grey and grey-green rocks. Medium-grained, in near-contact parts – porphyritic. Texture is hypidiomorphic-granular. Composition: K-feldspar – 40%, plagioclase – 35%, pyroxene – 15%, biotite – 10%; accessories: apatite, magnetite, sphene, zircon, brucite.

**MONZONITES** are represented by syeno-diorites and monzonites in various qualitative and quantitative ratios with gradual transitions. Texture is hypidiomorphic-granular, monzonitic, porphyritic. Composition: K-feldspar 25–36%, plagioclase 35–42%, pyroxene 0–10%, hornblende 4–16%, quartz 7–16%; accessory minerals — magnetite, sphene, zircon, apatite, rutile, pyrite, hematite, chalcopyrite, fluorite.

**S Y E N I T E S and Q U A R T Z S Y E N I T E S** (granosyenites) — pink, light-pink, pinkish-grey rocks. The diversity in the assemblage and relative content of rock-forming components among which A.N. Kurshbayev (1966, 1973) identified a whole series of petrographic varieties, the description of which we do not present here.

Apart from the Kubasadir massif, within the territory under investigation, rocks of the Ishim complex form the Shinsai massif. This intrusive body is located on sheet M-42-II to the east of the village of Monastyrka. The massif is practically not exposed, being covered by a veneer of Cenozoic formations; the only bedrock outcrops of its rocks are known in the channel of the Shinsai stream.

The Shinsai massif intrudes sediments of the Talsai Formation of the Lower–Middle Ordovician and the Shinsai Formation of Early Proterozoic–Cambrian age. Direct contact with rocks of the Talsai Formation is observed at Shinsai (Zharkoy, 1970). In the exocontact zone, weak hornfelsing of the host rocks is noted. According to data from previous researchers obtained as a result of exploratory drilling, driving of mine workings, and analysis of geophysical fields, the Shinsai massif represents two closely spaced but isolated bodies. The southern pluton is intersected by the channel of the R. Shinsai, and the northern one by the channel of the R. Chuzhakasai.

In the exposed part (R. Shinsai), the massif under description is composed of syenites and alkaline gabbros (shonkinites, essexites, essexite-porphyries).

In the concealed part, in addition to syenites and alkaline gabbroids, pyroxenites were also noted by previous researchers (Zharkoy, 1970). In general, the intrusive formations of the Shinsai massif in terms of rock composition may be compared with the undifferentiated formations of the alkaline-earth subcomplex of the intrusive facies of the Ishim complex of the Kubasadir massif.
