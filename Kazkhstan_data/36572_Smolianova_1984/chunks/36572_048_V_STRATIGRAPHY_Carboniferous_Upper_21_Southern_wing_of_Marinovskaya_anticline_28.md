Despite all the differences in the structure of the suite, certain features of similarity are characteristic of it as a whole, which are traceable throughout the entire studied territory.

Throughout the entire studied territory, the suite is divided into two parts approximately equal in thickness. At the base of each part lies a bed of coarser-grained rocks, and upward — a bed of fine-grained rocks. This is a distinctive manifestation of cyclicity, which we call **megacyclicity**. [201]

The subdivision into sub-suites has been carried out based on this manifestation of megacyclicity. Throughout the entire studied territory, the suite is confidently divided into two sub-suites: the lower Tersakkanian (C₃ tr₁) and the upper Tersakkanian (C₃ tr₂). The lower boundary of the sub-suites is drawn at the base of the coarsest-grained varieties in the section.

The division of sub-suites into lithological beds, in addition to the cyclicity of the section, takes into account the presence within them of rocks that appear in the suite episodically.

Thus, the lower Tersakkanian sub-suite in some structures is divided into two beds: the lower (C₃ tr₁¹) and the upper (C₃ tr₁²). In addition to the granulometric composition of the rocks, the presence of tuffogenic interbeds is taken into account when subdividing into beds: [illegible] for the lower bed, interbeds of tuffs and tuffites are characteristic. In the sandstones of the lower bed, zeolitic cement is very frequently encountered. For the upper bed, an increase in iron in the cements is characteristic. It may occur in ferrous and ferric form; in those areas where oxidizing conditions prevailed, hematite and iron hydroxides are present in the cement, and in grey-colored beds — chlorite and pyrite.

The upper Tersakkanian sub-suite in some structures is also divided into 2 beds. In them, just as in the lower Tersakkanian sub-suite, the lower bed is coarser-clastic than the upper, but tuffogenic interbeds are absent in it. In the upper bed, carbonate cements predominate in the sandstones. In it, a significant number of limestone interbeds also appear.

If the identification of submembers does not cause particular difficulties, and they can be traced across the entire studied territory, then the tracing of beds across the area is most often limited to one or two structures. When transitioning from one structure to another, their isochronous boundaries are not maintained. Many beds cannot be traced over long distances, and their lithological characteristics change significantly. Beds frequently transition from red-colored to grey-colored. Thus, the grey-colored upper bed of the lower Tersakan submember, identified on the eastern limb of the Tretyakovskaya syncline, transitions into an equally fine-conglomeratic but red-colored unit on the Proletarskoye and Poltavskoye uplifts (Fig. 49, Sheet 4). The undivided upper Tersakan coarse-conglomeratic submember on the Jenenskaya anticline transitions in the core of the Sarnobinskoye anticline into a submember clearly divided into two beds (Fig. 49, Sheets 2, 3).

If the thicknesses of submembers, as follows from Table 25, exhibit the same degree of consistency as the thicknesses of the formation as a whole, then the thicknesses of individual beds are not consistent even in closely spaced wells, at distances of 1–2 km. When geological mapping at a scale of 1:50,000, especially in covered areas, it is necessary to take into account the fact that in the formation section, lithologically similar beds repeat multiple times, while at the same isochronous level there may be beds that differ somewhat from one another in composition: in grain size, color, and textural characteristics.

When mapping such sequences, particular importance is acquired by careful study of the succession of changes in bed characteristics throughout the section, rather than any single individual bed characteristic. In our correlation of sections we have widely applied Heckle's principle of homaxiality of sections, investing in this concept a broader meaning, applying this principle in the understanding of S.V. Meyen.

Above we noted some general areal characteristics of the formation. Let us dwell on certain aspects of its variation within individual structures.

For the zone of the Kipshakskaya flexure, the presence of thick conglomerate interbeds throughout the entire section is characteristic; such a section character of the formation also occurs to the east of the studied territory as far as the Burkutbayskoye uplift. In this zone, flint pebbles frequently form from tuffogenic rocks.

For the Kenenskaya anticline, gray-colored interbeds are characteristic in the lower Tersakkan submember. A total of 12 horizons of gray-colored rocks are noted, and each contains elevated concentrations of copper, silver, and germanium.

For the structures in the interfluve of Syntас–Babdor and for the Moindykul'skaya syncline, located between the Obelikol'–Kyzylobinskaya and Mialinsko–Kolkazganskaya flexures, a red-colored section is characteristic with a distinct delineation of two submembers and a subdivision of the upper Tersakkan submember into two beds.

For the Taldyk syncline, a distinctive very fine-grained upper bed of the upper Tersakkan submember is characteristic. Dense carbonate bright-red siltstones, not encountered in other structures, are developed within it. The upper Tersakkan submember is characterized in this area by a widespread development of albite cements in sandstones.

Within the limits of the Mendash and Artyкpay domes, the Tersakkan suite is characterized by an unusually small thickness and the complete absence of coarse-clastic rocks.

In the northwestern part of the studied territory, the suite characteristics have been studied in isolated boreholes, and it is difficult to judge their lateral consistency across the area.

In the Tret'yakovskaya and Archalinaskaya synclines, the suite [illegible] is distinguished by a distinct subdivision into submembers and beds, by the presence of a chert horizon in the middle part of the lower Tersakkan submember. The role of each variety in the section is not the same: some constitute the bulk of the deposits, others are present in the section to one degree or another, emphasizing the difference between beds and members. The composition of both the principal components of the section and the episodic ones changes areally. This change is clearly visible from the attached table of the lithological composition of the Teresakkan Formation (Table 26).

**C o n g l o m e r a t e s** constitute in the formation section, in various structures, from 0.1 to 3%. By composition, polymictic conglomerates are distinguished, or, as they are sometimes called, poliformational conglomerates, or "Raimund-type" conglomerates, intraformational conglomerates, and conglomerates of mixed composition.

The maximum quantity of polymictic conglomerates is observed in the southern part of the studied territory, within the Mondykul syncline, Zhabdarsk and Kensin anticlines, in structures adjacent to the Kipchak flexure. In the north, this area of conglomerate distribution is bounded by the Saryobinsk and Kyzylobinskaya anticlines. Conglomerates are absent on the Zhunairkol uplift and in the Burumsay anticline. On the Mendybay dome they constitute only about 0.15 of the formation volume. A significant quantity of conglomerates is observed on the northwestern limb of the Mondykul syncline; fine-pebble conglomerates are present in the Tretyakovsk, Kivminsk, and Archalinsk synclines, but there they do not form persistent interbeds. In connection with their widespread occurrence in the southern part of the studied territory, they have been better studied there.

In all sections of the formation, a regular decrease in conglomerates from bottom to top through the section is observed.

Study of their granulometric composition showed that they vary from boulder and coarse-pebble to fine- and medium-pebble and gravelitic. In the south of the studied territory, pebble varieties predominate, while in the north — gravelitic ones. Conglomerates occur in the form of lenses, in which the coarseness of material increases in all parts of the formation toward the southeast. The lenticularity is of several orders: regional and local. The first characterizes, apparently, the spacing between the various streams carrying material from the denudation areas. The thickness of these lenses reaches 10–15 m, and their length 4–5 km. Lenticularity of a higher order is indicative of the dynamic nature of the depositional environment. The length of lenses in the latter case is 10–15 m at a thickness of up to 0.5 m.
