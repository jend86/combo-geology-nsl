# Age of the Ishim Complex

Within the studied territory, the youngest formations overlain by the volcanics of the Ishim complex and intruded by its intrusive bodies are the deposits of the Kubasadir Formation of conventionally Late Ordovician age.

The upper boundary of formation of the Ishim complex, based on geological data, is determined by the fact that on adjacent areas to the west, the described bodies are overlain by red-colored terrigenous deposits of Devonian–Frasnian age (Nurlybayev, 1966).

Figures of absolute age obtained in various years vary within the range of 374–488 Ma (potassium–argon method on biotite; Catalogue of determinations ........, 1970) or, according to data of A.N. Nurlybayev (1973), within the range of 405–460 **Ma**.

From the alkaline gabbroids of the first phase of the alkaline-earth subcomplex of the Ishim complex of the Kubasadir massif, two samples were selected for determination of absolute age. The samples were analyzed at the Laboratory of Absolute Age of the Geological Institute of the Academy of Sciences of the Kazakh SSR. By the potassium–argon method on biotite, the following figures were obtained:

| Sample No. | K | Ar⁴⁰ | Age |
|---|---|---|---|
| B 9252 | K=3.47% | Ar⁴⁰ = 117.6±5, 1 ng/g | 433±35 Ma |
| B 9252-б | K=5.88% | A⁴⁰ = 193 ng/g | 420 Ma |

The second sample is more reliable, as the biotite in it is better preserved. According to this determination, the age of the described rocks should be considered Silurian. However, taking into account the wide scatter of absolute age values obtained by previous authors, as well as the general controversial nature of this problem, we conventionally accept a **Silurian–Early Devonian age** for the described formations.

The intrusions of the Ishim alkaline complex are distinguished in gravity–magnetic fields by positive and negative anomalies.

Granites and syenites have the lowest density (δ avg. = 2.56 g/cm³, 2.57 g/cm³) and correspond to negative anomalies Δg; gabbros have a density of 2.89 g/cm³ and are mapped as positive anomalies of the gravity field.

By magnetic properties, the Ishim complex shows a clear differentiation from the enclosing strata. Theoretically non-magnetic granites (χ avg. = 10.10⁻⁶ CGS units) create alternating fields due to the hornfelsing of remnants of the enclosing rocks. Especially significant anomalies are observed over gabbro.

**2. Borovsky Complex (ϒS–D₁б)**

Normal and subalkaline granites of the Borovsky complex occupy second place in terms of distribution within the study area, after the granitoids of Late Ordovician age.

They partially compose the Karatassky and Obalykol'sky massifs and fully comprise the southern part of the Sandyksu massif.

Within the Karatassky pluton, the central and eastern portions are composed of Silurian–Early Devonian granites, where the outcrop area of the described formations is on the order of 24 km². Rock exposure is average to poor; a gently rolling relief with scattered bedrock outcrops is characteristic of the areas of their distribution. Interpretability is weak; on exposed sections, systems of prototectonic fractures can be identified. Later-stage formations are more exposed and, accordingly, better interpreted than the rocks of the main intrusive phase. Characteristic relief forms for them are ridges and mounds; on aerial photographs they are typically lighter than other rocks.

The granites of the Borovsky complex intrude into sediments of the Ulutau suite of the Upper Proterozoic, and also are in contact with alaskite-type granites of the Silurian (Aralaul'sky) complex. In the latter case, contacts are as a rule tectonic; in those areas where normal intrusive relationships are assumed, the contact zone is in all cases overlapped.

The described formations are overlain by conglomerates of the Daipin suite.

It should also be noted that to the northeast, the Silurian–Early Devonian granites are in contact with carbonate rocks of the Famennian and Lower Carboniferous. The contact boundary is marked by large northwest-trending faults delineating the zone of the so-called "Kireysky thrust," along which the junction of the Kishaksky trough with the Kireysky uplift occurs. On approaching this zone, fine fracturing appears in the granites, gradually intensifying, and in the immediate vicinity of the Famennian–Lower Carboniferous limestones they transform into friction breccias — light yellowish rocks composed of fragments of quartz crystals and weathered feldspar, enclosed in a thin structureless mass; rare angular fragments of carbonate rocks are also present.

The main petrographic rock type of the Borovsky complex within the Karatassky massif is biotite, amphibole-bearing [illegible]

...the porphyritic granites of the main intrusion phase. Macroscopically these are light-colored, pink rocks of varying grain sizes. Medium- and coarse-grained varieties predominate; fine-grained granites, having gradual transitions to medium- and coarse-grained types, are distinguished as a facies. The macrostructure is porphyritic; the microstructure is hypidiomorphic-granular, granitic. Porphyritic segregations are represented in the overwhelming majority of cases by alkali feldspar.

The mineral composition of the biotite granites is as follows: quartz — from 12 to 37%, commonly 25–27%; feldspar (orthoclase–perthite) — from 16% to 64%, commonly 31–35%; plagioclase (albite–oligoclase An 8–12) — from 12% to 40%; commonly 30%; biotite — up to 9%, commonly 4–6%; amphibole (common hornblende) — occurs not in all specimens — 1–3%. Accessory minerals are represented by apatite, sphene, zircon, and magnetite.

In addition to the rocks of the main phase of the Borovsky complex, previous researchers within the northern part of the Karatas massif described vein formations of Stage I (Grankin, 1970), which are represented by aplite-like, alaskite-like, and biotite–muscovite-bearing fine-grained granites having approximately the same chemical composition and differing in structural characteristics and in the proportions of rock-forming minerals.

The vein granites form both gently dipping and steeply dipping bodies, with the thickness of the former reaching 10 m, and of the latter 1–2 m. The aplite-like varieties range from 10–15 to 1.5 m. Along strike they are traceable for tens of meters.

Below, separate mention will be made of the dyke complex formations of the Karatas massif.

The Sandyksu massif is composed of granites of the Borovsky complex.

**S A N D Y K S U P L U T O N** belongs to the group of intrusions of the Ili Arc. It is located 2 km to the northwest of Lake Borlyköl in the immediate vicinity of the Kubasadir massif of alkaline rocks (M-42-VII). Within the study area, the southern and, to a minor extent, the western margins of the massif fall (exposed area on the order of 35–45 km²). From the west, the massif is entirely overlain by Cenozoic sediments.

The granites of the Borovsky complex intrude the formations of the Akkaprantin and Kubasadir suites of the Ordovician. The exocontact zone has an average width of about 250–300 m. In this zone the host rocks are hornfels-[illegible]

The granites of the Borovsky complex are overlain by Cenozoic deposits; according to data from V.V. Taranin, in the area of Lake Borlyankol — by rocks of the Lower Carboniferous, forming a small superimposed syncline here.

It is difficult to judge the form of the massif on the basis of available data. It is apparently characterized by an outward dip of the contact surface, which leads to an expansion of the exocontact zone and its merging with the exocontact zone of the Borlyankol massif, composed of quartz monzodiorites and quartz diorites of the Krykkuluk complex.

In the rocks of the Borovsky complex of the described territory, formations of the main emplacement phase are established, represented by two varieties of rocks: biotite–hornblende granites and leucocratic (alaskite-like) granites. Transitions between these varieties are gradual; in zones of xenolith concentration, the melanocratic character of the rocks increases (varieties close to granodiorites appear).

**BIOTITE–HORNBLENDE** granites of the Sandyksuy massif differ from the above-described rocks of the Karatas massif by a higher plagioclase content (43–45%) and greater basicity (No. 29–30), a somewhat lower content of K-feldspar (20–24%) and quartz (22–24%), and by the predominance of hornblende among the mafic minerals.

**LEUCOCRATIC (ALASKITE-LIKE) GRANITES** of the Sandyksuy massif are light pink rocks of medium- to coarse-grained and coarse-grained texture. Microstructure is granitic. The principal rock-forming minerals are: K-feldspar (up to 45–60%); quartz (up to 40%), plagioclase (oligoclase) (rarely more than 15%), biotite (no more than 2–3%, usually 2%). Accessory minerals: sphene, magnetite (accessory minerals are scarce).

Rocks of the supplementary intrusion phase are not developed in the work area. Vein formations of Stage I are represented by aplites and aplite-like granites, and dike formations of the second stage by granite porphyries, granodiorite porphyries, and plagiogranite porphyries (Taranin, 1967).

In general, the vein facies is poorly represented.

Taranin et al., alongside the Derzhavinsk massif, referred the granites of the Sandyksai massif to the second phase of emplacement of the Kriknkuduk complex (Taranin, 1967). Other authors (Prudius, 1968) considered these massifs to be of different ages and correlated the rocks of the Sandyksai pluton with the Borovsk complex. In our work we adhere to the second opinion (which, incidentally, found reflection in the "Geological Map of the Kazakh SSR at scale 1:500,000", 1981), since the rocks composing the Derzhavinsk and Sandyksai massifs, along with similar features, also have significant differences: thus in the composition of the main phase of the Derzhavinsk massif leucocratic (alaskite-like) granites are absent, and its phase of supplementary intrusions is represented by plagiogranites, in contrast to the Sandyksai pluton, where the supplementary intrusions (described in the adjoining territory to the north) are likewise composed of leucocratic granites. The widespread development of leucocratic substantially potassium-feldspar varieties brings the rocks of the Sandyksai pluton closer to the formations of the Borovsk complex, identified in the northern districts of Kazakhstan. Unfortunately, any more convincing data confirming this point of view are not available to us, and therefore the proposed correlation is of a provisional character.

Granites of the Borovsk complex are also identified in the eastern part of the Obalykol massif, where they are mapped from rubble-boulder alluvial outwash deposits. Due to poor exposure it is difficult to say anything definite about the structure of the massif and about its relationship with the enclosing rocks. The contact of the biotite granites of the Borovsk complex with leucogranites of the Aralpaul complex is, judging by deciphering data, tectonic. The rocks of the Borovsk complex are overlain (from the east) by Lower Carboniferous deposits.

The principal petrographic rock type of the complex in the Obalykol massif is biotite-bearing fine-grained granites and porphyritic varieties with aplite-like texture. The phase attribution of the rocks is not sufficiently clear; in all likelihood they represent a facies of the main phase of emplacement, as is the case within the Karatas massif. It is also possible that the aplite-like fine-grained granites are an analogue of the vein formations of Stage I.

Dyke formations distributed among the rocks of the Obalykol intrusive will be described jointly with analogous rocks of the Karatas pluton.
