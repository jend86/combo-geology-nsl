Incomplete thickness of the Teniz Formation: 595 m. The full thickness apparently does not exceed 650 m, since wells 143–144 overlap the formation almost entirely.

In the section two packs are distinguished: the lower — without lenses and interbeds of limestones and with a large number of interbeds of grey-coloured sandstones; the upper — with interbeds of grey and buffy-grey pelitomorphic limestones. Within the packs the ratio of grey-coloured to red-coloured rocks differs: the ratio of grey to red in the lower pack is 1:3, and in the upper 1:6. Mapping both packs in the field proved impossible due to poor exposure of the formation.

At the surface the formation forms discontinuous ridges and elongated, strike-parallel hills with alluvial wash-

layers of green-gray medium-grained sandstones and very rarely brown gravelites and limestones.

On the adjacent sheet to the east, during deep mapping at a scale of 1:200,000 (Petrilak, 1983), this rock sequence was assigned to the upper member of the Vladimirskaya Suite. During geological survey at a scale of 1:50,000 on the area of sheet M-42-67 (Zavraanov, 1983), this sequence was assigned to the Archalinskaya Suite. Indeed, in its appearance it resembles sections of both suites, but is actually an analogue of the greater part of the Kayraktinskoye Suite.

The newly distinguished Tenizskaya Suite, according to our data, can be confidently mapped over the area adjoining the studied area from the east, from the eastern boundary of sheet M-42-XV to the Burkutbayskaya anticline, located on the area of sheet M-42-XVII. For a confident judgment regarding its distribution in the northeast of the depression, data are currently not available.

Among the rocks of the Tenizskaya Suite, terrigenous sedimentary rocks are distinguished, chiefly sandstones, siltstones, and gravelites. Limestones in the suite section constitute no more than 1%.

**Sandstones** of the Tenizskaya Suite are most commonly medium-grained, with well-rounded but poorly sorted clastic material with less dense packing than in the sandstones of the Tersekkanskaya Suite.

In terms of clast composition, the sandstones of the Tenizskaya Suite are practically indistinguishable from the sandstones of the Tersekkanskaya Suite, and a significant difference exists only in the composition of the feldspars: whereas the feldspar clasts of the Tersekkanskaya and also the Kayraktinskaya suites are represented by plagioclases, in the Tenizskaya Suite they are represented by potassium feldspars.

The cement, by character of fill, is semi-basal rather than pore cement. In composition, the cement is predominantly carbonate, hydromica-carbonate, and only isolated pores are filled with quartz cement. Regeneration of grains is observed rarely. Chloritic and ferruginous "rims" are observed as frequently as before. Fine-grained varieties differ only in grain size. In the sandstones, a large quantity of fine plant detritus is noted, which emphasizes both the cross- and microtexture of the rock. In terms of saturation of the rocks with plant detritus, they are unconditionally more similar to the rocks of the Kayraktinskaya Suite.

In thin section 144/353, the cement of the sandstone shows a significant barite content. In thin sections of fine-grained sandstones, a basal crystalline carbonate cement is frequently observed, locally approaching poikilitic texture (section 144-310).

**SILTSTONES AND GRAVELSTONES** are practically indistinguishable in composition from the siltstones and gravelstones of the Tersakkansk Suite, but in the Teniz Suite the latter do not form persistent beds; they form discontinuous, thin lenses of limited lateral extent.

**Limestones** of the Teniz Suite are polymictic, clayey, analogous to the limestones of the Kayraktinsk Suite. Traces of mud-eaters are frequently observed in them. They represent tubes 0.1–0.15 mm in diameter, the walls of which are composed of cryptocrystalline material, and the central part of crystalline calcite.

In limestone from borehole 144, at a depth of 80 m, a "cone-in-cone" microstructure was observed.

In nodular limestones, sintered carbonate pellets and the argillaceous-carbonate mass cementing them are observed. In individual sections of the limestones, a breccia-like texture is visible.

The physical properties of the rocks of the Teniz Suite do not differ from the physical properties of the rocks of either the Tersakkansk Suite or the Kayraktinsk Suite; therefore, the described rocks are not mapped in physical fields.

With respect to magnetic properties, the deposits of the Teniz Suite are predominantly non-magnetic (χ mean = 50·10⁻⁶ CGS units for sandstones; for siltstones χ mean = 70·10⁻⁶ CGS units). Isolated specimens are encountered whose magnetic susceptibility reaches 410·10⁻⁶ CGS units. Such an increase in magnetic properties is evidently associated with the presence of magnetite.

The density of the rocks of the described suite varies from 2.49 g/cm³ to 2.68 g/cm³; the mean density of sandstones is 2.57 g/cm³, of siltstones 2.61 g/cm³.

The age of the Teniz Suite deposits is determined primarily by their stratigraphic position. They overlie that part of the Tersakkansk Suite which is unquestionably Upper Carboniferous, and partly replace the latter. In addition, they replace along strike the lower Kayraktinsk, middle Kayraktinsk

In the deposits of the Teniz Formation from borehole 143, at depths of 39 and 50 m, a spore-pollen assemblage was identified. Counts were carried out on sample 143/39 up to 100 forms, and on sample 143/50 up to 22. The species composition of the assemblage is given in the layer-by-layer description of the section. In the assemblage, according to the conclusion of A.I. Lopatina, typically Permian pollen predominates: *Striatopinites*, *Coniferites*, *Vittatina*, *Podocarpites*, but characteristic of it, as well as of the assemblage of the Terakkan Formation, is the presence, in addition to transitional Carboniferous miospores (*Calamospora*, *Crassispora*, *Lycospora*), of typically lower Carboniferous miospores: *Lophosonatriletes fuscatus*, *Acanthosonotriletes*. Possibly, they are reworked, but their presence distinguishes the spore-pollen assemblage of this formation from the spore-pollen assemblage of the Kayraktin Formation.

The age of the Teniz Formation is accepted, in connection with its poor state of study, as upper Carboniferous – lower Permian. Although we allow only a lower Permian age for the formation.
