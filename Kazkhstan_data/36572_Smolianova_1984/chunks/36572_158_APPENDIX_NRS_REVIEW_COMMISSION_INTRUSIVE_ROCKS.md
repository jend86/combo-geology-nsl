**INTRUSIVE ROCKS**

The intrusive rocks of the area range in age from Ordovician to Middle Devonian inclusive. They are represented by several granitoid complexes forming part of the basement of the overlying late Devonian–Permian age plains.

In the characterization of intrusive formations, the materials of predecessors have been used, supplemented and refined by the authors' investigations. The description of the intrusive complexes and specific plutons is complete and fully meets the required standard of the present work.

# Tectonics

The authors describe the tectonic structure of the area from traditional "fixist" positions, which allow, without particular difficulty, to present the modern spatial relationships and correlations of structural complexes, stages, and tiers of various ages and types, etc. The main tectonic structure defining the tectonic "face" of the area is the superimposed quasi-platform Carboniferous–Permian Teniz Depression. The polycyclic geosynclinal–orogenic complex (complexes) forms the basement of the depression, exposed along the periphery of the area and in the cores of local uplifts within the depression.

The authors briefly describe the Caledonian tectonic structures of the depression's basement and its framing, and dwell in detail on the characterization of the structure of the Teniz Depression. As throughout the report, the authors thoroughly describe the plicative and disjunctive structures of the depression. Analyzing the causes of the formation of local tectonic structures, the authors arrive at a reasonable, in our view, conclusion about the dominant role of block movements of the basement, expressed in fold deformations of the quasi-platform cover. At the same time, we consider that the authors have insufficiently emphasized the cause-and-effect relationships of specific basement faults (of positive or negative sign) and the cover.

The configuration of internal flexures and the boundaries of uplifts of the Teniz Depression, even at the present-day erosional level, testifies to their direct subordination in form to faults with predominantly extensional displacement. This is also addressed in the section on the history of development, where paleogeography is treated predominantly and there is no complete analysis of the history and regularities of the formation of the lower and upper quasi-platform tiers. However, these are matters of secondary order; on the whole, the chapter "Tectonics" raises no objections. The tectonic scheme provides a layout of structures in the form of a generalization of the geological map. In our view, it would also be advisable to compile a scheme of the main structure-forming faults, zones of various deformation types reflecting the authors' interpretations, in addition to the basement scheme.

# Mineral Resources

The main attention of the authors in studying the region was devoted to assessing the copper-bearing potential of the quasi-platform cover of the Teniз Basin. From the authors' point of view, the copper-bearing sandstones are genetically associated with deltaic, alluvial, and alluvial-lacustrine facies, which are confined to seven stratigraphic levels from the Middle–Upper Devonian to the Upper Permian. The authors describe copper mineralization of specific objects at various stratigraphic levels, analyze the conditions of formation of copper mineralization, and attempt to determine the causes of concentration or, conversely, dispersal of copper during sediment accumulation. Descriptions of polymetallic occurrences in the carbonate sediments of the Famennian are provided. The occurrences of chrysotile-asbestos are characterized in detail.

The authors conducted an analysis of the regularities of distribution of mineral deposits and, above all, copper. The main factors controlling the distribution of copper occurrences are: stratigraphic, lithological-facies, structural, and geochemical, given the initially sedimentary genesis of the mineralization. The authors' conclusions are based on extensive compiled and original material presented in the report.

The main exploration criteria and the predictive-prospective assessment of the territory have been analyzed by the authors with equal thoroughness. The report is accompanied by a large number of graphic appendices. The geological map produces a very good impression in terms of detail and drafting precision. Nevertheless, there are a number of remarks: **1.** The map contains few isoline elements, which reduces the possibility of three-dimensional perception of structures. It appears that the authors have sufficient material to introduce these additions. **2.** The structure of the basement appears to be shown in an overly simplified manner on the cross-sections and maps. This can no longer be corrected and, regrettably, the cross-sections will not enhance the map. **3.** A remark not directed at the authors. I believe that on geological maps at scales of 1:200,000 (not to mention 1:50,000), lithological coloring should be applied to Paleozoic sedimentary rocks; even on the map of the Teniz Basin, where standard "horizon-marking" contour lines are densely drawn, lithological coloring would lend greater expressiveness — not only visual but also substantive.

A large number of detailed columnar sections are appended to the report—

*...of* sections, which are valuable materials and of good quality.

In conclusion, I wish to emphasize that the present report represents a fundamental work. One may disagree with the stratigraphic interpretations of the authors, doubt the correctness of the age assessment of the deposits, but here the position of the authors is clearly and comprehensively set forth, based on the enormous body of material presented in the report. The report unconditionally deserves an excellent rating.

The work under review presents the results of a group geological re-study at a scale of 1:200,000, carried out in the western part of the Teniz Depression in 1978–1984. The study conducted forms an integral part of the programme for the comprehensive investigation of the Teniz Depression with the aim of providing a general assessment of the copper-bearing potential of the Upper Palaeozoic deposits of this region.

Table I presents the planned and actually completed volumes of work. It would have been appropriate to indicate the volumes of profile geophysical investigations not only in monetary terms but also in physical terms.

The report contains a very complete catalogue of geophysical surveys carried out in the western part of the Teniz Depression over various years. Some of the investigations are now of purely historical interest. From the cartograms of geophysical coverage presented, it follows that practically the entire territory of the geological re-study is provided with standard-quality maps of physical fields at scales of 1:50,000–1:200,000. In addition, during the course of field work, the author team completed a considerable volume of profile gravimetric and magnetic prospecting observations for the solution of specific geological problems and for the quantitative interpretation of geophysical anomalies. The methodology and techniques of the profile geophysical investigations give rise to no objections. The measurement errors obtained as a result of independent quality control for both gravimetric and magnetic prospecting observations are considerably lower than the design values, which attests to a sufficiently high quality of the field measurements. The authors' attention should be drawn to the fact that the drift graphs of the gravimeter null-point shown in Fig. 2 cover too narrow a time interval [illegible]

range, from June 22 to 26, and cannot characterize the instrument's operation throughout the entire field period.

The authors have collected and systematized extensive material on the physical properties of rocks of the western part of the Teniз depression, contained in numerous reports of predecessors. In the course of geological re-study, an additional 860 samples were selected from drill core for the study of physical properties of Upper Paleozoic rocks. Although the collected material is far from uniform in its representativeness, its statistical processing and subsequent analysis made it possible to reliably identify the physical boundaries of medium interfaces. The chapters "Physical Properties of Rocks" and "Stratigraphy" present detailed data on density, magnetic susceptibility, and velocity properties of rocks of various stratigraphic subdivisions. In addition, the section "Stratigraphy" contains a description of the physical fields associated with various formations. Apparently, in the course of describing the physical properties, it would have been advisable to indicate the number of samples on which the study of parameters was based. This would have made it possible to judge the representativeness of the sampling and, consequently, the reliability of the parameters themselves. Knowledge of the physical properties of rocks in turn enabled the authors to construct density cross-sections along complex geophysical profiles. The high degree of reliability of the cross-section selection becomes apparent from the comparison of observed and theoretical gravity curves. In addition, information was obtained on the elements of occurrence of disturbing bodies by means of quantitative interpretation of local anomalies in the magnetic and gravity fields. The calculated parameters are plotted on the structural scheme of the Caledonian basement. Incidentally, on this scheme the apparent thickness of the bodies is erroneously referred to as "strike width" — this needs to be corrected.

Very important data were obtained from the results of seismic exploration by the reflected wave method. It is this method that made it possible to determine quite accurately the thicknesses of the sub-platform cover deposits.

To study the lithological composition of rocks of the Caledonian basement, the authors made extensive use of well-known relationships between magnetic anomalies and gravity anomalies, which were formulated in the works of previous researchers and have repeatedly found their confirmation within the western part of the Teniz depression. In addition to the lithological composition, the combined analysis of gravitational, magnetic, and wave fields made it possible to establish the presence of zones of major faults within the studied area, which determined the block structure of the Caledonian basement. The validity of the interpretation methodology for geophysical anomalies chosen by the authors can be demonstrated using the example of the Oksanovskaya trough. Here, a positive magnetic anomaly with an intensity of up to 300 nT, having a northwest strike, and a negative anomaly in the gravity field of the same orientation have been identified. On the basis of such a relationship between the gravimagnetic anomalies, the conclusion was drawn that a granodiorite massif is present in the basement of the trough, which was indeed penetrated by borehole "A" at a depth of 1000 m. The productivity of the combined analysis of all geophysical data, carried out by the authors of the report, can be demonstrated using the example of the Kolutonskoye trough. According to seismic data, the basement of the depression in its most deeply subsided part is located at a depth of 2.5 km. It is represented, probably, by a large granodiorite massif, since a positive magnetic anomaly with an intensity of up to 200 nT and a negative gravity anomaly are also noted here. The Bunkarkol'skaya anticline, on the other hand, is mapped by a negative magnetic anomaly of up to 200 nT and a positive gravity anomaly. The Caledonian basement is represented by a terrigenous-siliceous formation of the Ordovician and the deposits of the Bratolyubovskaya series.

As already noted above, the results of seismic exploration play a particularly important role in characterizing the structure of the quasi-platform cover of the Teniz depression. Thus, according to geological-geophysical data, the thickness of the epi-Caledonian cover in the Ashilynskaya trough varies from 4.5 kilometers to two. In this case, the key reflecting horizon T₁, which corresponds to the base of the terrigenous marine formation of the transgression period, is located in the center of the trough at a depth of 4.3 km. The reflecting horizon T₃, corresponding to the base of the Tersakkanskoye suite, is located in the center of the trough at a depth of 2.7 km. According to gravity survey data, the depth to the basement in the central part of the trough amounts to 4 km, which is in full agreement with the seismic exploration results.

In connection with this, there are only a few remarks. Thus, gravimagnetic anomalies allow one to conclude that the Kolboldin syncline is underlain by a granodiorite intrusion. For some reason, this fact is not considered in the report. As for the Archalin syncline, judging by the ratio of gravimagnetic fields, it most likely has gabbro-diabase in its basement. With the authors' supposition that the Zharkainarghachsky anticlinorium continues eastward, beyond the boundaries of the studied territory, one can agree, although the absence in the report of geophysical materials for a large part of sheet M-42-IV does not allow this to be affirmed with full justification. In general, it should be emphasized that the results of interpretation of geophysical fields, incorporated into the cartographic material of the report under consideration, are carefully tied to the available geological data. However, the authors approached the description of physical fields in the report text in a formal manner. They use mainly such concepts as "positive field," "negative field," and "inexpressive field" (!). Of course, this is insufficient. There are generally accepted characteristics of geophysical anomalies, such as intensity, gradient magnitude, form, dimensions, azimuth of elongation, and other features, which must invariably accompany the description of each anomaly. Without this, a well-grounded comparison of various disturbing objects is impossible. In the report, for example, it is stated only that the Kubasadar and Shansai intrusions of the Ivin alkaline complex are characterized by "positive and negative gravity anomalies" and "alternating-sign magnetic fields." From such a description it is impossible to draw a conclusion about the affiliation of the indicated intrusions to a single complex. True, in all fairness it should be said that maps at a scale of 1:500,000 do not always allow a detailed geophysical characterization of geological objects to be given. For this purpose, one could have made use of inset maps constructed at a larger scale. Unfortunately, such maps are absent from the report.
