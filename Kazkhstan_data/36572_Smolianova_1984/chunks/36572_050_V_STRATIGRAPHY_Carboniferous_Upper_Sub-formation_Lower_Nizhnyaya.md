As a result of observations on the granulometric composition of conglomerates in the south of the studied territory, V.M. Smolyaninova (1970) established several flows transporting material from the Sarsu-Teniz uplift: the first — between lakes Kipshak and Kunakei; the second — in the eastern half of sheet M-42-XV, east of Lake Kirei; the third — in the area of the Iyano-Mailikol syncline; the fourth — in the area of the Kenensky anticline. In the north, from the Kokshetav arch, gravel material was transported in the area of the western limb of the Montikul syncline, on the eastern limb of the Tretyakov syncline and on the northern limb of the Archalin syncline.

Sorting of material in the conglomerates is moderate. Sorting coefficients, determined by the method of P.D. Trask, fall within the range up to 2.5. A deviation toward better sorting of material (coefficient 1.2) is observed in the northern part of the studied territory, within the Tretyakov syncline. Coefficients exceeding background values by 1.5–2 times, indicating poor sorting of material, are noted in individual samples on the Kenensky anticline, along the southern limb of the Iyano-Mailikol syncline and in the lower beds of the formation in the zone of the Kipshak flexure, east of Lake Kirei.

The shape of the clasts is usually rounded, less commonly flat and isometric. In terms of roundness, rounded and well-rounded varieties predominate. Angular-rounded varieties are encountered occasionally in the uppermost beds of the formation, and in the southeastern part of the studied territory south of Lake Tengiz in the lower beds of the formation.

Polymict, deformational conglomerates are associated in section with coarse psephitic sediments: coarse-, large- and medium-grained sandstones. They occur mainly at the base of the Mesozoic. The composition of the clastic material in them is varied and very diverse. Throughout the studied territory, the following have been identified in the clast composition: liparites and their tuffs, dacites and their tuffs, andesite porphyrites and their tuffs, diabases, quartzites, marbles, slates, cherts, siliceous siltstones, gneisses, granite-gneisses, quartz, coals, granitoids, limestones with Upper Devonian fauna, tourmaline, biotite, porphyritic limestones, sandstones, siltstones, argillites, spheroidites. In the north of the studied territory the composition of the pebbles is less diverse; they contain no limestones with fauna, fewer effusives of acidic composition, but the quantity of quartz increases.

The conglomerates have their own specific petrographic composition in each lens. Thus, the lowermost bed of conglomerates in the Konensky anticline is characterized by the most varied pebble composition, including almost all the petrographic varieties of rocks noted above. In the Kino-Mailikol'skaya syncline, the pebble composition is uniform — these are mainly rock fragments of the Upper Devonian–Lower Carboniferous marine carbonate complex. In the eastern part of the Bishakskaya flexure, the lower bed contains crushed granitoids and pebbles of acid effusives. In the Archalinsk trough — gravel of siliceous schists and cherts.

For the beds of the middle part of the Tereakkansk suite, a varied composition of pebbles and gravel is characteristic throughout the entire studied territory. An exception in this part of the section is the northeastern limb of the Konensky anticline and the Kino-Mailikol'skaya syncline, where the pebble composition in the middle part of the section is homogeneous carbonate. In the central parts of the studied territory, interbeds of polymictic conglomerates are absent in the middle part of the section; they are replaced by coarse-grained sandstones and conglomerates of mixed composition. For the uppermost horizons of the Tereakkansk suite, a varied composition of clastic material is observed in the conglomerates of the Tsentral'no-Tenizskaya trough. For all other structures of the southern half of the studied territory, a simple limestone pebble composition is characteristic. In the north of the studied territory, except for the Archalinsky trough, conglomerates in the upper-suite sections are absent.

Pebbles and boulders in the conglomerates of the southern part of the area have a dense and very dense packing; traces of indentation of dense siliceous pebbles and sand grains of cement into softer limestone and argillite pebbles are frequently observed (Fig. 28). The latter often show traces of crushing. Crushing fractures were observed not only macroscopically but also under the microscope (Fig. 29).

In the central parts of the studied territory and in individual interbeds in the north, the conglomerates acquire a "pudding" character, where individual pebbles and boulders lie sporadically within a sandstone mass.

Upon weathering, all conglomerates disintegrate, with the pebbles forming wide spreads that exceed the true thicknesses by 10–20 times. This leads to confusion among many researchers who believed that conglomerates constitute the upper Paleozoic sections in the Tersekkan River basin ubiquitously up to 30–40m.

Conglomerates of the second type, referred to by us as intraformational, are most commonly confined to thin rock varieties at the tops of mesoritms, and in the northern part of the studied area and in its central parts — also at their bases. They occur at the base of sedimentary microrhythms; their thicknesses do not exceed 0.5–1m. The lenses of these conglomerates pinch out very rapidly. By granulometric composition they may be classified as fine-pebble.

Conglomerates of this type within the studied area consist exclusively of angular fragments of sandstones, siltstones, and pebbles of argillites of the Tersekkan suite. Argillite pebbles, more correctly called clasts, sometimes "capture" the sandy cement material (Fig. 30). This indicates, apparently, the softness and plasticity of the argillite clasts during the formation of intraformational conglomerates. Compression marks are frequently observed in them. Intraformational conglomerates are cemented by sandy material.

Particularly abundant intraformational conglomerates occur in the cores of anticlinal structures and in the northern half of the studied area, where practically in every rhythmic unit siltstone clasts can be encountered.

On the limbs of many anticlinal structures, mixed-type conglomerates appear, where reworked material of the suite itself is added to polymictic pebbles. A great many mixed-type conglomerates occur on the limbs of the Kyzyl-Obinsky anticline, especially on its southern limb. The appearance in the sections of mixed-type conglomerates on the flanks of anticlines indicates, apparently, the cosedimentation nature of the anticlinal structures.

Polymictic conglomerates were formed, apparently, in river valleys, deltas, and coastal parts of lakes. The large quantity of conglomerates in the section of the suite, the presence of granitoid pebbles in them, along with the wide distribution of coarse-grained sandstones throughout the entire section of the suite — testifies, on the one hand, to physical weathering in the denudation areas and to their proximity to the Teniz depression; and on the other hand, to the strongly dissected relief of the denudation area adjacent to the south of the Teniz depression. Such an area could have been the Sarisu–Teniz uplift. Comparison of the petrographic composition of the clastic fraction of the conglomerates with the composition of rocks of various formations of the Sarisu–Teniz watershed demonstrated their complete identity. Erosion from the Kokchetav massif was lesser. The relief on it was less dissected. More dissected and, apparently, higher was the relief to the west of the Teniz depression.

**G R A V E L I T E S** occupy an intermediate position between fine-pebble conglomerates and coarse-grained sandstones. The composition of clasts in them is analogous to the conglomerates. In the north of the studied territory they form independent lenses and interbeds, irregular in vertical and horizontal extent. In the south they are associated with conglomerates, frequently connected to them by gradational transitions. The character of the cement in them is the same as in the sandstones.

**S a n d s t o n e s** constitute the main part of the Tereakkan suite in all sections. Their content in individual structures is given in Table 26, from which it follows that the dominant role belongs to medium- and fine-grained red-colored varieties. Grey and greenish-grey sandstones were observed in only a few structures in the southern part of the area and in the majority of structures to the north of the Atbasar uplift.

In the southern half of the studied territory, grey and greenish-grey varieties were observed only in the Konensky anticline. In the central parts they are encountered in the core of the Kyzylобинская anticline. In the western part of the described area they are absent. In the Tretyakovskaya and Vladimirskaya synclines they form thick beds in the lower sub-suite and individual interbeds and lenses at the transition to the Kayrakt suite. Grey-colored rocks at the transition to the Kayrakt suite are present in the Mendybay dome, Nurumsa anticline, and on the southern limb of the Atbasar uplift.

Among the sandstones, as well as among the rest of the terrigenous components of the sections, pattum varieties play an enormous role. This is evidence of poor sorting of the material and proximity to the source areas. The textures of the pattums do not lend themselves to precise definition, and therefore they are given double names: medium-fine-grained (fig. 31), medium-coarse-grained, if fragments of the corresponding grain size occur in approximately equal quantities in the rock; gravelly sandstone, aleurolitic sandstone, argillaceous sandstone, if one slightly predominates over the others.
