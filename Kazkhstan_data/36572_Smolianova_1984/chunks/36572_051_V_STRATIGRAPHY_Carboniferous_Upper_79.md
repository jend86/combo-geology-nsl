Fine-grained sandstones are confined to the upper parts of mesorrhythms, are associated with aleurolites and argillites, and very often have gradational transitions with them. Their composition over the entire studied area is uniform, changes little laterally, but upward through the section, at the transition to the Kayraktinsky suite, quartz and feldspars sharply predominate among the clastic material of the fine-grained sandstones. In all other parts of the Tersakkanian suite sections, both brown and grey fine-grained sandstones consist of angular to sub-rounded fragments, the size of which does not exceed 0.15 mm. Composition of the clastic material: feldspars 30–35%, with potassium feldspar predominating over plagioclase in the upper parts; quartz 20–25%; aleurolites and argillites 20–25%; the main mass of acidic and intermediate effusives approximately 5%; diabase in the south up to 10%, in the north does not exceed 5%; the main mass of trachytes 5%, limestones no more than 5% in the south, less than 1% in the north; sericitic, sericite-chloritic schists 1–2% up to 10% in the north. In all sandstones a significant admixture of titanomagnetites is observed, the content of which in some cases, in the brown varieties, reaches 5–10%. In addition, in rare clasts the following were observed: serpentinite, glauconite, flakes of biotite, muscovite, topaz, sphene, epidote, tourmaline, apatite, zircon.

The type of cementation in fine-grained sandstones is mainly basal, less commonly pore-type. By composition the cement is calcareous-argillaceous-aleurolitic, calcareous-argillaceous-ferruginous. In the latter case the colour of the rock becomes cherry-brown. In the grey varieties there is much plant detritus along the bedding planes; in the greenish-grey varieties the cement is pore-type, less commonly basal with chlorite. Textures in fine-grained sandstones are most often massive or wavy-laminated, sometimes horizontally laminated. Almost constantly observed in them are thin (up to 10 cm) and very thin (1.5–2 mm) interlayers of siltstones and argillites, which accentuate the lamination pattern.

Medium-grained and coarse-grained sandstones are noted in the lower horizons; they are associated with fine-grained varieties and are connected with one another by gradational transitions, have lenticular interbedding with one another, and together in cross-section are associated both with conglomerates, gravelites, and with siltstones. They are observed in cross-sections and areally in a wide range of the same colors — from pink, cherry-red, brown, and buffy to greenish-grey and grey. Most often the coloration within the layers changes gradually.

The clastic material in them is sub-rounded; a dependence between the composition of the clasts and the degree of rounding is observed. Thus, grains of siltstones, serpentinites, diabases, quartzites, and acidic effusives are well rounded; semi-rounded or angular are clasts of feldspars and limestones. The clastic material in the south of the studied territory has a dense and tightest packing (fig. 32), with indentation of some grains into others observed. For the central and northern areas the packing is less dense; in places the cement is basal.

The composition of the clastic material is analogous to the composition of the fine-grained sandstones, with the composition of the grey-colored varieties being perfectly analogous to the red-colored ones. Plagioclases in the south of the studied territory comprise from 15% to 25%, and in the north up to 30–35%; quartzites, siliceous argillites and siltstones 10–15%; sandstones and siltstones 5–10%; limestones in the south up to 15%, in the north do not exceed 5%. Acidic and intermediate effusives, diabases, andesite porphyrites, trachytes, serpentinites, ferruginous quartzites, epidosites, microgranites, quartz, feldspars, hornstones, biotite flakes, titanomagnetites — are encountered as rare clasts. Besides these, there are individual grains of sphene, glauconite, tourmaline. Across the entire area a certain regularity is observed in the appearance of sandstones enriched with biotite flakes (fig. 3B): they appear from the middle part of the lower Neteresakkanian substage and are nowhere present at the base of the formation.

As noted above, grain packing is most often dense; therefore, sandstones most commonly exhibit a pore-type cementation (Figs. 34, 35), less frequently basal or mixed types. In composition, the cements of red-colored sandstones differ substantially from those of gray-colored ones. All coarse-grained red-colored sandstones are characterized by ferruginous "shirts" that "clothe" the grains of these sandstones. The "shirts" are composed of iron oxides and hydroxides; they are clearly visible both in thin sections and under the microscope (Fig. 33). In greenish-gray varieties, "shirts" are also observed, but they consist of chlorite and sometimes hydromica.

Pore voids in red-colored sandstones are filled with argillaceous-carbonate, siliceous-argillaceous, and siliceous-aleurolitic cement, always with a significant admixture of iron hydroxides. In brownish-gray and gray-brown varieties, the cements are predominantly siliceous-carbonate and carbonate-siliceous, with an admixture of argillaceous and aleurolitic material. A distinguishing feature of the cements of greenish-gray sandstones is the presence of chlorites in them. Gray and greenish-gray sandstones are cemented by carbonate, siliceous-carbonate, and siliceous cement with albite.

For cements of all sandstones, a direct relationship between the composition of the cement and the composition of the clastic fraction is characteristic. Sandstones containing significant quantities of limestone clasts most commonly have carbonate, argillaceous-ferruginous-carbonate cement; sandstones with siliceous clasts have siliceous-argillaceous and siliceous-albite cement.

Regeneration-type cement is frequently observed throughout the section and across the entire territory. Quartz grains are regenerated by quartz (Fig. 37), feldspar grains by albite, marbles and marmorized limestones by calcite.

Siliceous cement frequently has the character of crystallization cement. In thin sections it is clearly visible how pyramidal quartz crystals grow on the walls of pores, replacing the argillaceous mass. In cases where the pores are completely filled by quartz, this cement acquires the appearance of pore-type cement. There is no specific spatial association with a defined area for this type of cement. Such pores may be encountered individually in thin sections alongside other types of cement, but only within the Tersakan Formation. Albite is present in the cement virtually everywhere, but its maximum quantity is noted in the west and southwest of the studied territory, especially abundant in the deposits of the Tersakkan Formation described in reference boreholes 14A, 14B, the reference Baksy–Kayrakt section, i.e., in the Taldinsky and Kiyminsky synclines and in the axial part of the Kenensky anticline. Albite in pore cavities is usually distinguished after quartz.

The Tersakkan Formation is characterized by a significant content of zeolites in the sandstone cement in the lower part of the formation. The sharp increase of zeolites in cements is apparently related to intensification of volcanic activity in adjacent areas. The appearance of zeolites in the cement is closely associated with the appearance in the sections of tuff and tuffite beds. The first zeolitized tuffaceous tuffs and admixture of zeolites in sandstone cements were described in the Teniz depression by E.M. Smolyaninova (1970) for the lower part of the Tersakkan Formation in the southwestern part of the depression; they were subsequently described by N.V. Dmitrovsky for the Archalinsky syncline and along the Kulan–Utpes River. However, he assigned this zeolite-bearing sequence to the Kireyskaya Formation.

The cement of all sandstones is carbonate to one degree or another. However, in the sections of individual structures there are beds and groups of beds that are practically carbonate-free. Systematic observation of the carbonate content of the section was conducted only for the southwestern part of the studied territory. For the lower Tersakkan sub-formation in reference sections on the Kenensky anticline, alternation of carbonate and non-carbonate cement beds is characteristic. Such alternation of beds is evidence of the instability of the CO₂ regime during the period of deposition of the lower formation sediments. This instability may have been caused by volcanic activity in adjacent areas, where each eruption raises the concentration of CO₂ in the atmosphere; by shallowing of the basin with penetration of CO₂ into deeper parts of the basin; by suppression of plant activity in the water. Alternation of beds with carbonate cement and beds with non-carbonate cement is characteristic of sandstones and siltstones of the lowermost part of the formation. For the upper half, all terrigenous beds contain carbonate in the cement.

In the areas south of the Atbasarsky uplift, in the upper half of the formation, so-called "cavernous" sandstones are observed. These are medium- to fine-grained sandstones with areas of ferruginous cement. The size of these areas is 1.5–2 cm in diameter. Under surface conditions and in shallow boreholes these areas weather more intensively. A "cavern" forms, and the rocks acquire a characteristic pockmarked surface.

**A l e u r o l i t e s** and **a l e u r o - s a n d s t o n e s**, as follows from Table 26, have a very wide distribution. They are confined to the tops of mesorhythms and microrhythms, associate with fine-grained sandstones and argillites, and are linked to both the former and the latter by gradational transitions. Widely distributed are aleurolites with an admixture of sandy material of coarser grain size. Color ranges from red to black.

The clastic material is angular to subrounded. The degree of sorting of the material is better compared to the sandstones. The clastic material is represented by the same rock types as in the sandstones, but in them the quantity of quartz and feldspars in the composition of the clastic fraction increases; in addition, the clasts contain much groundmass of acidic effusives, limestones, titanomagnetites, locally much epidote, and in the transition to the Kairaktinsky Formation in the south of the studied territory, the clastic fraction contains many limestone fragments, shell debris, and rare well-rounded glauconite fragments. Rarely encountered are practically monomineral plagioclase varieties.

The cement is most commonly basal argillaceous-carbonate, argillaceous-ferruginous, less commonly carbonate poikilitic or heterogranular aggregate – for brown varieties; argillaceous-carbonate, chloritic-argillaceous, argillaceous with silica – for green and grey varieties. In black and dark-grey aleurolites there is a great deal of fine plant detritus and rounded coal fragments.

In all cross-sections the concretions are carbonate, calcitic with an admixture of terrigenous material. In the central parts of the concretions, radial calcite seams are sometimes observed. A relationship exists between the composition of the cement, the abundance of their segregations, their size and form. Thus, in rocks with carbonate-clayey cement the concretions are more numerous and larger in size. Their form is isometric with an uneven, hummocky surface. For carbonate-free varieties of siltstones, the absence or negligible content of concretions is characteristic.

Study of the relationship of concretions to bedding indicates their sedimentary-early diagenetic origin. For siltstones, numerous chemical and thermal analyses have been carried out by previous researchers both for the southern and northern parts of the studied territory. In the brown varieties, the ratio of ferrous iron to ferric iron is somewhat lower than for the gray varieties, but this difference is less than in sandstones. Siltstones are characterized by higher values of the alumina-to-silica ratio, which is apparently related to the increased content of clay minerals in siltstones compared to sandstones.

For siltstones, massive and sometimes clotted spotted textures are characteristic. Silty sandstones are massive, wavy-bedded, and discontinuously horizontally laminated.

In the roof of siltstone interbeds, desiccation cracks are frequently observed. A particularly large number of them is observed in the crest parts of consolidation anticlinal structures. Throughout, in siltstones and silty sandstones, traces of bioturbation are visible. They are observed not only macroscopically but also in thin sections. Sometimes on the surfaces of siltstone and argillite interbeds, raindrops, ice crystal imprints, and ripple marks are visible.

The quantity of gray-colored siltstones increases sharply to the north of the Atbasar uplift. In the gray-colored and especially in the greenish-gray siltstones and silty sandstones, small concretions of pyrite and marcasite are frequently observed. They are confined to massive and horizontally bedded varieties. The concretions are most commonly syngenetic, as indicated by the relationship of the concretions to the bedding of the rock. In aleurolites, small veinlets of pyrite and marcasite, 10–15 cm × 0.5–1 cm in size, are often observed concordant with the bedding. In massive varieties of greenish-gray aleuritic sandstones, a uniform dissemination of well-formed pyrite crystals is more commonly observed. The crystals have the form of pentagonal dodecahedra, less commonly cubic form. Under surface conditions they are replaced by iron hydroxides and are sometimes leached out.

In the Tersakkansk suite, fairly thick fine-detrital rock beds enriched in pyrite are locally observed. Their stratigraphic position within the suite section varies at different structural locations, but most often they occur in the upper part of the substage.

In red-colored aleurolites, pyritization was observed only in reference-parametric borehole 1-14B at a depth of 1080 m. In association with pyrite, small nests of barite-celestine are present in them.

**Argillites** occur very rarely in the section of the Tersakkansk suite, are of clearly subordinate significance, and are associated with aleurolites and limestones. These are, as a rule, well-silicified rocks with a schistose arrangement of clay particles. According to data by A.D. Gosteev (1970), they have a montmorillonite, sladisto-montmorillonite composition with an admixture of beidellite. By color, red, brown, brownish, gray, greenish-gray, and bluish-gray varieties are distinguished; in the latter, shrinkage cracks and carbonate concretions are most frequently noted. All argillites are carbonaceous. In the brown varieties, coatings of iron hydroxides are frequently encountered (Fig. 41). Thermal analysis of a bright-red argillite from borehole O1 at a depth of 424 m yields a thermogram on which kaolinite, chlorite, calcite, goethite, zeolites, limonite, and hematite are clearly identified.

Very unusual lens-shaped spotted bright-red dense argillites were encountered in the Taldyk depression, where they are penetrated by reference boreholes 1-14B and 1-14A in the uppermost part of the Tersakkansk suite. The spottiness in them is caused by the lens-shaped spotted distribution of iron oxides and hydroxides: in the darker lenses their content reaches up to 60%, whereas in the lighter ones no more than 10–15%. Such argillites contain an admixture of ash material (borehole 14B–1156.5). Along with ash material, rounded glass fragments resembling lapilli, up to 0.5 cm in size, are present in the rock. Slightly higher in the section (well 14B-1027.3) the argillites become more siliceous. The carbonate content, distributed uniformly throughout the rock, remains significant. In these argillites, "porphyritic" segregations are also noted — somewhat nest-like accumulations of such segregations. The nest sizes are small, 2.5–3 mm in diameter; they are distributed uniformly throughout the rock and constitute about 10% of the rock. Within a nest there is usually not one but several tabular, irregularly shaped segregations with distinct outlines. They are composed of an aggregate of calcite, albite, and rare needles of biotite and ore mineral. Fine-grained, tabular albite with good twinning, together with calcite, makes up the main part of these segregations.

Similar formations also occurred in wells on the northeastern limb of the Taldyk syncline, in the red-colored siltstones of the Tersakkansk Formation, which underlie the ore-bearing sequence of the Lower Permian.

Over the entire remaining studied territory, such segregations were not observed.
