Among the deposits of the Kireyskaya Formation, sandstones, silty sandstones, siltstones, limestones, gravelstones, intraformational and deformational conglomerates, and tuffites are identified. **Sandstones** constitute in the sections of the Kireyskaya Formation from 19 to 65%. As a rule, the most thick interbeds of sandstones are observed in the lower half of the formation, which in the southwest, precisely due to the predominance of sandstones in the section, is distinguished as the lower Kireyskaya sub-formation.

Among the sandstones, coarse-grained, medium-grained, and fine-grained varieties are distinguished. The ratio of these varieties varies in different cross-sections.

Medium- and coarse-grained sandstones form the bases of mesorhythms, frequently occurring with erosional contact on the underlying siltstones; they exhibit lenticular, small-lenticular, and wavy-bedded, strongly cross-cutting textures; horizontal-bedding and massive textures are less commonly observed. The latter are characteristic of zones I and II. Unidirectional oblique series have been encountered at the Kyzylobinskaya anticline, and in the Zholboldinskaya and Tretyakovskaya synclines.

Fine-grained varieties are associated in cross-sections both with medium-grained sandstones and with siltstones. They are distributed both in individual cross-sections and across the entire studied territory fairly uniformly. Their textures are either wavy or horizontal-bedded, sometimes massive.

Coarse- to very coarse-grained varieties of sandstones are rarely encountered in cross-sections. They constitute 1–2% of them and have therefore been included in the medium-grained group in the table.

In them, the admixture of fragments of lesser uniformity is significant; by their structure they approach gritstone.

The composition of the clastic material of all sandstones is very varied, but uniform in different cross-sections in terms of structure and color. Among the clastic material, the following are encountered in order of decreasing abundance: liparites, dacites, feldspars, quartz, andesites, diabases, micropegmatites, argillites brown and green, siliceous and calcareous, calcareous pelitomorphic, crystalline, organogenic, silicified limestones and cherts, trachytes, trachydacites, quartzites, serpentinites, biotite, muscovite, titaniferous magnetite, pyroxenes, grain coatings of glauconite.

Fragments are most frequently angular or sub-rounded; grain packing is tight and very tight; traces of pressure solution of grains into one another are observed. In individual thin sections, good and moderate roundness of clastic material is observed.

In all thin sections, an admixture of carbonate is present in the cement. In individual interlayers in the northern part of the depression, quartz-albite and albite cements are observed in some rocks. In these rocks, an admixture of newly formed minerals of the epidote group is common (thin sections 1023/167, 298).

In sandstones of the southern part of the studied area, basal carbonate cement and carbonate cement with an admixture of gypsum were observed in individual thin sections.

Very characteristic are quartz and albite regeneration rims developed respectively around grains of quartz and albitized feldspars. The optical orientation of regeneration rims usually coincides with the orientation of the grains — polysynthetic twins of feldspars continue into the regeneration rims.

In the cement of red-colored sandstones, iron hydroxides are present in significant quantities in the form of thin films ("rinds") around detrital grains and filling pores; iron hydroxides replace part of the detrital grains. The uneven distribution of iron hydroxides emphasizes the layering in the sandstones.

The described cement types frequently occur together, which allows some conclusions to be drawn regarding the sequence of their precipitation. The earliest is ferruginous cement. Ferruginous films on grains were already present during deposition of the clastic material, at the same time precipitation of colomorph clots occurred in pore spaces. Under reducing conditions, ferric iron converted to ferrous iron and participated in the formation of chlorite. The formation of regeneration quartz and albite rims and, apparently, part of the carbonate cement occurred at later stages of lithogenesis.

Special attention should be given to the so-called magnetite sandstones. Although they do not determine the character of the formation section, they very strongly influence the magnetic activity of the formation section. On the studied territory, magnetite sandstones are encountered in the Kishpak section. Here they form lens-shaped accumulations in individual sandy interlayers. The thickness of individual lenses is up to 1.5–2 cm, their extent — several metres. The lenses clearly pinch out in

**A L E V R O L I T E S** constitute 40% of the formation sections. They are associated with fine-grained sandstones and argillites, forming the tops of mesorrhythms. The textures of the aleurolites are massive, horizontally-laminated, rarely wavy-laminated.

They are brittle, with fine-scaly parting, significantly more rarely thin-laminated, when the interbeds are enriched with carbonaceous matter. The clastic material is angular and semi-rounded; fragment sizes are 0.04–0.05 mm. The composition of the fragments is mainly quartz-feldspar, with admixture of rock fragments of siliceous composition, as listed above for sandstones. An admixture of larger sand grains is present. They are, as a rule, better rounded. The cement in green varieties is basal clay-hydromica-chloritic, in greenish-grey — clay-hydromicaceous, in brown — clay-carbonate, clay-ferruginous. In the northwest of the Tretyakovskaya depression, the cement contains a significant admixture of minerals of the epidote group and leucoxene. Silicification of the rocks is also observed here (thin section 1023/146).

Frequently in the aleurolites an admixture of ash particles is observed; in this case the aleurolites acquire a very distinctive spotted appearance. These textures are named "leopard" [illegible] when describing sections. From the spotted aleurolites, completely gradual transitions are observed to equally spotted tuffites. The spotted aleurolites and tuffites to a significant degree define the appearance of the formation. They constitute about 30% of all aleurolites of the formation.

**A L E U R O P S A M M I T E S** occupy an intermediate position in grain size between aleurolites and fine-grained sandstones. Among them, spotted varieties with tuffaceous admixture are also very commonly encountered. The character of the cement in them is analogous to that of sandstones and aleurolites.

**C O N G L O M E R A T E S**, intraformational, consisting of pebbles of argillites and siltstones of the Kireyskaya Formation, constitute approximately 0.5% of the formation section. In individual structures their quantity increases to 0.9% (Kipchakskaya syncline), while on the Kyzylobinskaya anticline and Aksuiyskaya anticline, on the contrary, their content in the section decreases to 0.2–0.4%.

Conglomerates of the intraformational type with a polymictic pebble composition form lenses and interbeds of 0.6–0.8 m thickness in the uppermost part of the formation, near its boundary with the Vladimirskaya Formation. These lenses are developed only in the south of the studied area, along the Kipchakskaya flexure. The pebble composition within them includes: polymorphic and "porphyritic" limestones, individual gravel grains of quartzites, acidic and intermediate effusives and quartz. The gravel-pebble material is cemented by medium-grained polymictic sandstone.

**T U F F I T E S** form interbeds 10–20 cm thick among siltstones, frequently connected to them by gradational transitions. Throughout the section, tuffites of three types are observed: grey, greenish-grey dense waxy, pink dense silicified spotted.

The spotted tuffites have a very distinctive coloration: yellow-grey, green and violet-grey colors are distributed in a spotted to wavy pattern. The coloration is determined by the clotted-coagulated texture of the rock. The dark, grey-violet spots contain a significant (up to 15–20%) admixture of ash material of dacitic affinity, which is replaced by prehnite. Along the periphery of the dark coagulated spots, replacement by prehnite is less intensive. The dimensions of such spots are up to 1–1.5 cm. All the remaining mass of the tuffite (matrix) represents siltstones with an insignificant (up to 5%) admixture of crystal-ash material and rare and isolated grains of prehnite, uniformly distributed throughout the entire rock mass.

Tuffites of this characteristic appearance are encountered in all sections of the Kireyskaya Formation. In the Tretyakovskaya trough, in the dark spots of grey-violet color, admixtures of ash material are absent, but in

A pink-colored tuffogenic rock is described in the Kireyskaya suite from layer 57 of the Archalinsk reference section. This is a typical ash tuff [illegible]. Macroscopically it is a dense rock of pink color. Under the microscope it is visible that it is composed of ash fragments of intricate shapes without significant terrigenous admixture. The size of ash particles is up to 0.16 mm. The rock is completely prenitized. Prehnite is characterized by an optical axial angle of 10–20°, birefringence of 0.020–0.021, straight extinction and negative elongation. In addition to prehnite, the rock contains minerals of the prehnite group — epidote and quartz. Throughout the entire rock, nests of microcrystalline calcite are uniformly observed.

Tuffs of the third type were well studied by Golubovskaya T.N. and Smolyaninova E.M. from the Kishkinsk reference section of the southwestern part of the studied territory. These are chloritized and prenitized crystal-ash tuffs of dacites.

**Limestones** in the Kireyskaya suite are not distinguished by great variety. They are grey, grey with brown spots, pelitomorphic. Very often "porphyritic" (Fig. 20). "Porphyritic" segregations of acicular and rhombic form up to 2 mm in size are represented by aggregates of microcrystalline calcite. In one of the limestones from the roof of the suite, relicts of monocrystals of gypsum were observed in porphyritic segregations. The groundmass of the rock is pelitomorphic or recrystallized fine-crystalline. An orientation in one direction of recrystallized elongated-prismatic calcite crystals is observed. Such recrystallization, apparently, occurred under conditions of unilateral pressure.

**Cherts**, layered, black-grey-white, form in the roof of the suite a well-defined interlayer of approximately 0.3 m thickness. Visually, in alluvial boulders of cherts on the surface, relicts of pink rocks resembling tuffs and relicts of grey pelitomorphic rocks resembling limestones are visible. In a single thin section from this horizon, sampled in the interfluve of the Shabdar–Syntас rivers, relicts of rocks were not detected. Characterizing the mineral composition of the formation, one cannot fail to mention the concretions and shrinkage formations present in all cross-sections; most frequently these are carbonate formations with "porphyroid" structures. Moreover, in some cases the lamination "flows around" them, while in other cases the shrinkage formations "pierce" the lamination. That is, there appear to be, apparently, both epigenetic and syngenetic carbonate formations.

In the cross-section of borehole T-20, anhydrite inclusions are observed throughout the entire interval. Their form is close to isometric, with dimensions up to 2 cm in diameter; they are composed of an anhydrite aggregate, sometimes with an admixture of gypsum and calcite.

Thus, the Kireyskaya Formation has the following characteristics:
1) very inconsistent thickness, varying practically across all structures;
2) compared to the Terekhansskaya Formation, a finer granulometric composition of rocks;
3) characteristic coarsening of material from south to north;
4) presence of spotted siltstones and tuffites;
5) presence of interlayers of prenitized ash tuffs of dacitic composition;
6) presence of "porphyroid" limestones with relict gypsum in "porphyroid" segregations;
7) presence of anhydrite concretions in siltstones and gypsum cements in sandstones;
8) absence of macro-fauna and abundant flora.
