**Mineral Composition of Rocks of the Krykkundy Complex**

**First Intrusive Phase**

The principal rocks comprising the first intrusive phase of the Krykkundy complex are quartz diorites and, in subordinate amounts, their subalkaline equivalents (quartz monzodiorites), as well as granodiorites. Diorites and biotite–hornblende granites are developed in subordinate quantities. In hybridized varieties, several authors have described more mafic rocks approaching gabbro-diorites in composition.

**Q u a r t z d i o r i t e s a n d q u a r t z m o n z o - d i o r i t e s** form independent bodies within the Karatas massif, the Borlinköl pluton, and a number of small

massifs in the area of Ishimskaya Luka, as well as the peripheral parts of the Bulandy–Akkol massif. These are typically grey and light-grey rocks, medium-grained and medium-coarse-grained. Textures are equigranular, less commonly porphyritic, hypidiomorphic-granular. Fabrics are massive or planar-parallel due to the uniform orientation of dark-coloured mineral grains.

The principal rock-forming minerals are: plagioclase, amphibole, biotite, quartz.

**P l a g i o c l a s e** constitutes 50–70% of the rock. Its content is very consistent in similar rocks of all massifs.

Tabular idiomorphic crystals up to 3 mm in equigranular varieties, and up to 7 mm in porphyritic segregations (Borlankol massif). They frequently form two-generation segregations. The first generation is represented by zonal varieties and has undergone significant secondary alteration. Crystals of the second generation are of a more "fresh" appearance; polysynthetic twins are characteristic of them. In composition it corresponds to andesine (An 38–45). Secondary alterations are expressed in saussuritization, affecting the central parts of the grain.

**A m p h i b o l e** (content varies: in the rocks of the Karatas massif 5–35%; in the Borlankol and Bulandy–Akkol massifs 7–20%).

It is ubiquitously represented by common hornblende. Typically small (< 1 mm) idiomorphic grains of prismatic habit. Pleochroic in green-yellow, blue-green-yellow tones according to the scheme Ng > Nm > Np. Frequently replaced by chlorite-group minerals. Intergrowths with biotite are not uncommon.

**B i o t i t e** (up to 15%, commonly less than 7%). Tabular crystals of small dimensions (< 1 mm). In fresh condition it is brown; upon replacement by chlorite (a common process) it acquires greenish colouration, and the birefringence simultaneously decreases. Besides chlorite, epidote-group minerals are secondary formations after biotite.

**Q u a r t z** (up to 14%, commonly 7–10%). Xenomorphic grains occupying the space between the plagioclase and dark-coloured mineral segregations. Undulatory extinction is characteristic.

**K a l i f e l d s p a r** — in quartz monzodiorites — up to 12%. Xenomorphic segregations in the groundmass. Represented by orthoclase- and microcline-perthite.

**G r a n o d i o r i t e s** play the most significant role in the structure of the Bulandy–Ankolsky massif. The rocks are predominantly light-grey and pinkish-grey in colour. From fine- to medium-grained to coarse-grained, hypidiomorphic and (rarely) granitic texture.

They differ from quartz diorites by higher quartz content (20–30%) and potassium feldspar (up to 35%) with a decrease in the amount of mafic minerals (~15%). Biotite and biotite-hornblende varieties are distinguished. The accessory mineral assemblage is analogous to that of the quartz diorites.

**B i o t i t e** and **b i o t i t e – h o r n b l e n d e g r a n i t e s** play a considerable role in the structure of the Bulandy–Ankolsky pluton.

They are characterised by an increased content of potassium feldspar (up to 40%).

**D i o r i t e s** and **g a b b r o – d i o r i t e s** are regarded by the majority of authors as products of hybridisation of more acidic rocks forming the main mass of the plutons. They are distributed in subordinate quantities and are confined, as a rule, to the marginal parts of the massifs, or to small intrusive bodies situated at some distance from the main pluton. Less commonly they occur as inclusions in the central

parts of the massifs.

The rocks have grey and dark-grey colouring, often inequigranular textures. The content of dark-coloured minerals may increase to 40–60%, with pyroxenes appearing. The quantity of quartz decreases to complete disappearance. With the rocks of the main series, gradual transitions occur.

**Second Intrusive Phase**

The rocks of the second intrusive phase within the territory of the works comprise the Derzhavinsk, Sarsymbaisk, and Kolutonsk massifs.

The principal petrographic varieties of the formations of the second phase of emplacement of the Krasnoduksk complex in the studied area are biotite, amphibole-biotite, and biotite-amphibole granites. The more melanocratic varieties, usually represented by granodiorites, are encountered rarely. In individual cases, varieties practically entirely devoid of potassium feldspar may be distinguished — essentially plagioclase granites.

**B i o t i t e, a m p h i b o l e - b i o t i t e** and **b i o t i t e - a m p h i b o l e g r a n i t e s** have a variable quantitative mineral composition, varying both within a single massif and between massifs. The rocks commonly display pinkish-grey, grey, and pinkish colours. Textures are medium- to coarse-grained, coarse-grained, frequently porphyritic owing to large phenocrysts of quartz, potassium feldspar, and plagioclase. With respect to the degree of idiomorphism of the rock-forming minerals, the textures are predominantly hypidiomorphic-granular to granitic.

**P l a g i o c l a s e** in the granitoids of the Karatas massif is represented predominantly by oligoclase (An 15–20); in other bodies, oligoclase-andesine (An 26–30) is most widespread. Idiomorphic tabular crystals and their intergrowths reach 2.5×1.6 mm. Polysynthetic twins are common.

**P o t a s s i u m f e l d s p a r** — microcline-perthite and orthoclase-perthite. In porphyritic phenocrysts it attains sizes of 5 mm. Xenomorphic with respect to the other minerals.

**Q u a r t z** forms xenomorphic grains with undulatory extinction, 1–2 mm in size. In porphyritic phenocrysts up to 4 mm.

**M a f i c m i n e r a l s** are represented by small grains of greenish-brown biotite and green hornblende in varying proportions.

**A c c e s s o r y**: apatite, sphene, ore minerals. Less commonly encountered: zircon, tourmaline. Secondary alteration is expressed in sericitization of plagioclase, pelitization of potassium feldspar, and chloritization of mafic minerals.

**G r a n o d i o r i t e s** differ from granites by a lower content of quartz and potassium feldspar coupled with a sharp increase in the amount of plagioclase — up to 70%.

**E s s e n t i a l l y p l a g i o c l a s e g r a n i t e s** are characterised by an almost **complete** absence of potassium feldspar together with a high quartz content of up to 40%. Described by M.S. Granitsin within the Karatas massif (Granitsin, 1970).

**Phase of supplementary intrusions**

Rocks of the supplementary intrusion phase have been identified within the described territory in the limits of the Bulandy–Ankol'sky massif (Yakimenko, 1964; Zhukov et al., 1972). They are represented by porphyritic granites (granite-porphyries). These are typically pink, greyish-pink rocks of massive, less commonly schlieren, texture. Porphyritic. represented by quartz, well-bounded crystals of feldspars (up to 1–1.5 cm).

The texture of the groundmass is medium- to fine-grained. The groundmass is composed of quartz, K-feldspar, plagioclase, less commonly biotite, and, in isolated cases, grains of hornblende.

Accessories: zircon, apatite, frequently orthite, monazite, ore minerals.

The granite-porphyries of the supplementary intrusion phase of the Bulandy–Akkol massif have their own vein facies represented by lenses and veins of zonally structured pegmatites and aplite dikes. The zonality of the pegmatites is manifested in the isolation of a small quartz core, coarse-blocky microcline, sometimes zones of graphic texture, but most commonly an aplitic selvage.

**Vein formations of the first stage.**

Within the studied area, the vein formations of the first stage of the Kriккuduk complex have been identified and described by previous researchers, primarily within the rock compositions of the Karatass and Bulandy–Akkol massifs. For other intrusive bodies of the district, composed of rocks of the Krikkuduk complex, veins of the first stage were either not identified — for example, in the Bordzhakol intrusion — or their identification and mapping are hampered by poor exposure.

The principal petrographic types of rocks of the first-stage veins are **FINE-GRAINED BIOTITE GRANITES, APLITES, AND GRANITE-PORPHYRIES.** The composition of the rocks is approximately uniform; they are composed of K-feldspar, quartz, and plagioclase, with K-feldspar strongly predominating over the other minerals. Biotite in the fine-grained granites reaches 1–2% and is not characteristic of the other rock types. The rocks differ in microstructure (aplitic, hypidiomorphic-granular, etc.).

The suite of accessory minerals is uniform for all rocks: zircon, orthite, apatite, fluorite, tourmaline, ore minerals.

Greisénization is characteristic of the first-stage veins of the Bulandy–Akkol massif.

complex, most widely distributed within the Bulandy-Akkol massif, more precisely in its southern part (Brofeyevsky intrusion). Isolated dikes of spessartites have been noted within the Sarymbaisk massif. For the granitoid bodies of the Ishim Bend area (Derzhavinsk and Borlykol massifs), dikes of the second stage are not characteristic, at least for those parts of the massifs that lie within the boundaries of the survey area. The dike complex of the Karatas massif is described separately ([illegible]).

The most widespread rocks forming the dikes of the second stage are **S P E S S A R T I T E S**. These are greenish-grey, grey, sometimes pinkish-grey rocks of frequently spotted texture, fine-grained, porphyritic structure. Composed of hornblende and plagioclase; phenocrysts are elongated-prismatic grains of hornblende. In some varieties there is a significant admixture of K-feldspar, owing to which a transition to vogezites (alkaline lamprophyres) is indicated.

Quartz and biotite are present in small quantities. Accessory minerals: ore mineral, sphene, less commonly apatite and zircon. Table No. 46 gives the chemical composition of a spessartite rock from one of the dikes of the Sarymbaisk massif.

**D I O R I T E S** are closely associated with lamprophyres. Grey, greenish-grey, dark-grey rocks, among which two varieties are distinguished: dioritic porphyrites and microdiorites. The former contain porphyritic segregations. From lamprophyres (spessartites), diorites are microscopically distinguished, in particular, by the composition of the phenocrysts, which in them are composed of plagioclase and not hornblende as in spessartites.

As secondary minerals, biotite, quartz, and sometimes K-feldspar are present.

Accessory minerals: ore mineral, apatite, zircon.

**D I A B A S E S** – not widely distributed. Rocks are massive, fine-grained, dark-green in colour. Microstructure is intersertal, consisting of laths of plagioclase, the interstices between which are filled by an aggregate of secondary minerals (biotite, chlorite) of dark colour.

Accessory minerals: ore mineral, tourmaline, rarely apatite.

**Petrochemical Characteristics of Rocks of the Krizhkuduk Complex**

To study the petrochemical features of the rocks of the Krizhkuduk complex, both the results of silicate analyses selected by the authors of the report and a number of analyses taken from the works of previous researchers were used (Table No. 47). The classification scheme developed by the Terminological Commission of the Petrographic Committee of OTT AS USSR, published in 1981 ("Classification ......", 1981), was taken as the basis for rock classification. The foregoing applies only to analyses of the Western-Teniz intrusive complex; in the case of analyses taken from previous works, the names given by the authors of those works were retained (Table No. 47). It should also be noted that the main attention was devoted to studying the petrochemical characteristics of rocks of the first and second intrusive phases, as the authors do not have data on the phase of additional intrusions.

The principal petrochemical parameters characterizing a rock and allowing it to be assigned to one or another classification subdivision are the contents of SiO₂ and the sum Na₂O + K₂O. Analysis of the diagram illustrating the relationship of the quantities SiO₂ – (Na₂O+K₂O) (Fig. No. 77) allows the following conclusions to be drawn: 1) rocks of the first and second phases of the Krizhkuduk complex belong to the groups of intermediate and acid rocks with SiO₂ content from 56% to 66% (first phase) and 66%–72% (second phase); 2) by alkali content, the rocks described form two series — a series of rocks of normal alkalinity from quartz diorites to normal granites, and a series of subalkaline rocks represented mainly by quartz monzodiorites, subalkaline quartz diorites, and further, through subalkaline granodiorites, to subalkaline granites. Two features that clearly manifested themselves on the proposed diagram should be noted: a) within virtually all massifs, rocks of both the normal and subalkaline series are represented; b) the "deflection" into the subalkaline field is most characteristic of the group of intermediate rocks, while the more acid varieties (with SiO₂ content of 64%–66%) more often belong to the normal series, which is apparently explained by the influence of assimilation processes that manifested themselves most prominently during the formation of the relatively melanocratic varieties of rocks of the Krizhkuduk complex.
