The report submitted for review consists of 888 pages of main text, 16I pages of textual appendices, and I09 sheets of graphic appendices.

The main text of the report consists of 3 volumes and contains all necessary sections.

The "Introduction" is written sufficiently comprehensively, presenting the necessary information on the objectives set before the party, the volumes of work completed, and the specifics of conducting geological re-survey at scale 1:200,000.

The chapter "Physical-Geographical Overview" is written fully and reflects all information about the area of operations. In the chapter "Geological-Geophysical State of Investigation," the authors analyze in detail all previously completed work, noting all positive and negative aspects of the preceding geological surveys. At the same time, the authors' assertions that "in reports of a later period there are numerous distortions of the views of researchers of an earlier period..." (p. 37) and "...violation of elementary rules and principles of stratigraphic subdivision..., alongside violations of the methodology for mapping mute red-coloured continental sequences, and simply the irresponsibility of individual performers, have led to the fact that the majority of maps at scale 1:50,000 do not tie in at sheet borders..." sound incorrect with respect to their colleagues.

Chapters III and IV deal with the methodology of geophysical work and the methodology for interpreting their materials. In the table of physical properties of rocks of the area presented, attention is drawn to the insufficient (statistically) number of analyzed samples for many lithological varieties, including Lower Palaeozoic,

which calls into question the validity of the quantitative interpretation of geophysical data.

The chapter "Stratigraphy" is written fairly thoroughly and generally makes a good impression; however, there are a number of comments on it, most of which are of a debatable nature.

In describing the Bratolyubovka Series, the authors indicate that Dmitrovsky M.V. (1977) identified, within the rock succession of this series in the area of well Д-179, two independent stratigraphically distinct sequences (the core of the Shunkurkol brachyanticline). In that same location, Dmitrovsky M.V. collected a fauna of brachiopods and radiolarians from the lower part of the section, which, according to the conclusions of R.G. Tenyakova and B.Sh. Klinger, belongs to the Ordovician age. The authors of the report under review, however, assign this sequence to the Proterozoic and correlate it with the Bratolyubovka Series of the Ishim Luka without adequate justification (p. 125, Vol. I).

The assignment of the Kargalinsk Suite to the Upper Ordovician formations (Ashgillian) has been made without adequate justification. The conclusion of N.F. Mikhailova based on graptolite collections from this sequence indicates a middle Caradocian (late Caradocian) age, to which the authors themselves make reference on p. 166 (Vol. I); however, they accept the Ashgillian age.

Doubt is also raised regarding the necessity and validity of introducing, at the stage of geological re-investigation at scale 1:200,000, new (previously known) suite names within the Carboniferous formations not ratified by the Stratigraphic Conference of 1971. This is all the more so given that in ongoing geological mapping works at scale 1:50,000, in both the western and eastern parts of the Teniz Depression, suite names remain unchanged. The authors unquestionably have the right to propose changes to suite names and to introduce new ones (Kypshak), but this should have been done within the text, without entering them on maps and stratigraphic columns prior to fulfilling the requirements of § 3, Ch. VIII of the Stratigraphic Code.

Particular attention should be drawn to the Kairaktin and Tersakkan suites identified by the authors. In published works and reports (Golubovsky et al.) concerning the southern part of the Teniz Depression, it is proposed to distinguish the Tersakkan and Ashchilin suites, and for the northern part — the Vladimirovo, Archalin, and Kairaktin suites. The authors, however, despite acknowledging the difference between the southern and northern sections of the Teniz Depression, identify the Tersakkan and Kairaktin suites for the depression as a whole.

In this respect, the identification of the Teniz Formation is unclear, as is the disappearance of the Archalin Formation and the assignment of the Tersakkansk Formation to the Upper Carboniferous, given that the majority of organic remains collected from the deposits of the Tersakkansk Formation are identified as Upper Carboniferous–Lower Permian (data of the authors).

It is unclear why the identification of the Archalin Formation, as a facies analogue of the Kairaktin Formation according to the authors' conclusion (p. 169, Vol. III), is inadvisable, while the Teniz Formation, as an analogue of the upper part of the Tersakkansk and the greater part of the Kairaktin Formation, is considered advisable? At the same time, on p. 159, Vol. II, the authors write: "... on the adjacent area to the east, during the GGK 1:200,000 survey (Petrylak, 1983), this rock sequence was assigned to the upper member of the Vladimirka Formation. During geological mapping at scale 1:50,000 on the area of sheets M-42-67 (Zavrazhny, 1983), this sequence was assigned to the Archalin Formation. Indeed, in its appearance it resembles the section of both formations, but is an analogue of the greater part of the Kairaktin Formation." From all of the above, the question inevitably arises: how then should one understand the necessity of identifying the Teniz Formation, if the existence of the Vladimirka and Archalin Formations is not accepted by the authors?

On p. 107, Vol. II, the authors write that the sequence of undivided Middle–Upper Carboniferous is overlain by "... variegated sediments of the undivided upper member of the Upper Tersakkansk Submember and the lower member of the Kairaktin Formation. The identification of the latter is also carried out by correlation, but now with the Archalin reference section."

The authors of the report provide numerous tables of fauna and flora collections from various stratigraphic subdivisions, but unfortunately draw conclusions regarding age themselves, without providing the conclusions of specialists.

The intrusive formations and tectonic structure of the western part of the Teniz Basin are described sufficiently fully; it is noteworthy only that the authors, having devoted considerable attention to the description of plicative structures and the history of the area's development, paid little attention to disjunctive dislocations. A significant portion of the flexural bends, so characteristic of the Teniz Basin and identified from seismic survey data, as well as fault dislocations recorded on the interpretation schemes of geophysical materials, have not been reflected on the cross-sections accompanying the geological maps.

In the chapters "Mineral Resources" and "Patterns of Mineral Resource Distribution," the authors have devoted primary attention to the copper-bearing potential of the western part of the Teniz depression. These chapters are written quite thoroughly, with the involvement of an enormous body of factual material, carefully analyzed and processed. The association of copper occurrences with 8 stratigraphic levels has been confirmed. Their lithological and structural controls have been established. On the basis of the established criteria and indicators of copper mineralization, prospective areas for mineral deposit exploration have been identified and recommendations have been provided regarding the direction of further work.

Regarding the chapter "Patterns of Mineral Resource Distribution and Prognostic Assessment," a number of comments are in order.

By Protocol of the NTS PGO dated May 18, 1981, it was determined that copper content of 0.2–0.3% is subject to accounting, with a minimum thickness of the mineralized interval of 1.5 m; consequently, calculating prognostic resources based on copper contents of 0.03% (Site 1 B, Site 2 B, etc.) has no meaning whatsoever.

As a rule, when describing the prospects of the western part of the Teniz depression for copper, only isolated copper content values are cited (often far from commercial grade) without indication of thickness. The depths at which drilling of boreholes is recommended to encounter "commercial" ores are in the range of 500–2000 m. As is evident from the catalogue of copper occurrences presented in the report, the thickness of the mineralized intervals (sometimes not indicated at all), even in the Tersakan suite, does not exceed, with rare exceptions, 2–2.5 m, and is generally within the range of the first centimeters to 1 m. The question naturally arises: are expenditures on exploration in the near future justified for such "deposits," where prognostic reserves are based on isolated copper showings, and their total does not exceed 500,000 tonnes of ore?

The greater part of the deep boreholes recommended in the report for copper exploration in the western part of the Teniz depression appears to the reviewer to serve not so much an exploratory purpose as to resolve disputed questions of stratigraphy.

Overall, the assessment of the western part of the Teniz depression for the exploration of cupriferous sandstones, based on the results of the work carried out, is highly negative.

The authors' recommendations for the exploration of other types of raw materials should be agreed with.

The graphic materials presented in the report are executed at a high professional level; the geological maps are distinguished from previously compiled ones by their very high level of detail and drafting precision, which should undoubtedly be credited to the authors.

The text of the report reads easily, is generally logically structured, and is well edited. Regret is caused by the absence of a borehole catalogue, which leads to certain difficulties when searching for one or another borehole on the maps.
