This bed is distinguished by its textural characteristics: it has a nodular texture, with numerous burrow traces and large patches of "porphyritic" structure. During geological surveys at a scale of 1:50,000 (Smolyaninova 1970, 1972, 1980), its base was used to draw the lower boundary of the upper member of the Kiiminsk Formation. On the proposed report maps, the upper boundary of the Kiiminsk Formation has been drawn at the base of this bed.

Within the Kiiminsk group of synclines, the submember has a very indistinct upper boundary. This apparently explains the significant variations in the determination of the submember thickness in this area, ranging from 380 m to 640 m.

As noted above, the submember is terrigenous in composition, but the presence of interbeds of "crypto-porphyritic" limestones in its upper part and the enrichment of the aleurolithic component of the section with interbeds of "porphyritic" concretions has allowed the submember to be divided into two members in individual structures:

P₁₋₂ km²¹ — without limestones,
P₁₋₂ km²² — with limestones and calcareous concretions.

These members have been mapped in the Aukelbek syncline, within the Zhenachat dome, and on the southeastern limb of the Shoptykul syncline. In the Algabas and Kiiminsk synclines, poor exposure of the formation's rocks did not permit their mapping, although an increase in the number of limestone interbeds in the upper part of the Upper Kiiminsk submember was also noted there.

In the Oksanov syncline, the deposits of the Kiiminsk Formation are identified conditionally (P₁₋₂ km?). As has been repeatedly emphasized above, the section of the Oksanov syncline is distinctive and greatly reduced in thickness.

In terms of rock composition, the Kiimin Formation is argillaceous-terrigenous; within it the following are distinguished:

1. Clastic and carbonate-clastic rocks.
2. Argillaceous and carbonate-argillaceous rocks.
3. Carbonate and terrigenous-carbonate rocks.

**Clastic and carbonate-clastic rocks** constitute the main rock mass of the formation; they are distributed throughout the entire section and are represented by fine- to medium-grained, fine-grained sandstones, siltstones, and alevropesчаники [aleuritic sandstones]. The coarsest-grained varieties are most developed to the northwest of the Shurumсай Uplift, along the northern and northwestern limbs of the synclines, which were previously assigned to a single synclinal structure referred to as the Kiimin Syncline.

The sandstones are polymictic in clast composition. Clastic grains constitute 60% to 90% in the sandstones in poorly sorted varieties, and only with a significant carbonate content does their quantity decrease to 45–50%. Clast sizes in the sandstones range from 0.05 to 0.25 mm. Sorting of clastic grains is, as a rule, poor to moderate. Clast shapes are highly varied; semi-rounded and angular forms predominate, rounded forms are less common. Clastic grains of the Upper Kiimin Subformation in many cases bear ferruginous "coatings."

Siltstones differ from sandstones only in clast size and occasionally in packing.

-175-

In composition, both types are polymictic, consisting of rock fragments, feldspars, and quartz.

Based on the ratio of these three components, the following types are distinguished among the clastic rocks of the Kiiminsk Formation: arkosic graywackes, quartz-feldspathic graywackes, quartz-graywacke arkoses, feldspathic graywackes, and quartz-free graywackes. The most widely distributed are arkosic graywackes, particularly in the upper Kiiminsk sub-formation. Feldspathic and quartz-free graywackes are characteristic of the lower portions of the upper Kiiminsk sub-formation in the northeastern part of the study area.

The distinction among all graywackes lies only in the ratio of the principal components of the clastic grains; in terms of the character of admixtures and cement, **they are all similar.**

Rock fragments are represented by argillaceous-siliceous, siliceous rocks, quartzites, and mafic effusives. Feldspars throughout the area are represented by plagioclases. As a clastic admixture, ore minerals are constantly present (magnetite, titanomagnetite); their content frequently reaches 10–15%, but is usually 2–3%. Zircon and limestone fragments are noted. For the rocks of the upper unit of the upper Kiiminsk sub-formation in the Aukelbek syncline, the presence of detrital epidote in significant quantities is characteristic.

Cement is typically pore-type, less commonly pore-basal, occasionally film-type. In mineral composition, it is, as a rule, complex. All clastic rocks in the cement contain carbonate — either calcite or siderite. The carbonate forms pelitomorphic or crystalline, unevenly granular cement, rarely poikilitic. In addition to carbonates, the cement contains areas of argillaceous, chloritic, and ferruginous cements, which most often occur in combination with calcite cement. Individual pores, as in the rocks of the Tereakkan Formation, may be filled with coarsely crystalline chlorite, quartz, barite, barite-celestine, and quartz with epidote.

With a low cement content, cementation of the rock occurs through the indentation of fragments into one another with the formation of microstylolitic sutures.

**Clayey and carbonate-clayey rocks** are distributed in the Kiymin Formation in subordinate quantities. They most commonly form thin interbeds among siltstones, from which they are poorly distinguished during field descriptions. They are composed of a clay-carbonate uniformly mixed material with an admixture of clastic grains of sandy-siltstone size. This admixture constitutes from 5 to 20% and is represented by quartz, feldspars, rock fragments, ore minerals, zircon, and epidote. The forms of the clastic grains are typically corrosion-type.

Clayey-carbonate and carbonate-clayey rocks can be highly ferruginous. The content of iron hydroxides is uneven throughout the rock. In some cases, the clayey rocks are completely filled with carbonaceous detritus.

The textures of the clayey rocks are frequently micro-laminated. The micro-lamination is caused by the uniform orientation of clay and carbonaceous particles, or by the alternation of laminae with varying granulometric compositions.

Typical argillites among the rocks of the Kiymin Formation are extremely rare. They contain virtually no carbonate or clastic grains and are composed of a homogeneous, weakly birefringent, ferruginized clay material.

**Carbonate and terrigenous-carbonate rocks** of the Kiymin Formation are represented by microcrystalline chemogenic limestones, "cryptocrystalline porphyritic" limestones, and limestone concretions.

Microcrystalline limestones are characteristic of the Lower Kiymin Subformation. Higher up the section they are encountered extremely rarely. The rock texture is microcrystalline, uniformly crystalline, or clotted. An admixture of siltstone material is common (up to 10%). In addition to the terrigenous admixture, the rock almost always contains organic remains: fragments of algae, shells of ostracods, phyllopods, and pelecypods.

Microsilicification is characteristic of the rocks of the Kiymin Formation. Silica forms micro-lenses up to 1.2 mm in size, with thicknesses of tenths of a millimeter.

In the Kiyminskaya sub-suite, calcite veinlets are observed that literally permeate the thin limestone beds from base to top (fig. 58, 59). In texture, these limestones resemble the nodular limestones of the Kayraktinsky suite. Under the microscope, relicts of algae are visible in them, and ostracods are frequently noted in considerable numbers.

Concealed porphyritic texture is a characteristic structural feature of the "porphyritic" limestones of the Kiyminskaya suite, in contrast to the porphyritic limestones of the Yagovkinsky horizon, the Kireyskaya suite, and the Shoptykul'skaya suite.

Porphyritic segregations in these limestones are visible only on weathered surfaces. Their form in natural size is shown in fig. 60. On a fresh break, such limestones do not reveal a porphyritic structure. The rock appears as a uniform cryptocrystalline mass. Under the microscope, the presence of two structural elements in the rock is clearly visible: the groundmass and the porphyritic segregations enclosed within this mass.

The groundmass of the limestones is composed of microcrystalline calcite; it sometimes exhibits a clotted structure, less commonly pelletoid. A terrigenous admixture of up to 10% of silt-grade size is frequently present in the groundmass. Most commonly it is represented by quartz, feldspars, and rock fragments. Practically all limestones of the Kiyminskaya suite contain fragments of valves, valves, nuclei, and whole shells of ostracods, phyllopods; very frequently, especially in the lower Kiyminskaya sub-suite, fish scales and finely comminuted plant detritus are encountered.

Porphyritic segregations represent, in the overwhelming majority of cases, pseudomorphs of medium- to fine-grained calcite with more recrystallized outer portions and less recrystallized inner portions of the porphyritic segregations (fig. 61). Relicts of sandy-silty admixture are visible in them.

The sizes of porphyritic segregations reach 2 mm; average sizes are 1–1.5 mm. The shape of the "cryptoporphyritic" segregations is most commonly elongated rhombic, rhombic, or rectangular.

In the central parts of such concretions, voids formed by their compression are frequently observed, filled with white crystalline calcite.

In the upper part of the formation, in the upper bed of the upper Kiima subformation, porphyritic carbonate concretions are frequently observed. Porphyritic segregations are visible in them both on fresh fracture and on weathered surfaces. They are composed of white crystalline calcite, which develops frequently after zeolites, but most commonly after gypsum.

**T e r r i g e n o u s - c a r b o n a t e r o c k s** of the Kiima Formation comprise rocks consisting of more than 50% calcite but containing terrigenous admixture in amounts exceeding 30%. Silty-sandy limestones and silty-clayey limestones are distinguished.

Silty-sandy limestones contain terrigenous admixture up to 40% by rock volume. It is represented by rock fragments, quartz, plagioclase, and clayey material. Calcite in these rocks occupies more than 50% of rock volume. It is heterogeneous: pelitomorphic and crystalline. In the pelitomorphic mass, chlorite flakes, clayey-ferruginous material, and quartz are frequently observed.

Rocks of this type are most widespread in the lower levels of the upper Kiima subformation.

Silty-clayey limestones consist of a clayey-carbonate mass at 70–80% and a terrigenous silty admixture. The carbonate in these rocks is typically pelitomorphic or microgranular, well separated from the clayey constituent; it forms clots and concretions.

In many cases the rock is saturated with carbonized plant remains and ostracod valves. Such rocks are frequently silicified.

In the sandstones and siltstones of the lower levels of the upper Kiima subformation, tuffogenic admixture is frequently observed; particularly large amounts of it have been noted in the Atsilinsky, Aukelbek, and Sytasskaya synclines.

The physical properties of rocks of the Kiyminskaya Formation were studied on 137 samples.

The density of sandstones varies from 2.63 g/cm³ to 2.66 g/cm³, siltstones from 2.61 g/cm³ to 2.66 g/cm³. The weighted mean density of the rocks is 2.64 g/cm³, which is practically indistinguishable from the weighted mean density of the Upper Paleozoic deposits; therefore, mapping the Kiyminskaya Formation in the gravity field is not considered feasible.

With respect to magnetic properties, all rocks of the Kiyminskaya Formation are non-magnetic; magnetic susceptibility ranges from 3 to 42·10⁻⁶ CGS units.

Among the sandstones, isolated samples of weakly magnetic sandstones are encountered, whose magnetic susceptibility reaches 150·10⁻⁶ CGS units.

In the magnetic field above the deposits of the Kiyminskaya Formation, weakly expressed negative anomalies are observed.

The age of the deposits of the Kiyminskaya Formation is determined by their stratigraphic position above the Kairaktinsky Formation, for which a Lower Permian age has been established, and by numerous finds of ostracod and phyllopod fauna, spore-pollen assemblages, vertebrate fauna remains, and plant remains.

From the deposits of the Kiyminskaya Formation, numerous ostracods have been collected, which were identified by S.B. Mamutova. The location of vertebrate remains is confined to the upper part of the upper Kiiyma subformation. The first collections of terrestrial vertebrates were made here by N.V. Litvinovich in 1950. From these, amphibians and reptiles were identified by I.A. Yefremov.

Subsequently, in 1951, an expedition of the Paleontological Institute of the USSR Academy of Sciences was organized to the burial site, under the leadership of Ye.D. Konzhukova. Numerous and diverse vertebrate remains were collected. Among the remains, amphibians were identified by Ye.D. Konzhukova and N.A. Yefremov: Labyrinthodontia, Batrachosauria, Inorimosuchus satpaevi Efr., and reptiles: Cotylosauria, Diadectomorpha (?) gen. et sp. nov., Pelycosauria (?) gen. et sp. nov.

On the basis of the indicated fossils, the age of the deposits was determined by N.A. Yefremov as Lower Permian.

At the burial site, during additional study, supplementary searches for fossil vertebrates were organized. Unfortunately, they did not yield new valuable finds, although individual fragments of fossilized bones were discovered.

During geological follow-up study, a very large number of samples from the deposits of the Kiiyma Formation were selected for spore-pollen analysis. A great number of these samples proved to be unproductive. In individual samples, isolated spores and pollen were found, which cannot refine the age of the formation beyond confirming its Permian age.
